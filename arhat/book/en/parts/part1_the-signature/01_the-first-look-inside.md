## 1.1 The First Look Inside the Door

Ask the world about arhats, and nine questions in ten begin with: what can he *do*? Can he fly? Can he see past lives and futures? Can he read the thoughts in another's mind? As if the proof of a finished path were the abilities that appear in the finisher's hands.

The question points the wrong way. The surest method for seeing how, is to go back to the places where the Buddha himself defined the word "arhat" — and watch when, where, to whom, and what he said.

### One Word in the Deer Park

The place is the Deer Park outside Vārāṇasī — the ground where the Buddha first turned the wheel of the Dharma. The Saṃyukta-āgama preserves there a run of very short suttas: the Blessed One, dwelling in the Deer Park, teaching the monks the four noble truths over and over, each telling shifted by only a few words. First the declaration: there are four noble truths — suffering, its arising, its cessation, the way. Then the charge: of the truth of suffering, *one ought to know and understand it*; of its arising, *one ought to sever it*; of its cessation, *one ought to realize it*; of the way, *one ought to cultivate it* — four "oughts," the words of the road: what should be known, what should be severed. Then, in the next sutta, the four "oughts" become four "dones." And exactly where the words turn to "done," the name appears:

> When a monk has, of the noble truth of suffering, known it and understood it; of the noble truth of its arising, known it and severed it; of the noble truth of its cessation, known it and realized it; of the noble truth of the way to its cessation, known it and cultivated it — such a monk is called an arhat: the taints all exhausted, what was to be done is done, the burdens laid down, his own good attained, the fetters of being all ended, rightly liberated by perfect knowledge.

Read the definition slowly. After the word "arhat" comes nothing but the language of settlement: the taints all exhausted — the leaking defilements, ended; what was to be done is done — the work, finished; the burdens laid down — the load, set off the shoulders; his own good attained — the ultimate good that was his to reach, reached; the fetters of being all ended — the ties binding him to existence, severed; rightly liberated by perfect knowledge — released, well and truly, by right understanding. Not one word says what he *can do*. In the same park where the wheel first turned, the Buddha defined the road's end — and every word of the definition is the language of a burden coming off a shoulder.

There is a layer of grammar to see as well. The sutta does not say "the taints exhausted, *and then* liberation is obtained besides"; the whole string — burdens laid down, his own good attained, fetters ended, rightly liberated — is the unfolding of the one fact "the taints all exhausted." The taints being ended, the burden is of itself off, the fetters of themselves gone, the knowledge of itself free. A comparison: the moment a load leaves the shoulder, the lightness is already there on the shoulder; no one sets down a load and then goes to collect a lightness. Exhaustion is the substance; the laid-down burden and the ended fetters are its aspect.

And that is the word this whole book turns on: the mastery is the **signature** of exhaustion. See a signature, and you know its writer reached this page; see the freedom of "the burdens laid down," and you know the taints are gone. The mastery is not a treasure issued beyond the exhaustion of taints; it is what that exhaustion looks like.

### The Same Passage, the Other Face

The exhaustion of taints itself has its standard scripture in the third of the three knowledges. The Saṃyukta-āgama records, elsewhere: the Blessed One, dwelling at Jeta's Grove in Anāthapiṇḍika's Park at Sāvatthī, told the monks that one beyond training has three knowledges — the knowledge of past lives, the knowledge of beings' passing and arising, and the realization-knowledge of the exhaustion of taints. The third is defined thus:

> What is the realization-knowledge of the exhaustion of taints? The noble disciple knows as it really is: this is suffering; this is its arising; this is its cessation; this is the way to its cessation. Knowing thus, seeing thus, his mind is liberated from the taint of sensuality, liberated from the taint of being, liberated from the taint of ignorance; and in the knowledge of liberation he knows: "Birth is exhausted; the holy life has been lived; what was to be done is done; I myself know there is no further becoming." This is called the realization-knowledge of the exhaustion of taints.

Some readers will find this passage familiar. It should be — this book's twin volume, *The Four Fruits of the Renunciant's Path*, set down its pen on exactly these lines. That book wrote the walking: four fruits, station by station, how each is reached; and at the settling of the last cut it said: what lies inside the door belongs to another volume. The same passage, the same sentence — that book read "the last cut completes"; this book reads "the freedom begins here." *His mind is liberated from the taint of sensuality, from the taint of being, from the taint of ignorance* — looking back, it is the period at the end of the subtraction; looking on, the three clauses say precisely this: the mind, with respect to the three taints, is already free and unbound. The subtraction's last step and freedom's first step are one and the same step. That book stopped at the threshold; this book begins from its inner side.

As for the four sentences that follow — "Birth is exhausted; the holy life has been lived; what was to be done is done; I myself know there is no further becoming" — this is the arhat's self-attestation, and the whole of it is a statement of accounts settled, not a list of acquisitions. It does not say "I have attained vast and radiant states"; it says only: here, the account is closed. The highest arrival, attesting to itself, refuses even the grammar of addition. That refusal is the first thing to see clearly inside the door.

### Not a Seam's Width Between

Before going further in, one question must be settled at the door: are the exhaustion and the freedom two events — first the severing completes, then, an instant later, the freedom arises?

Do not underrate that instant. Grant even a hairsbreadth of "afterwards," and the freedom becomes a fruit *obtained* once the exhaustion is done — and we are back in the grammar of addition: finish the work, then collect the reward.

The treatises closed that gap. The *Abhidharmakośa* (composed by Vasubandhu), treating the last step in the severing of defilements, says: the adamantine concentration severs the final grade of defilement, and the knowledge that arises immediately upon it is called the knowledge of exhaustion —

> Because this path of release arises at the very first together with the exhaustion of all the taints, it is called the knowledge of exhaustion. When this knowledge of exhaustion has arisen, one becomes an arhat, beyond training.

"Together" is the load-bearing word: co-arisen, with no before and no after. When the knowledge of exhaustion arises, the taints are ended; when the taints are ended, the knowledge of exhaustion has arisen — not severing first and knowing after, not exhaustion first and freedom after, but two faces of a single instant. A seal and the thing it seals are set down in one impression; no one finishes the deed and then comes back to stamp it. So the "is" in "the exhaustion of taints *is* mastery" counts in time as well: the consummation of the severance is the inception of the freedom, with not a seam's width between.

### The Freedom on the Side of "Nothing"

Then what, exactly, does "mastery" name? Two kinds of freedom must be told apart. One is *power-to*: to fly, to vanish, to read minds — the more abilities, the more free. The other is *free-from*: not one rope left that can bind — nothing that can fasten, and therefore nowhere one is not free.

The arhat's mastery is the second kind. The evidence, again, is the Buddha's own words. The Saṃyukta-āgama records: the Blessed One, at Jeta's Grove, teaching the monks "the seven points of skill and the three modes of contemplation" — a discipline of contemplating the five aggregates. And in his opening sentence, before the discipline itself, he stated where the whole of it ends:

> There are the seven points of skill and the three modes of contemplation. Bringing this teaching to its end, one attains the exhaustion of taints, attains the taintless — the liberation of mind, the liberation by wisdom — and in this very life one knows for oneself, witnesses in one's own person, and dwells in full possession of it …

Brought to its end, what does the discipline yield? The exhaustion of taints, the attainment of the *taintless* — gains floored, both of them, on the word "nothing." The liberation of mind — the mind unfastened from craving; the liberation by wisdom — ignorance seen through and broken. "In this very life one knows for oneself, witnesses in one's own person, and dwells in full possession of it" — the last words matter: this is a freedom *dwelt in*, settled and complete, not a state that flashes and is gone. And when the long teaching was finished, the Buddha gathered it by saying the opening words once more — head and tail, the whole discipline framed inside that freedom of "nothing."

Like a man released from prison: his freedom consists in there being no wall left that can hold him, not in some treasure handed to him at the gate. The one who can fly is not thereby free — adepts outside the path, by concentration, can also fly; the one whose taints are exhausted is necessarily free — for not one taint remains that can bind him. The full accounting between powers and freedom belongs to Part Three of this book; at the door, keep one sentence: the name of the mastery is tied to the exhaustion of taints, not to any power.

### On the Final Journey

How much weight this carried in the Buddha's own heart can be seen in a single place.

The Sūtra of the Final Journey in the Dīrgha-āgama records the Buddha's last road. Eighty years old — "I am old now; my years are full eighty" are his own words on this road — he left Rājagṛha and walked north, toward the place of his own passing. Not long out of Rājagṛha he came to a bamboo grove, took his seat in the hall, and spoke to the monks on virtue, concentration, and wisdom:

> Cultivating virtue, one gains concentration, and the reward is great; cultivating concentration, one gains wisdom, and the reward is great; cultivating wisdom, the mind is cleansed, and one attains even liberation, exhausting the three taints — the taint of sensuality, the taint of being, the taint of ignorance. Liberation attained, the knowledge of liberation arises: birth and death are exhausted; the holy life has been lived; what was to be done is done; there is no further becoming.

Virtue, concentration, wisdom — step upon step, and where does the stair finally open? Onto "exhausting the three taints" — the exhaustion of taints, again. Later, at the village of Ambagāma — by then the Buddha had already relinquished his remaining span of life, the date of his passing fixed — the sutta records him giving the assembly these same words once more. A teacher on the road to his own passing, and what he repeated along that road was not any wonder or power, but where the whole path leads: the taints exhausted, liberation, birth and death ended, no further becoming.

### The Road This Book Will Walk

The first look inside the door has seen exactly this much. The mastery is the signature of exhaustion — in the Deer Park's definition the name appears with the word "done," and everything after it is the language of settlement. The seal and the thing sealed are set down in one impression, with no before and after — co-arisen, not a seam's width between. And what is sealed is the freedom of "nothing left to bind," not the abilities of "more things one can do" — the head and tail of the seven points of skill frame that for good.

From here the book reads the signature face by face. Part Two: the substance of the freedom — the liberation of mind, the liberation by wisdom, and the weight of the word "unshakable." Part Three: the three knowledges and six powers — where the account between powers and freedom is settled in full. Part Four: the attainment of cessation — the freedom inside stillness. Part Five: "no further becoming" and "worthy of offerings" — how this freedom faces death, and how the world comes to see it. The faces are five; the seal is one: the exhaustion of taints.

The door is behind us now. First, the substance of the freedom.
