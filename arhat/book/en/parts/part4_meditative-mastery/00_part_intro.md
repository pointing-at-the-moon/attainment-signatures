# Part Four · The Eight Liberations and Cessation · Freedom Inside Stillness

Part Three settled the account of the powers: positive competence is not where the mastery lies. Yet one positive attainment has been held back, not yet faced directly — Part Two noted that the both-ways liberated arhat adds one signed line: "the eight liberations, traversed forward and in reverse, witnessed in one's own person." The eighth of that deep series bears the name: the attainment of cessation. Perception and feeling both extinguished; the activities of body, speech, and mind stilled together. The summit of the meditative art, entered by the noble alone.

Stilled to that degree — is the person still there? How does this differ from death? And in a state where even perception and feeling have ceased, by what right is there said to be any "freedom" in it?

This part's single chapter finds the answers in two strikingly homely scenes. One in a mango grove: a lay householder questions a monk about the difference between cessation and death, and presses the question to the bottom. One in Sāvatthī: a laywoman questions a nun, who answers point after point until the answers end at "nirvāṇa has no counterpart" — and the Blessed One afterward sets his seal on her words: had she asked me, I would have said the same sentences.

The deepest of the attainments, and the canon chose the plainest of dialogues to make it clear. Go in.
