## 2.2 No Twigs, No Leaves

The suttas record two kinds of arhat: liberated by wisdom, and liberated both ways. Wherever people see "two kinds," the mind starts seating them in rank: which is higher? Which one has truly arrived?

There is no need to guess. The Buddha did the counting himself, aloud — on the full-moon night that closed a rains retreat.

### The Count on the Full-Moon Night

The Madhyama-āgama records: the Blessed One was at Rājagṛha, in the Bamboo Grove of Kalandaka, keeping the rains with five hundred monks. The three months complete, on the fifteenth day, the night of the mutual invitation — when the assembly invites one another's correction — he took his seat before the monks and opened: I am a brāhmaṇa, and have attained final cessation; the unsurpassed physician-king; this body I now bear is the very last. You are my true sons, born from my mouth, formed by the Dharma.

Śāriputra rose from his seat three times, palms joined. The first question: does the Blessed One find anything to reproach in my conduct of body, speech, or mind? The Buddha said no — and praised him: as a wheel-turning king's crown prince receives and turns the wheel his father turned, the wheel of Dharma I have turned, you can turn after me. The second: does the Blessed One find anything to reproach in these five hundred? The answer: nothing. These five hundred monks have all attained non-attachment; the taints exhausted, the holy life lived, the work done, the burden laid down, the fetters of being ended — save one monk only, and of him too I have already declared that he will reach final knowledge in this very life. Then Śāriputra asked the third question: Blessed One, of these five hundred, how many have attained the three knowledges? How many the liberation both ways? How many the liberation by wisdom? The Buddha answered:

> Śāriputra! Of these five hundred monks, ninety have attained the three penetrating knowledges, ninety have attained the liberation both ways, and the remaining monks the liberation by wisdom. Śāriputra! This assembly has no twigs and no leaves, no knots and no warps: pure, true, standing right.

Read the count slowly. Five hundred whose taints are exhausted: ninety liberated both ways, ninety with the three knowledges — and the rest, more than three hundred, liberated by wisdom. Liberation by wisdom is not the rare exception; it is the greater part of this assembly. And the sentence the Buddha added the moment the count was done is the eye of the whole sutta: **this assembly has no twigs and no leaves** — like one sound timber, no branch, no leaf, no knot: pure and true through and through. If the wisdom-liberated were a second-grade arhat, this assembly would have ranks sticking out of it like branches. The Buddha's own simile closes that door.

### Two Definitions, One Clause Apart

Then where exactly does the difference lie? In the canon the two definitions happen to stand in a single sutta, back to back, and the difference shows itself at a glance.

The Mahānidāna discourse of the Madhyama-āgama opens in Kuru country, at the town of Kammāsadhamma — and it opens strangely. Ānanda, sitting in seclusion, had been thinking: this dependent arising is said to be deep, profoundly deep — yet to my eye it looks utterly plain. The Buddha said: Ānanda, do not say so! This dependent arising is profoundly deep, and deep in its appearing. There followed the deepest of all discourses on dependent arising — and near its end, the Buddha walked Ānanda through the seven stations of consciousness and the two spheres, asking at each: a monk who knows this station as it really is — would he delight in dwelling there? — No. All nine asked and answered, the Buddha said:

> Ānanda! If a monk knows these seven stations of consciousness and two spheres as they really are, and his mind, unstained by attachment, is liberated — this monk is called an arhat, named liberated by wisdom.

Mark it: by the end of that sentence, the word "arhat" has been given in full — the wisdom-liberated one is an arhat, not a syllable withheld. Only then does the text say "furthermore" — there are eight liberations: contemplating form in form; the formless within contemplating form without; the liberation of the beautiful witnessed in one's own person … down through the eighth. And only after the list comes the second definition:

> Ānanda! If a monk knows these seven stations of consciousness and two spheres as they really are, and his mind, unstained by attachment, is liberated — and, in addition, these eight liberations he traverses forward and in reverse, witnessing them in his own person, and by wisdom contemplates the taints exhausted — this monk is called an arhat, named liberated both ways.

Set the two definitions side by side: they differ by exactly one clause — "and these eight liberations he traverses forward and in reverse, witnessing them in his own person." Every other word is identical. The whole difference lies in concentration; none of it lies in the exhaustion.

### Two Hindrances, One Debt Discharged

The treatises put this "differs in concentration, not in the exhaustion" as a matched pair of terms. The *Abhidharmakośa*:

> Those arhats who have attained the cessation-attainment are called liberated both ways, for by the power of both wisdom and concentration they are freed from the hindrance of the afflictions and from the hindrance to liberation. The rest, who have not attained the attainment of cessation, are called liberated by wisdom, for by the power of wisdom alone they are freed from the hindrance of the afflictions.

There are two hindrances. The first is the **hindrance of the afflictions** — greed, hatred, delusion, all the taints, the bondage that ties a person to birth-and-death; the severing of this hindrance is precisely the exhaustion of the taints. The second is the **hindrance to liberation** — also called the hindrance of concentration; what it obstructs is not release from birth-and-death, but the arising of the deepest concentrative states (the eight liberations, the attainment of cessation). Read the treatise's division cleanly: the two kinds of arhat **alike** sever the hindrance of the afflictions — alike in the exhaustion of taints, alike in no-more-becoming; the one liberated both ways is parted, in addition, from a second hindrance, having gained as well the bodily witnessing of the attainment of cessation.

Take an image. Two people have each cleared a whole life's debt — both unburdened, both past the reach of any creditor who could come knocking again; and one of them, besides, has mastered some deep art. Mastery of that art is a further accomplishment, over and above the clearing of the debt — it does not make the debt any "more cleared" for the added skill. A debt cleared admits of no "more cleared"; the taints exhausted admit of no "more exhausted." The "both" in *liberated both ways* is the both of two powers, wisdom and concentration; the extra portion he carries lies in the concentration, not in the exhaustion.

### Why There Are Two Kinds

Then why does it fall into two kinds at all? The canon's answer lies in the faculties one comes with. In a string of suttas on the faculties in the Saṃyukta-āgama — at Jeta's Grove in Sāvatthī, where the Buddha taught the monks the five faculties: faith, energy, mindfulness, concentration, wisdom — one sutta divides it this way:

> If a monk's five faculties are surpassing, keen, and complete, he gains the arhat's liberation in both parts. If they are soft, if they are weak, he gains the bodily witness …

Faculties surpassing, keen, and fully complete: liberation in both parts — that is, liberated both ways. Faculties somewhat soft or weak: in descending order, the bodily-witness, the seen-attainer, the faith-liberated. The variable that does the sorting is the keenness or dullness of the faculties, the depth or shallowness of the concentration attained — and nowhere, first to last, does it speak of a "depth or shallowness in the exhaustion of taints."

There is a second witness, of the homeliest kind. The *Field of Merit* discourse of the Madhyama-āgama records: the householder Anāthapiṇḍika came to the Victor's Grove and asked the Buddha — how many kinds of person are a field of merit in the world? A donor giving alms always wants to know whose receiving makes the gift bear most. The Buddha answered: two kinds, the learner and the one beyond learning; eighteen of the learners, nine of those beyond learning. Counting the nine beyond learning one by one, "liberated by wisdom" and "liberated both ways" stand shoulder to shoulder among them — both beyond learning, both a field of merit, both worthy of the offerings of gods and men. On the very roster the Buddha taught to a giver, the two stand level, with no upper and no lower seat between them.

### One and the Same Summit

Gather the chapter into one image. The exhaustion of taints is like reaching a summit: two people stand on the same peak — to have reached the top is to have reached it, and there is no "more reached the top." One of them can also walk the ridge and look out over all four quarters (the deep concentration of the eight liberations, witnessed forward and in reverse); the other simply stands at the top. The one who stands at the top has not, for "never having looked out," somehow "not reached the top." The summit is one and the same; whether or not one looks out is a further view to be had at the top, not a difference of height.

In this book's terms: the seal of exhaustion is one and the same for the two kinds; the one liberated both ways has, beside that single seal, countersigned one further line — *mastery in concentration*. What is added is a countersignature, not a deeper seal. Let no one who "has not gained the deepest concentration" fall to doubting himself — "my taints are not exhausted, my mastery not whole." The seal of mastery hangs on the ending of the hindrance of the afflictions, not on the depth of one's concentration.

### Onward

Only — in that count on the full-moon night, one figure was left unaccounted: ninety monks have attained the three knowledges. The three knowledges — three kinds of knowing — what are they? And behind them stands a still more dazzling word: the higher powers. The next part goes to reckon that most dazzling account of all.
