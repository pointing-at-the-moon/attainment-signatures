## 2.1 The King's Named Jewel

The last chapter brought us through the door: the mastery is the seal of exhaustion, and what is sealed is "nothing left to bind." But standing inside, the first question is this one — is the freedom secure? Could it, some day, slip back? A freedom that can still be gained and lost — has it reached its floor?

The canon's answer rests on one word: *unshakable*. To feel the weight this word carried for the Buddha, go to a full-moon night at the Bamboo Grove.

### Thirty Possessions, and One Jewel

The Madhyama-āgama records: the Blessed One was staying at Rājagṛha, in the Bamboo Grove of Kalandaka, keeping the rains retreat with a great assembly of monks. On the fifteenth day, the full moon, at the recitation, he took his seat before the assembly, entered concentration, and surveyed the monks' minds — and saw the whole assembly sitting silent, utterly silent, free of drowsiness, seated deep, utterly deep, stilled to the last, subtle to the last. He called Śāriputra and said: an assembly such as this — who could be worthy to honor and serve it? Śāriputra answered: no one, Blessed One; only the Blessed One himself.

Then, to Śāriputra, the Buddha counted out the possessions of a king, one by one — and matched each with a monk's discipline. As the king and his ministers have ornaments of every kind, so the monk's ornament is the virtue of the precepts; as the king has drinks of every kind, so the monk's drink is the taste of the Dharma; as the king has the four revenues, so the monk's revenue is the four foundations of mindfulness; as the king has the four armies, so the monk's four armies are the four right efforts; as the king has chariots, so the monk's chariot is calm and insight; as the king has a charioteer, so the monk's charioteer is right mindfulness; as the king has a fine road, level and straight, leading only to the pleasure-park, so the monk's road is the noble eightfold path, level and straight, leading only to nirvāṇa; as the king has a general, so the monk's general is wisdom; as the king has a royal couch, so the monk's couch is unobstructed concentration — thirty in all, not one missing: the whole road's work, virtue, mindfulness, effort, concentration, wisdom, each given its place.

Near the end of the count came the singular one:

> Śāriputra! As the king and his ministers have a named jewel-treasure — even so, Śāriputra, the monk and the nun have the unshakable liberation of mind as their named jewel-treasure.

The named jewel — the one gem that bears a name, the king's alone, the single treasure by which its owner is known. Thirty possessions, all of them royal equipment; this one alone is the mark by which a king is a king. And the Buddha gave that place to the unshakable liberation of mind. Of all the road's works — virtue the ornament, wisdom the general, concentration the couch — only the liberation can be the named jewel; and only the liberation that is *unshakable*.

### What "Unshakable" Means

The exact sense of the word, the treatises state most plainly. The *Abhidharmakośa* (composed by Vasubandhu) glosses the name:

> The unshakable lineage is named last; this itself is called the unshakable liberation of mind, because there is no falling-back and the mind is liberated. It is also called the time-independent liberation, because it does not wait upon occasion and is liberated: samādhi manifests at will, without waiting for the favorable conditions to assemble.

Two senses, both to be held firmly.

The first: **no falling-back** — this liberation has no movement of regression in it. The treatise then cites the sūtra itself, carrying the matter to its limit:

> The sūtra says: the unshakable liberation of mind witnessed in one's own person — I declare definitively that there is no cause or condition from which it could fall back.

"I declare definitively" — the Buddha's word, settled: no cause, no condition exists in the world that could make this liberation fall. Mark the force of the sentence: not "very hard to lose," but **nothing from which it could be lost** — no circumstance anywhere that could take it away.

The second: **not waiting upon occasion** — "samādhi manifests at will, without waiting for the favorable conditions to assemble." To enter concentration is to enter it at once, without waiting for requisites at hand, health intact, a suitable place — without conditions falling luckily into line. Even that last sliver of unfreedom — "able, but only when circumstances permit" — is gone.

Put the two senses together and the word shows its grammar: still subtraction. "Unshakable" does not add a layer of firmness on top of the liberation; it removes the last two places where anxiety could lodge — the gap through which it could be lost, and the dependence on conditions. As "bone-dry" is to "dry": bone-dry is not another kind of moisture, but dryness carried to the point from which no dampness can return. The unshakable is not another liberation; it is the name of the same liberation come to its floor.

The canon also records the contrast. The treatises distinguish six kinds of arhat; the first five are called "time-dependent in liberation" — the gloss runs: "constantly cherishing and guarding it, and liberated in mind … entering concentration only when requisites, health, place, and the favorable conditions assemble." Attained — yet needing to be guarded at all times, for fear of loss; concentration entered only when circumstances permit. The sixth alone is named "unshakable by nature": manifesting at will, with nothing from which to fall. Whether an arhat can in fact regress, and what regression would even mean, the early schools disputed — this book does not enter that dispute; it takes only the contrast, to see the unshakable by its own light. To hold something fast is still to have a holder and a held; to have nothing that could be lost — that alone is through to the bottom.

### A Debt Cleared, and Cleared Past Reopening

The state the word names, the Buddha taught in another place, in the homeliest of terms.

The Madhyama-āgama records: the Blessed One, at Jeta's Grove in Sāvatthī, put to the monks a string of worldly questions. For a person of desires, is poverty a great suffering? — It is, Blessed One. And going into debt? — It is. And the interest mounting upon the debt? — It is. The creditor demanding payment? The creditor coming again and again? Being seized and bound at last by the creditor? — It is; it is; it is.

Then the Buddha turned the figure. In my noble teaching, to be without faith in good states is the poverty; misconduct of body, speech, and mind is the borrowing; concealing that misconduct, unwilling to confess it, is the interest mounting; being pointed out by one's fellows in the holy life is the creditor demanding; thoughts of desire, ill-will, and harm arising in solitude are the creditor coming again and again; and the fall, at the body's breaking, into evil destinations is the seizure and binding. And the arhat whose taints are exhausted — these three sufferings of bondage he has known and extinguished, pulled up by the root, never to come again. Then the Buddha gathered the teaching in verse, and the closing lines run:

> Having gained the bliss that needs no feeding, as one bathed clean in water: the unshakable liberation of mind — all the fetters of being exhausted. Without sickness: that is nirvāṇa, called the unsurpassed lamp; without sorrow, without dust, at ease — this is named the immovable.

The first chapter read the arhat's self-attestation as a statement of accounts settled — the account is closed. This verse carries the settlement one step further: *the unshakable liberation of mind — all the fetters of being exhausted* — not merely that the account is paid, but that the ledger itself can never be reopened. A debt once cleared can be borrowed again; the clearing of the unshakable pulls up by the root the very possibility of owing. And the verse's final word is exactly this: immovable.

### Onward

The unshakable is the floor of the freedom. But the suttas plainly record one further thing: there are two kinds of arhat — liberated by wisdom, and liberated both ways. One exhaustion of taints; how does it come in two kinds? Is one of them higher? That account the Buddha settled aloud, on another full-moon night. The next chapter goes to that night.
