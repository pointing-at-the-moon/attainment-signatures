# Cessation-Attainment

**Why the Freedom Within Stilling Is the Deepest Signature**

*中文主題：滅盡定——止息中的自在何以是最深之簽名*

## Abstract

This paper opens Part 4, "The Eight Liberations and Cessation-Attainment," treating the aspect, within the arhat's mastery, of the deepest concentration — *cessation-attainment* (the cessation of perception-and-feeling, *nirodha-samāpatti*). P03 already established that "concentrative mastery is a distinct face, not the face of taints," and P05 broke "positive competence is where mastery lies"; this paper must answer a sharper question: cessation-attainment being the deepest concentration (the eighth of the eight liberations, the summit of the nine successive attainments, gained by the noble alone), what is it within the arhat's mastery? The "highest mastery," or still only "one face of concentrative mastery"? The thesis: cessation-attainment is the *deepest signature* of concentrative mastery, yet still a face of "concentrative mastery," not a higher "mastery of exhaustion" — holding to the frame of P01 and P03. Its distinctiveness is twofold, both concerning "freedom within stilling": the three formations of body, speech, and mind all cease, yet life-warmth does not depart and the faculties are not broken, unlike death (the deepest stilling, yet not annihilation); and only the noble gain it by the power of the noble path (the worldling cannot arouse it, fearing annihilation), and to gain it is to become liberated both-ways — so it is the demarcating mark of "liberation both-ways," the apex of concentrative mastery. The argument runs in three layers: the sequence and nature of the eight liberations, nine successive attainments, and cessation-attainment; cessation-attainment's distinctiveness as "stilling yet not death, supreme bliss surpassing neither-perception-nor-non-perception"; and the criterion of mastery — cessation-attainment is the apex of concentrative mastery as "freedom in arousing-and-ceasing," the deepest signature within the reach of liberation both-ways, yet not an augmentation of the mastery of exhaustion. Conclusion: within stilling there is yet freedom — one in cessation-attainment, within the deepest stilling, still gains "freedom in arousing-and-ceasing," the deepest seal of concentrative mastery; yet what the seal seals is still exhaustion (only the exhausted enter without fear), so the deepest concentrative signature too turns, in the end, about the pivot of exhaustion.

**Keywords**: cessation-attainment, cessation of perception-and-feeling, eight liberations, nine successive attainments, liberation both-ways, stilling, gained by the noble alone, freedom in arousing-and-ceasing, concentrative mastery, nirvāṇa here-and-now, nirodha-samāpatti

## 摘要

本篇開第4部，論最深定境滅盡定（想受滅）於阿羅漢自在中之相。本文主張：滅盡定是定自在之最深簽名，然仍屬定自在一面，非漏盡自在之更高（守 P01·P03）。其特殊處有二，皆關乎止息中的自在：三行俱滅而壽暖不離·諸根不壞，異於死；唯聖者由聖道力得，得之即成俱解脫。判準：滅盡定是「於起滅得自在」之定自在極點、俱解脫所及之最深定相，非漏盡自在之增上。結論：止息中仍有自在，然印之所印仍是漏盡——唯漏盡者無怖而入，故最深之定簽名終亦繞漏盡之樞。

**關鍵詞**：滅盡定、想受滅、八解脫、九次第定、俱解脫、止息、唯聖者得、起滅自在、定自在、nirodha-samāpatti

## I. The Live Question

Cessation-attainment (the cessation of perception-and-feeling) is the summit of Buddhist concentration-learning: the eighth of the eight liberations, the last of the nine successive attainments, arousable by the noble alone. Even "perception" and "feeling" are stilled — the subtlest activity of mind too is quieted. Facing so extreme a point, the axis must answer a sharp question: what place does cessation-attainment hold within the arhat's mastery?

The sharpness is this: cessation-attainment being the "deepest concentration," is it then the "highest mastery"? If so, then "mastery has degrees," and P01's frame (the exhaustion of taints is mastery, the signature one and the same) would loosen; P03's judgment (concentrative mastery is a distinct face, not the face of taints; the two kinds of arhat are equal in exhaustion) would need re-estimating. The paper must give a settlement, between "cessation-attainment is indeed the deepest concentration" and "the signature of mastery is one and the same (exhaustion)," that does not conflict.

The thesis: cessation-attainment is the *deepest signature of concentrative mastery*, yet still a face of "concentrative mastery," not a higher "mastery of exhaustion" — holding to P01 and P03. Its depth is the depth of "concentration" (the apex of stilling), not a greater depth of "exhaustion" (exhaustion has no two graded levels, P03). Its distinctiveness is twofold, both bound to the paradox of "freedom within stilling": the three formations of body, speech, and mind all cease, yet life-warmth does not depart and the faculties are not broken, unlike death — the deepest stilling, yet not annihilation; and only the noble gain it by the power of the noble path (the worldling cannot arouse it, fearing annihilation), and "gaining freedom in arousing-and-ceasing it" is to become liberated both-ways — so cessation-attainment is the demarcating mark of "liberation both-ways," the apex of concentrative mastery.

The key insight is "within stilling there is yet freedom." Common sense takes "stilling" and "freedom" as opposites — once stilled (even perception-and-feeling ceased), whence freedom? But cessation-attainment shows precisely this: within the deepest stilling there is yet a freedom — "freedom in arousing-and-ceasing" (entering and emerging, arousing and ceasing it, by the power of the noble path). This freedom is not "still being able to act within the stilling" (that would not be cessation-attainment) but "the autonomy over the entering, emerging, arousing, and ceasing of this deepest stilling." Yet this freedom is still concentrative mastery (autonomy over a concentration), not the mastery of exhaustion (the unobstructedness of liberation). And what the seal seals is still exhaustion: only the exhausted (the noble) can enter this attainment without fear (the worldling, fearing annihilation, cannot arouse it) — so the deepest concentrative signature too turns, in the end, about the pivot of exhaustion.

This paper opens Part 4, "The Eight Liberations and Cessation-Attainment." The argument: §II presents canon and śāstra (the sequence of the eight liberations and the eighth = cessation-attainment; the nine successive attainments; cessation-attainment gained by the noble alone and liberation both-ways); §III argues in three layers (stilling yet not death; gained by the noble alone and so the mark of liberation both-ways; freedom in arousing-and-ceasing as the apex of concentrative mastery, not an augmentation of exhaustion); §IV is cut; §V corrects; §VI concludes.

## II. Canonical Basis

### 2.1 The Sequence of the Eight Liberations and the Eighth = Cessation-Attainment

Cessation-attainment's place in the system of concentration first appears in the sequence of the eight liberations. The *Madhyama-āgama*, fascicle 24 (the Mahānidāna), enumerates the eight, ending at the eighth:

> Furthermore, Ānanda! there are eight liberations. What eight? Having form, one contemplates form — the first liberation. Further, inwardly without form-perception, one contemplates form externally — the second. Further, the pure liberation, realized with the body and dwelt in fully — the third … Surpassing every base of neither-perception-nor-non-perception, the liberation of the cessation of perception-and-knowing, realized with the body and dwelt in fully, and the taints exhausted and known by the contemplation of wisdom — this is the eighth liberation.[^1]

The eight liberations begin from "having form, one contemplates form," pass through the four formless attainments, and end at the eighth, "the liberation of the cessation of perception-and-knowing (cessation-attainment), realized with the body." Note that scripture appends to the eighth a clause: "and the taints exhausted and known by the contemplation of wisdom" — the consummation of the eighth liberation is "the bodily realization of the cessation of perception-and-feeling" (the deepest concentration) *together with* "the taints exhausted and known by wisdom" (wisdom's exhaustion). So cessation-attainment as realized in the arhat must coexist with the wisdom-vision of exhaustion (see 2.4, 3.2). The śāstra plainly judges the eighth liberation to be cessation-attainment, *Abhidharmakośa*, fascicle 29 (Chapter on Concentration):

> There are eight liberations: first, the liberation of contemplating outer forms with inner form-perception; second, of contemplating outer forms without inner form-perception; third, the pure liberation realized with the body and dwelt in; the four formless attainments as the next four liberations; the attainment of the cessation of feeling-and-perception as the eighth liberation. Of the eight, the first three have non-greed as their nature, being a proximate cure of greed … the eighth liberation just is cessation-attainment.[^2]

The śāstra judges the nature of the eight: the first three have non-greed as their nature (a proximate cure of greed), the next four are the four formless attainments, "the eighth liberation just is cessation-attainment (the cessation of feeling-and-perception)." Cessation-attainment is the end of the eight liberations, the deepest of the concentrations.

### 2.2 The Nine Successive Attainments and "Gaining Freedom, Entering at Will"

Cessation-attainment is also the last of the nine successive attainments (the four meditations, the four formless, and cessation-attainment). The *Kośa*, fascicle 5, discussing the nine successive attainments, touches a crucial sense of "freedom":

> [The view that] the fourth meditation has cessation-attainment necessarily fails. Why? Because the nine successive attainments are taught by the scripture. If this must be so, how can there be the sense of a "leaping attainment"? This attainment's "successiveness" is taught with reference to the beginner; when one has gained freedom, one leaps in at will.[^3]

The śāstra cites "the nine successive attainments are taught by the scripture" to prove cessation-attainment's place in sequence (reached necessarily in order, hence only at the summit of existence); yet it also judges "this attainment's successiveness is taught with reference to the beginner; when one has gained freedom, one leaps in at will" — the "must follow the sequence" of the successive attainments is said for the beginner, but *when one has gained freedom, one can leap over and enter at will* (the leaping attainment). The three words "gained freedom" here are the paper's eye: one fluent and autonomous over the concentrations can leap the sequence and enter and emerge at will. The eight root attainments underlying the nine successive attainments, *Kośa*, fascicle 28:

> The meditations and formless [attainments] here discussed, the root attainments, are eight in all; of these the first seven each have three [kinds], the attainment of the summit of existence has only two, this stage being dull and inferior, without the taintless.[^4]

The root attainments are eight (four meditations, four formless), the first seven each having the savoring, pure, and taintless kinds, only the summit of existence (neither-perception-nor-non-perception) being dull and inferior, with the savoring and pure only (no taintless). These eight root attainments plus cessation-attainment compose the concentration-ground structure of the nine successive attainments. Cessation-attainment is not in the list of these eight root attainments (it is the cessation of perception-and-feeling, not an ordinary attainment) but is the capstone of the nine successive — above every concentration-with-mind.

### 2.3 Cessation-Attainment Differs from Death and from the No-Perception Attainment: The Deepest Stilling, Yet Not Annihilation

Cessation-attainment is most easily mistaken for "like death" — perception-and-feeling both ceased, how does it differ from death? Scripture plainly judges the difference. *Saṃyukta-āgama*, fascicle 21 (sūtra 568):

> Casting off life-warmth, the faculties all broken, body and life parted — this is called death. In cessation-attainment the formations of body, speech, and mind cease, yet life is not cast off, warmth does not depart, the faculties are not broken, and body and life remain conjoined — this is the difference in mark between dying and entering the attainment of cessation.[^5]

Death: casting off life-warmth, the faculties broken, body and life parted. Cessation-attainment: the three formations of body, speech, mind ceased (the deepest stilling), yet "life is not cast off, warmth does not depart, the faculties are not broken, body and life remain conjoined." The three formations all cease, yet life-warmth-faculties-life persist — this is cessation-attainment's paradox: the deepest stilling (even perception-and-feeling quieted), yet not annihilation (the base of life still abides). The Madhyama-āgama clarifies it by a double demarcation, *Madhyama-āgama*, fascicle 58 (the Dharmadinnā):

> In death, the life-span is exhausted, warmth has gone, the faculties are decayed. In a monk who has entered cessation-attainment, the life-span is not exhausted, warmth too has not gone, the faculties are not decayed … In a monk who has entered cessation-attainment, perception and knowing cease. In one who has entered the no-perception attainment, perception and knowing do not cease.[^6]

A double demarcation: against "death" — cessation-attainment's life-span, warmth, and faculties are unbroken, differing from death; against "the no-perception attainment" — cessation-attainment's "perception and knowing cease" (both), differing from the no-perception attainment (where perception and knowing do not cease). So cessation-attainment surpasses the no-perception attainment in "depth of stilling" (the no-perception attainment ceases perception only, cessation-attainment ceases both perception and knowing), and differs from death in "not being annihilation" (life-warmth-faculties-life still abide). It is a "deepest yet still living" stilling — and this is precisely why "freedom within stilling" is possible: the stilling though extreme, life is not severed, so there remains the room of autonomy to "arouse and to cease." The cessation of perception-and-feeling as the peak of concentration-bliss, *Saṃyukta-āgama*, fascicle 17 (sūtra 485):

> A monk, surpassing every base of neither-perception-nor-non-perception, the cessation of perception-and-feeling realized with the body and dwelt in fully — this is the surpassing bliss beyond that … "The recluse, the Śākya's son, speaks of the cessation of perception-and-feeling alone as the supreme bliss."[^7]

The cessation of perception-and-feeling (realized with the body and dwelt in) surpasses neither-perception-nor-non-perception, the "surpassing bliss beyond that" — the Buddha "speaks of the cessation of perception-and-feeling alone as the supreme bliss." Mark this: this "supreme bliss" is not the bliss of feeling (feeling has ceased) but the "bliss of cessation" of the apex of stilling — taking the cessation of all feeling as bliss, precisely the bliss peculiar to "within stilling."

### 2.4 Cessation-Attainment Gained by the Noble Alone, Freedom in Arousing-and-Ceasing, Becoming Liberated Both-Ways

The threefold mark of cessation-attainment (noble alone, the power of the noble path, becoming liberated both-ways), *Kośa*, fascicle 5 (Chapter on the Faculties):

> This cessation-attainment is gained by the noble alone, not arousable by the worldling — because of the fear of annihilation, because it is arousable only by the power of the noble path, because it is entered by the resolute conviction of nirvāṇa here-and-now … The World-Honored One had not formerly aroused cessation-attainment; how, on gaining the knowledge of exhaustion, did he become liberated both-ways? Because he gained freedom in arousing-and-ceasing the attainment — like one who has aroused it, he became liberated both-ways.[^8]

The threefold mark: first, "gained by the noble alone, not arousable by the worldling" — the worldling cannot arouse cessation-attainment, "because of the fear of annihilation" (facing the cessation of perception-and-feeling, the ordinary person fears it as death and cannot enter); only the noble can arouse it "by the power of the noble path," entering it "by the resolute conviction of nirvāṇa here-and-now" (understanding it as a nirvāṇa-realm realizable here-and-now, hence without fear). Second, the śāstra resolves a difficulty by the example of the World-Honored One: when the World-Honored One gained the knowledge of exhaustion (became an arhat), he need not have first aroused cessation-attainment, so how did he become liberated both-ways? The answer: "because he gained freedom in arousing-and-ceasing the attainment, like one who has aroused it, he became liberated both-ways" — gaining freedom over "arousing and ceasing cessation-attainment" (the capability to arouse and enter it at any time), he became, like one who has aroused it, liberated both-ways. See clearly: the key to becoming liberated both-ways is "freedom in arousing-and-ceasing the attainment" (the autonomous capability to enter cessation-attainment), not "a deeper exhaustion." Third, cessation-attainment is the demarcating mark of liberation both-ways, *Kośa*, fascicle 25 (Chapter on Concentration; continuing P03):

> Those arhats who have gained cessation-attainment are named liberated both-ways, because by the power of wisdom and concentration they are liberated from the hindrance of defilement and the hindrance to liberation. The rest, who have not gained cessation-attainment, are named liberated by wisdom, because by the power of wisdom alone they are liberated from the hindrance of defilement.[^9]

Those who gain cessation-attainment are named liberated both-ways (by the twin powers of wisdom and concentration, liberated from the hindrance of defilement and the hindrance to liberation), those who have not are named liberated by wisdom (by wisdom alone, from the hindrance of defilement) — this is the discrimination treated in full in P03. The Āgama layer too demarcates by the threefold of the bodily touching of the eight liberations, *Madhyama-āgama*, fascicle 51 (the Assaji):

> How is a monk liberated both-ways? If a monk dwells having touched the eight liberations with the body, and has by wisdom seen the taints exhausted and known, such a monk is liberated both-ways … If a monk dwells not having touched the eight liberations with the body, but by wisdom has seen the taints exhausted and known, such a monk is liberated by wisdom … If a monk dwells having touched the eight liberations with the body, but has not by wisdom seen the taints exhausted and known, such a monk is a body-witness.[^10]

By the presence or absence of "the bodily touching of the eight liberations" (including the eighth, cessation-attainment) combined with "the wisdom-vision of exhaustion," it demarcates three: liberated both-ways, liberated by wisdom, body-witness — one with both the bodily touching of the eight liberations *and* the wisdom-vision of exhaustion is liberated both-ways; one without the bodily touching but with the wisdom-vision of exhaustion is liberated by wisdom; one with the bodily touching but *without* the wisdom-vision of exhaustion is a body-witness (not exhausted, not an arhat). The point is in the third: even possessing the bodily touching of the eight liberations (including the deepest concentration, cessation-attainment), if without the wisdom-vision of exhaustion, one is merely a "body-witness," not an arhat — showing that the bodily realization of cessation-attainment of itself does not exhaust taints (one must see exhaustion by wisdom to become liberated both-ways). This proves cessation-attainment belongs to the face of concentration (concentrative mastery), not the face of taints (exhaustion).

> ✅ Doctrinally sound: 2.1 (the eighth liberation = cessation-attainment, appended with "the contemplation of wisdom, the taints exhausted") and 2.3 (cessation-attainment differs from death and the no-perception attainment; the cessation of perception-and-feeling as supreme bliss) are explicit canon; 2.2 (the nine successive attainments, gaining freedom and entering at will, the eight root attainments) and 2.4 (cessation-attainment gained by the noble alone, freedom in arousing-and-ceasing, the demarcation of liberation both-ways) are Kośa śāstric precision (Chapter on Concentration, Chapter on the Faculties). The MĀ195 threefold of 2.4 (one with the bodily touching of the eight liberations but without the wisdom-vision of exhaustion is a mere body-witness) is explicit canon, directly proving cessation-attainment belongs to the face of concentration, not the face of taints. The realm-bindings of cessation-attainment, the fine analysis of the mind-and-mental-factors of entering and emerging, and the like lie beyond this paper's theme and are not unfolded.

## III. Core Argument

### 3.1 Stilling Yet Not Death: Why Within the Deepest Stilling There Is Yet Freedom

"Freedom within stilling" at first sounds contradictory — once stilled (the three formations of body-speech-mind ceased, perception-and-feeling ceased), what room for freedom? This section first resolves the paradox: cessation-attainment's stilling, though extreme, is not annihilation, so there remains the room of autonomy to "arouse and to cease."

The ground is the double demarcation of 2.3. Cessation-attainment differs from death: the dead cast off life-warmth, the faculties broken, body and life parted; in cessation-attainment the three formations cease yet "life-warmth does not depart, the faculties are not broken, body and life remain conjoined." It also differs from the no-perception attainment: in cessation-attainment perception and knowing both cease (deeper stilling), in the no-perception attainment they do not. So cessation-attainment is a "deepest yet still living" stilling — the subtlest activity of mind (perception, feeling) is quieted, yet the base of life (life, warmth, faculties, the life-faculty) still abides. Just because life is not severed, this stilling is not a terminus (not death) but a concentration-realm that "can be entered, emerged from, aroused, and ceased."

So "freedom within stilling" stands: it is not "still having activity within the stilling" (that would not be cessation-attainment) but "the autonomy over the entering, emerging, arousing, and ceasing of this deepest stilling." To enter this deepest stilling without fear (not dreading it as death), to emerge at the fitting time (life-warmth-faculties-life unbroken, hence able to arise again) — this autonomy of entering-emerging-arousing-ceasing is just "freedom within stilling." The ordinary person's stilling (as sleep, fainting) is passive, not autonomous; cessation-attainment's stilling is active, autonomous — and this autonomy is the deepest aspect of concentrative mastery.

A simile: to dive to the deepest sea-floor and yet sink and rise at will, versus to drown and sink — both are "submerged in water," yet one is freedom (able to sink and rise, the breath-channel unbroken), one is going under (passive sinking, breath stopped). Cessation-attainment to death is as autonomous diving to drowning: both the deepest submersion (stilling), yet one has "the freedom of arousing-and-ceasing" (life-warmth not departed), one is annihilation (the faculties already broken). Within stilling there is yet freedom, precisely because that stilling is an autonomous dive, not a passive drowning.

### 3.2 Gained by the Noble Alone, and So the Mark of Liberation Both-Ways: The Bodily Realization of Cessation-Attainment Still Belongs to the Face of Concentration, and One Becomes an Arhat Only by the Wisdom-Vision of Exhaustion

Cessation-attainment is "gained by the noble alone" (2.4); this sense of "noble alone" must be finely distinguished from P04's "the power of exhaustion is restricted to the noble" — both are "noble alone," yet the reason for being noble-alone differs, and so does the consequence.

The power of exhaustion's being noble-alone is because it just is the cognitive face of exhaustion (the self-knowing of exhaustion, had by the exhausted alone, P04). Cessation-attainment's being noble-alone has a different reason: "because of the fear of annihilation, because it is arousable only by the power of the noble path, because it is entered by the resolute conviction of nirvāṇa here-and-now" (2.4). The worldling cannot arouse cessation-attainment, not because "lacking exhaustion he lacks the cognition," but because of "the fear of annihilation" — facing the deepest stilling of perception-and-feeling both ceased, the ordinary person dreads it as death and cannot enter; only the noble (who has severed the view of self, realized non-self) can enter it by "the resolute conviction of nirvāṇa here-and-now" (understanding this deepest stilling as a nirvāṇa-realm realizable here-and-now, hence without fear). So cessation-attainment's being noble-alone hangs on "the capability to enter without fear" (the power of the noble path, the conviction of non-self), a matter of concentration (the capability to enter the attainment), not the cognition of exhaustion itself.

More crucially, the MĀ195 threefold of 2.4 directly proves cessation-attainment belongs to the face of concentration: "one who has touched the eight liberations (including cessation-attainment) with the body but has *not* by wisdom seen the taints exhausted is a body-witness" — even possessing the deepest concentration of cessation-attainment, if without the wisdom-vision of exhaustion, one is merely a "body-witness" (a noble station, yet not exhausted, not an arhat). So the bodily realization of cessation-attainment *of itself does not exhaust taints*; there must separately be "the wisdom-vision of exhaustion" to become an arhat liberated both-ways. This nails down cessation-attainment's belonging: it is the face of concentration (concentrative mastery), not the face of taints (exhaustion). One who gains cessation-attainment is named "liberated both-ways" because he has both "exhaustion (wisdom)" *and* "the gaining of cessation-attainment (concentration)" (P03) — cessation-attainment is the "concentration" portion, exhaustion the "taint-face" portion; cessation-attainment demarcates only "whether both (also concentration)," not "whether exhausted" (in exhaustion the two kinds are equal, P03).

So cessation-attainment is the demarcating mark of liberation both-ways (gaining it, both-ways; not, by-wisdom), yet this demarcation is a demarcation of "the presence or absence of concentration," not of "the depth of exhaustion." Continuing the consistency of P03 and P05: positive competences (the concentrations, the powers) are all a distinct face, only exhaustion is where mastery lies; cessation-attainment, though the deepest concentration, is no exception — it is the deepest of concentrative mastery, not a greater depth of the mastery of exhaustion.

> ✅ Doctrinally sound: cessation-attainment's being noble-alone hangs on "the capability to enter without fear" (the power of the noble path, the resolute conviction of nirvāṇa here-and-now, no fear of annihilation through non-self), a capability of entering the attainment, not the cognition of exhaustion (distinct from P04's power-of-exhaustion being noble-alone). The MĀ195 threefold plainly proves: one with the bodily touching of the eight liberations but without the wisdom-vision of exhaustion is a mere body-witness (not an arhat) — the bodily realization of cessation-attainment of itself does not exhaust taints, one must see exhaustion by wisdom to become liberated both-ways. So cessation-attainment belongs to the face of concentration (concentrative mastery), not the face of taints. Continuing P03 and P05.

### 3.3 Freedom in Arousing-and-Ceasing: Cessation-Attainment Is the Apex of Concentrative Mastery, Not an Augmentation of the Mastery of Exhaustion

Finally, judge cessation-attainment's place by this axis's frame. P03 established "concentrative mastery is a distinct face, not the face of taints"; P05 established "positive competence is not where mastery lies, only exhaustion is mastery." This paper must, under this frame, settle the fact that "cessation-attainment is the deepest concentration" — it is the deepest, but what is deepest is "concentrative mastery," not "the mastery of exhaustion."

The criterion is the phrase of 2.4, "because he gained freedom in arousing-and-ceasing the attainment, like one who has aroused it, he became liberated both-ways." The key to becoming liberated both-ways, the śāstra plainly says, is "freedom in arousing-and-ceasing the attainment" — the autonomous capability over "arousing, entering, emerging, and ceasing" cessation-attainment. These words "freedom" point to cessation-attainment's exact place in the genealogy of mastery: it is "freedom in arousing-and-ceasing," the autonomy over the entering-emerging-arousing-ceasing of the deepest concentration — and this is the *apex of concentrative mastery* (autonomy over concentration, here reaching its limit, where even the deepest cessation of perception-and-feeling can be autonomously aroused and ceased).

Yet this apex is still the apex of "concentrative mastery," not a higher "mastery of exhaustion." Three grounds, all continuing the prior papers: first (continuing 3.2), the bodily realization of cessation-attainment does not exhaust taints (MĀ195: one with the touching but without the wisdom-vision is a mere body-witness), so it neither increases nor decreases on the axis of exhaustion, and so is not an augmentation of the mastery of exhaustion. Second (continuing P03), exhaustion has no two graded levels (the exhaustion of taints just is the forever-ending of all taints), the two kinds of arhat equal in exhaustion — the wisdom-liberated arhat without cessation-attainment is in no way deficient in the mastery of exhaustion, the both-ways arhat with cessation-attainment is in no way increased in it. What cessation-attainment increases is the face of concentrative mastery (one further measure of autonomy over the deepest concentration), not the quantity of the mastery of exhaustion. Third (continuing P05), positive competence (the concentrations) is a distinct face of mastery, not where mastery lies; cessation-attainment is the deepest concentration-realm, still the deepest of a distinct face, not a greater depth of "where mastery lies" itself.

So cessation-attainment is "the deepest signature of concentrative mastery" — within the deepest stilling (perception-and-feeling both ceased) it still gains the freedom of arousing-and-ceasing, the apex that concentrative mastery can reach, the deepest concentration-mark within the reach of liberation both-ways. But the "deepest" of "deepest signature" is the deepest of concentration, not a greater depth of exhaustion; it inscribes the thickest stroke of the face of "concentrative mastery," not a degree higher upon the signature of "exhaustion." Holding to P01, P03, P05: the signature of mastery (exhaustion) is one and the same, with no higher and lower; cessation-attainment merely lays the deepest stroke upon the countersignature of "concentrative mastery."

> ⚠️ A note on level: this paper's judgment that "cessation-attainment is the apex of concentrative mastery, not an augmentation of the mastery of exhaustion" is established on this axis's frame ("mastery = the signature of exhaustion," continuing P01, P03, P05). It does not claim cessation-attainment is of no supreme value — as the resolute conviction of nirvāṇa here-and-now, the peak of concentration-bliss (the cessation of perception-and-feeling as supreme bliss), and what makes one liberated both-ways, scripture exalts it fully, the summit of concentration-learning. The paper shows only: with respect to "the mastery of exhaustion," cessation-attainment is the apex of the concentration-face, not an increase of the taint-face. The apex of the concentration-face and the mastery of the taint-face are two matters not to be conflated — the former has degrees (cessation-attainment its apex), the latter is one and the same without higher and lower (exhaustion).

## IV. Interdisciplinary Convergence

(This paper deliberately omits the interdisciplinary section; the reason is given in §VI. The structure of "within the deepest stilling there is yet autonomy" does resemble modern consciousness studies' "minimal states of consciousness," "reversible deep sedation," or the contemplative-neuroscience description of "contentless awareness"; yet these argue on the experiential plane of "states of consciousness / brain," while cessation-attainment's "perception-and-feeling both ceased yet life-warmth undeparted" involves the special judgment of "the mind-dharmas temporarily severed while the life-faculty persists" (the schools dispute whether cessation-attainment has mind or no mind), a sense bound to the Buddhist-specific categories of non-self, the unconditioned, and the life-faculty, only superficially like neuroscience's description of consciousness-levels. To force "minimal states of consciousness" upon "the cessation of perception-and-feeling" would lead the reader to take cessation-attainment for "a deep hibernation of the brain," losing its liberative context of "the power of the noble path, the resolute conviction of nirvāṇa here-and-now, no fear through non-self." Better to leave it out.)

## V. Madhyamaka Corrections

**❌ Misreading one: "cessation-attainment is the deepest concentration, and so the highest mastery (the deepest exhaustion, the highest stage)."** 3.3 breaks this. Cessation-attainment's depth is the depth of concentration (the apex of stilling), not a greater depth of exhaustion. Exhaustion has no two graded levels (P03), the two kinds of arhat equal in exhaustion — the wisdom-liberated without cessation-attainment in no way deficient in the mastery of exhaustion, the both-ways with it in no way increased. What cessation-attainment increases is the face of concentrative mastery, not the quantity of the mastery of exhaustion. To take "deepest concentration" for "highest mastery" confuses the concentration-face with the taint-face, falsifying the height of mastery by the depth of concentration (mastery = exhaustion, one and the same without higher and lower).

**❌ Misreading two: "to gain cessation-attainment is to exhaust taints, to become an arhat."** 3.2 breaks this. The MĀ195 threefold plainly proves: one who has touched the eight liberations (including cessation-attainment) with the body but is without the wisdom-vision of exhaustion is merely a "body-witness" — a noble station, yet not exhausted, not an arhat. The bodily realization of cessation-attainment of itself does not exhaust taints; there must separately be the wisdom-vision of exhaustion to become an arhat liberated both-ways. To take "gaining cessation-attainment" for "exhausting taints" mistakes the capability of concentration for the severing of taints — the two plainly distinct (one who gains cessation must still see exhaustion by wisdom).

**❌ Misreading three: "cessation-attainment just is nirvāṇa, just is annihilation (like death)."** 3.1 breaks this. Cessation-attainment's life-warmth undeparted, faculties unbroken, body and life conjoined, differs from death (2.3); it is the "entry by resolute conviction" of nirvāṇa here-and-now (entered by understanding it as a nirvāṇa-realm realizable here-and-now, 2.4), a here-and-now enjoyment of "concentration," not "nirvāṇa-without-remainder" itself, still less annihilation. To take cessation-attainment for annihilation (like death) contradicts its explicit "life-warmth-faculties-life unbroken"; to equate it outright with nirvāṇa-without-remainder confuses "the temporary entry of a concentration-realm" with "the ultimate nirvāṇa." (The proper discrimination of nirvāṇa and no-more-becoming awaits P07.)

**⚠️ Correction four: this paper is confined to the single sense of cessation-attainment's "criterion of mastery" and does not exhaust its full import.** This paper, on the frame of "the mastery of exhaustion," shows cessation-attainment to be the apex of the concentration-face, not an increase of the taint-face; as to cessation-attainment's having-mind-or-no-mind, the fine analysis of the entering-and-emerging mind, how the life-faculty persists, the realm-bindings, and the like, which belong to the special topics of the Chapter on Concentration and of the mind-dharmas, the paper marks them as-is without unfolding. The reader should not take the paper as having treated the full substance of cessation-attainment — it establishes only its "place in the genealogy of mastery" (the apex of concentrative mastery), the rest awaiting the studies of the Chapter on Concentration.

## VI. Conclusion

**Restatement.** Cessation-attainment is the deepest signature of concentrative mastery, yet still belongs to a face of concentrative mastery, not a higher mastery of exhaustion (holding to P01, P03, P05). Its "freedom within stilling" is possible because cessation-attainment is a "deepest yet still living" stilling (life-warmth-faculties-life unbroken, differing from death), so there is the room of autonomy for entering-emerging-arousing-ceasing (3.1). Its being gained by the noble alone hangs on "the capability to enter without fear" (the power of the noble path, the conviction of non-self), and the MĀ195 threefold plainly proves the bodily realization of cessation-attainment does not exhaust taints (one must see exhaustion by wisdom to become liberated both-ways) — so it belongs to the face of concentration, the demarcating mark of liberation both-ways (3.2). Judged by this axis's frame: cessation-attainment is the apex of concentrative mastery as "freedom in arousing-and-ceasing," the deepest concentration-mark within the reach of liberation both-ways, yet not an augmentation of the mastery of exhaustion (exhaustion one and the same without higher and lower; cessation-attainment neither increases nor decreases on the taint-axis, 3.3).

**Practice guidance.** What the practitioner should understand about cessation-attainment is not the demand "I must gain cessation-attainment to have the highest realization" (which falsifies the height of mastery by the depth of concentration, falling into what P03 and P05 break), but the recognition that "cessation-attainment is the apex of concentrative mastery — the deepest concentration-signature, yet not a greater depth of exhaustion." The essential of tending toward liberation still lies in severing taints by wisdom (exhaustion), not in gaining the deepest concentration-realm of cessation-attainment. The wisdom-liberated arhat without cessation-attainment is in no way deficient in the mastery of exhaustion, in no way different in no-more-becoming — showing cessation-attainment is not necessary to liberation. Yet cessation-attainment too has its value not to be slighted: it is the resolute conviction of nirvāṇa here-and-now, the peak of concentration-bliss, what makes one liberated both-ways, the summit of concentration-learning, to be realized — as the faculties and the power of concentration allow — to round out the face of concentrative mastery. The essential is not to invert: do not let the apex of the concentration-face (cessation-attainment) usurp the seat of the taint-face mastery (exhaustion); do not, by "not gaining the deepest concentration," doubt that "taints are not exhausted"; nor, by "gaining the deepest concentration," presume that "exhaustion is deeper." Within stilling there is indeed freedom (the autonomy of arousing-and-ceasing), yet what seals this deepest stilling is still exhaustion — only the exhausted (without fear of annihilation through non-self) can enter it without fear.

**Carrying the axis forward.** This paper opens Part 4, treating the aspect within the arhat's mastery of the deepest concentration (cessation-attainment), and establishes it as "the apex of concentrative mastery, not an augmentation of the mastery of exhaustion." With this, Parts 2 (the two liberations of mind and wisdom, the unshakable, the distinction of the two kinds), 3 (the three knowledges and six powers, the power of exhaustion as pivot, powers not mastery), and 4 (cessation-attainment) have each judged, of the arhat's mastery's several "faces" — liberation of mind, liberation by wisdom, concentrative mastery, cognitive freedom — their relation to the pivot of exhaustion, consistently holding to P01's frame: every face turns about the pivot of exhaustion, and where mastery lies is exhaustion alone. This opens onto Part 5, "The Worthy and No-More-Becoming" (P07, P08): P07 will treat positively the doctrine of "no-more-becoming" (the resolution of birth-and-death in the arhat's mastery — the proper discrimination of the unanswered, that it is not annihilation), P08 the "worthy" (the social legibility of the arhat's mastery in the world — the field of merit), closing the division. This paper's establishment that "the apex of the concentration-face still turns about the pivot of exhaustion" continues into P07's "no-more-becoming is also a dimension of exhaustion (the resolution of birth-and-death)" — both showing that every aspect of the arhat's mastery returns, in the end, to the one signature of exhaustion.

**Open questions.** First, this paper judges "within stilling there is yet freedom (the autonomy of arousing-and-ceasing)"; yet the schools' dispute over whether cessation-attainment has mind or no mind (whether the mind-dharmas are wholly severed on entering, and if wholly severed, where the subject of "the autonomy of arousing-and-ceasing" resides), the paper only establishes "there is room for arousing-and-ceasing because life-warmth-faculties-life persist," without entering the having-mind-or-no-mind debate, awaiting the studies of the Chapter on Concentration and the mind-dharmas. Second, the paper cuts §IV (cessation-attainment is only superficially like minimal states of consciousness); whether "perception-and-feeling both ceased yet the life-faculty persisting," distinct in kind from all "states of consciousness / brain" descriptions, admits a positively constructible deep structure, is left to a later paper. Third, the relation of cessation-attainment's "entry by the resolute conviction of nirvāṇa here-and-now" (2.4) to the "no-more-becoming, nirvāṇa-without-remainder" P07 will treat — whether cessation-attainment's "nirvāṇa here-and-now" is a foretaste of nirvāṇa or only a figure — the paper does not finely distinguish, to be viewed together with P07's doctrine of no-more-becoming.
