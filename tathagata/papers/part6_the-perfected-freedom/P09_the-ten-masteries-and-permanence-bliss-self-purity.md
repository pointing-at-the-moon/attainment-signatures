# The Tathāgata's Ten Masteries and Permanence-Bliss-Self-Purity

**Why a Buddha's Ten Masteries Are the Fruit-Level Perfected Mastery (Not the Eighth-Ground Causal Set Brightened), and Permanence-Bliss-Self-Purity Are the Four Virtues of the Great Nirvāṇa (Not the Permanence-Bliss-Self-Purity Ordinary Beings Imagine)**

*中文主題：如來十自在與常樂我淨——佛之十自在何以是果位圓成之自在（非八地因位之調亮），常樂我淨何以是大涅槃之四德（非凡夫所計之常樂我淨）*

## Abstract

This paper opens Part 6 (the ten masteries, the great nirvāṇa, the perfection of non-abiding), gathering up the perfected aspect of the buddha-fruit's mastery. This series takes "the ten masteries" as the essence-frame of this division (the Tathāgata's ten masteries, perfected); this paper takes up precisely these, together with the four virtues of the great nirvāṇa — permanence, bliss, self, purity. It asks: are the Tathāgata's ten masteries the same set as the eighth-ground bodhisattva's? Are permanence-bliss-self-purity the same in sense as what ordinary beings imagine? The thesis: **the Tathāgata's ten masteries are the fruit-level perfected mastery, not the eighth-ground causal set brightened — the *Avataṃsaka* itself lists two sets: the immovable-ground bodhisattva's ten masteries (life, mind, wealth, action, birth, vow, understanding, as-wished, wisdom, dharma — the causal level) and the buddhas' ten masterful dharmas ("Detachment from the World" — a different list and order, the fruit level), differing in subject, list, and order; the Tathāgata's take "effortless spontaneity" (manifesting all buddhas in a single thought, yet never using the least skillful effort) as their fruit-level aspect, not the causal set magnified. Permanence-bliss-self-purity are the four virtues of the great nirvāṇa, not what ordinary beings imagine — the *Nirvāṇa* judges the worldly four-inversions' permanence-bliss-self-purity (taking the impermanent as permanent, the selfless as having self) to have words without meaning, and the supramundane great nirvāṇa's to have words with meaning, the four answering on the face of the words to the Tathāgata's dharma-body (permanence) · nirvāṇa (bliss) · the Buddha / tathāgatagarbha-buddha-nature (self) · the buddhas' and bodhisattvas' true dharma (purity).** In the eighty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka* and the *Mahāparinirvāṇa-sūtra*: an immovable-ground bodhisattva accomplishes the ten masteries of life, mind, wealth, action, birth, vow, understanding, as-wished, wisdom, and dharma (*Avataṃsaka*, fascicle 38, the causal level); the buddhas have ten masterful dharmas, mastering all dharmas, unobstructed in eloquence … and at last manifesting all buddhas in a single thought yet never using the least skillful effort (*Avataṃsaka*, fascicle 46, the fruit level); the great nirvāṇa takes permanence-bliss-self-purity as its four virtues, "self is the sense of Buddha, permanence the sense of the dharma-body, bliss the sense of nirvāṇa, purity the sense of dharma," "the selfless is named birth-and-death, the self is named the Tathāgata" (*Nirvāṇa*, fascicle 2); "self is the sense of the tathāgatagarbha; all beings have the buddha-nature, which is the sense of self" (*Nirvāṇa*, fascicle 7). Conclusion: the ten masteries (the perfected fruit-level capacities) and the four virtues (the virtues of the great nirvāṇa) together disclose the perfected aspect of the buddha-fruit's mastery — carrying forward the axis's frame of "the fruit-level is not the causal-level brightened" (P02's two sets of ten masteries here gaining the *Avataṃsaka*'s two lists as full witness), and opening the closing paper P10's ultimate non-abiding nirvāṇa (the four virtues' "permanence" and "bliss" gathered into the not-abiding in nirvāṇa that benefits beings to the end of the future). The *Nirvāṇa*'s "self" virtue — the self / no-self middle way, the true self versus the ordinary being's imagined self, the universality of the buddha-nature, the definitive sense of the marks' binding to the buddha-nature (P07) — are major adjudications of the tathāgatagarbha line, recorded without adjudicating, reserved to the author.

**Keywords**: the Tathāgata's ten masteries, the eighth-ground ten masteries, life-mind-wealth-action-birth-vow-understanding-as-wished-wisdom-dharma, the buddhas' ten masterful dharmas, effortless spontaneity, permanence-bliss-self-purity, the four virtues of the great nirvāṇa, self is the sense of Buddha, self is the sense of the tathāgatagarbha, all beings have the buddha-nature, the worldly four-inversions, words with meaning, the fruit-level is not the causal-level brightened

## I. The Question

The first five Parts set down the buddha-fruit's mastery as name (the ten epithets, P01–02) · body (the three bodies, the marks, P03 · P07) · wisdom (the four wisdoms, P04) · function (the ten powers, four fearlessnesses, eighteen distinctive dharmas, four analytical knowledges, great compassion, all-kinds wisdom, P05–08). This paper opens Part 6, gathering up the perfected aspect of the buddha-fruit's mastery, taking two things: the Tathāgata's ten masteries and the great nirvāṇa's four virtues of permanence-bliss-self-purity.

Their being taken is no accident. First, "the ten masteries" is this division's essence-frame — from the outset of its planning, this series set "the Tathāgata's ten masteries" (perfected) as the heading-locus of the *Mastery of the Tathāgata* division; the prior P02 already set the discrimination of "two sets of ten masteries" (the bodhisattva's eighth-ground ten masteries as causal, path-bound; the Tathāgata's as fruit-level, perfected), and this paper takes up precisely these Tathāgata's ten masteries, supplying their full witness. Second, "permanence-bliss-self-purity" is the great nirvāṇa's four virtues — the "virtue" aspect of the buddha-fruit's mastery, completing, with the ten masteries' "capacity" aspect, a whole.

This paper asks two things. First, are the Tathāgata's ten masteries and the eighth-ground bodhisattva's ten masteries the same set of ten capacities, only brighter at the buddha level? Second, is the great nirvāṇa's permanence-bliss-self-purity the same in sense as the permanence-bliss-self-purity ordinary beings imagine? Both are extensions of this series' frame of "the fruit-level is not the causal-level brightened" — the former asking whether the fruit-level's capacities and the causal level's are one set, the latter whether the supramundane's virtues and the worldly's reckoning are one sense.

This paper holds both to be no "same-set / same-sense brightening." **First, the Tathāgata's ten masteries are the fruit-level perfected mastery, not the eighth-ground causal ten masteries brightened.** The proof is in the *Avataṃsaka* itself: in the "Ten Grounds" chapter's immovable ground it lists a bodhisattva's ten masteries (life, mind, wealth, action, birth, vow, understanding, as-wished, wisdom, dharma), expressly marking their subject as a "bodhisattva" on the "immovable ground" (the causal level); and in the "Detachment from the World" chapter it separately lists "the buddhas, the World-Honored Ones, have ten masterful dharmas" (mastery over all dharmas, unobstructed eloquence … and at last manifesting all buddhas in a single thought yet never using the least skillful effort), expressly marking the subject as "the buddhas, the World-Honored Ones" (the fruit level). The two lists differ in subject (bodhisattva / buddhas), in items (life-mind-wealth-action… / mastery over dharmas, manifesting buddhas in a thought…), and in order — the *Avataṃsaka* itself, by its two lists, judges the causal and fruit-level ten masteries plainly distinct, not one set brightened. And the fruit-level ten masteries' aspect lies in "effortless spontaneity" (manifesting all buddhas in one thought, yet "never using the least skillful effort") — precisely the aspect by which the fruit-level differs from the causal (the eighth ground, though attaining the effortless, still has it within the causal). **Second, permanence-bliss-self-purity are the four virtues of the great nirvāṇa, not what ordinary beings imagine.** The proof is in the *Nirvāṇa* itself: it plainly judges "the worldly too has permanence-bliss-self-purity; the supramundane too has permanence-bliss-self-purity; the worldly has the words without the meaning, the supramundane has the words with the meaning" — the worldly's permanence-bliss-self-purity being the four inversions (taking the impermanent as permanent, the selfless as having self), having the words without the meaning; only the supramundane great nirvāṇa's permanence-bliss-self-purity having words with meaning, the four answering to the Tathāgata's dharma-body (permanence) · nirvāṇa (bliss) · Buddha / tathāgatagarbha-buddha-nature (self) · true dharma (purity). So the great nirvāṇa's four virtues are no magnification of what ordinary beings reckon, but supramundane virtues in the definitive sense.

This carries forward the Attainment axis's constant frame and gathers up the perfected aspect of the buddha-fruit's mastery. This series holds the buddha-fruit's virtues all to be aspects of "turning / severing / manifesting," distinct from the causal level (P02): the ten epithets are not the causal-level name brightened, the four wisdoms not the causal-level wisdom brightened, the ten masteries not the eighth ground brightened, the four virtues not the worldly reckoning. The Tathāgata's ten masteries take "effortless spontaneity" as their fruit-level capacity-aspect, the great nirvāṇa's four virtues take "words with meaning" as their fruit-level virtue-aspect — the two together disclosing the perfection of the buddha-fruit's mastery: capacity (the ten masteries) and virtue (the four virtues) alike fruit-level perfected, not the causal magnified, not the worldly reckoned. And this division's keynote of "unobstructed mastery" appears here too: the first of the fruit-level ten masteries is "mastery over all dharmas · unobstructed eloquence" (the *Avataṃsaka*), and the "permanence" of the four virtues is the permanence of the Tathāgata's dharma-body (the permanent abiding of unobstructed wisdom).

This paper rests on the eighty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka* (the two lists of ten masteries, causal and fruit) and the *Mahāparinirvāṇa-sūtra* (the four virtues, the "self" virtue). The argument runs in three moves (§III): the Tathāgata's ten masteries are the fruit-level perfected, not the eighth-ground causal brightened (the two *Avataṃsaka* lists as witness, 3.1); permanence-bliss-self-purity are the great nirvāṇa's four virtues, not what ordinary beings imagine (the words-with-meaning judgment, 3.2); the ten masteries (capacity) and the four virtues (virtue) together disclose the perfected aspect of the buddha-fruit's mastery, completing the frame and opening P10 (3.3). Section IV cuts the interdisciplinary convergence (rationale in §VI). Section V offers middle-way corrections and marks the *Nirvāṇa*'s "self" virtue's self / no-self middle way, the true self versus the imagined self, the universality of the buddha-nature, the "信自在" gap in the ten masteries, and the item-correspondence of the two lists as reserved to the author. Section VI concludes and hands the ultimate non-abiding nirvāṇa (gathering the four virtues' permanence and bliss, and the terminus of both axes) to P10.

## II. The Canonical Basis

### 2.1 Two lists of ten masteries: the eighth-ground bodhisattva (causal) and the buddhas (fruit)

The *Avataṃsaka*, in the "Ten Grounds" chapter's eighth, immovable ground, plainly lists the ten masteries an immovable-ground bodhisattva accomplishes, marking the subject as a "bodhisattva":

> 佛子！菩薩成就如是身智已，得命自在、心自在、財自在、業自在、生自在、願自在、解自在、如意自在、智自在、法自在。得此十自在故，則為不思議智者、無量智者、廣大智者、無能壞智者。[^1]
>
> *"Buddha-sons! A bodhisattva, having accomplished such a body and wisdom, attains mastery of life, of mind, of wealth, of action, of birth, of vow, of understanding, of the as-wished, of wisdom, and of dharma. Having attained these ten masteries, he becomes one of inconceivable wisdom, of measureless wisdom, of vast wisdom, of indestructible wisdom."*

These ten masteries — life · mind · wealth · action · birth · vow · understanding · as-wished · wisdom · dharma — are the ten an immovable-ground (eighth-ground, causal) bodhisattva accomplishes, the locus where the ten masteries take causal-level form. 〔Note: this list reads "vow · understanding · as-wished," with a "mastery of understanding" but no separate item of the traditionally named "mastery of resolve-and-understanding"; the *Avataṃsaka*'s immovable-ground text is as it is, and this paper records without supplying, see §V, Correction 3.〕The *Avataṃsaka* then, in the "Detachment from the World" chapter, separately lists the "ten masterful dharmas" of "the buddhas, the World-Honored Ones," the subject being "the buddhas, the World-Honored Ones" (the fruit level):

> 佛子！諸佛世尊有十種自在法。何等為十？所謂：一切諸佛於一切法悉得自在，明達種種句身、味身，演說諸法辯才無礙，是為諸佛第一自在法。……一切諸佛於一念頃現一切世界微塵數佛……一念一切現，一念無量住，而未曾用少方便力，是為諸佛第十自在法。[^2]
>
> *"Buddha-sons! The buddhas, the World-Honored Ones, have ten masterful dharmas. What are the ten? Namely: all buddhas attain mastery over all dharmas, comprehending the various phrase-bodies and syllable-bodies, expounding the dharmas with unobstructed eloquence — this is the buddhas' first masterful dharma. … All buddhas, in a single thought, manifest buddhas as numerous as the dust-motes of all worlds … in one thought all are manifested, in one thought measureless abide, yet never using the least skillful effort — this is the buddhas' tenth masterful dharma."*

These ten masterful dharmas take "the buddhas, the World-Honored Ones" as their subject, their list marked by sense (mastery over all dharmas · unobstructed eloquence … and at last manifesting all buddhas in a single thought yet never using the least skillful effort), differing from the immovable-ground bodhisattva's ten masteries (life-mind-wealth-action-birth-vow-understanding-as-wished-wisdom-dharma) in subject, items, and order. Most to be marked: the last of the buddhas' ten masterful dharmas says plainly "in one thought all are manifested, in one thought measureless abide, yet never using the least skillful effort" — "never using the least skillful effort" being the aspect of "effortless spontaneity": the buddhas manifest all buddhas in one thought, without borrowing the slightest contrived effort. This effortless spontaneity is precisely the fruit-level aspect. The *Avataṃsaka* itself, by these two lists (immovable-ground bodhisattva / the buddhas), marks the causal and fruit-level ten masteries plainly distinct, not one set brightened.

### 2.2 The four virtues of the great nirvāṇa: permanence-bliss-self-purity with words and meaning

The *Mahāparinirvāṇa-sūtra*, in the "Lamentation" chapter, sets the great nirvāṇa's "permanence-bliss-self-purity" as its four virtues, and judges the difference of words and meaning between the worldly four-inversions' and the supramundane great nirvāṇa's permanence-bliss-self-purity:

> 我者即是佛義，常者是法身義，樂者是涅槃義，淨者是法義。……無常計常、常計無常，是顛倒法。無我計我、我計無我，是顛倒法。……世間亦有常樂我淨，出世亦有常樂我淨。世間法者有字無義，出世間者有字有義。……無我者名為生死，我者名為如來；無常者聲聞緣覺，常者如來法身；苦者一切外道，樂者即是涅槃；不淨者即有為法，淨者諸佛菩薩所有正法。[^3]
>
> *"Self is the sense of Buddha; permanence the sense of the dharma-body; bliss the sense of nirvāṇa; purity the sense of dharma. … Taking the impermanent as permanent, the permanent as impermanent — this is an inverted dharma. Taking the selfless as having self, the self as selfless — this is an inverted dharma. … The worldly too has permanence-bliss-self-purity; the supramundane too has permanence-bliss-self-purity. The worldly has the words without the meaning; the supramundane has the words with the meaning. … The selfless is named birth-and-death, the self is named the Tathāgata; the impermanent the śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas, the permanent the Tathāgata's dharma-body; suffering all the heterodox, bliss is nirvāṇa; the impure the conditioned dharmas, the pure the true dharma possessed by the buddhas and bodhisattvas."*

The scripture makes plain the four virtues' correspondence: self is the sense of Buddha, permanence the sense of the dharma-body, bliss the sense of nirvāṇa, purity the sense of dharma. It further judges the worldly's permanence-bliss-self-purity to be the four inversions (taking the impermanent as permanent · the selfless as having self), "the words without the meaning"; only the supramundane great nirvāṇa's having "the words with the meaning." The closing passage answers them on the face of the words: self is named the Tathāgata, permanence the Tathāgata's dharma-body, bliss is nirvāṇa, purity the true dharma possessed by the buddhas and bodhisattvas. So the four virtues are no permanence-bliss-self-purity ordinary beings imagine, but supramundane virtues in the definitive sense, answering to the Tathāgata · dharma-body · nirvāṇa · true dharma. The scripture, in the "Tathāgata-Nature" chapter, further glosses the "self" virtue as the sense of the tathāgatagarbha and the buddha-nature:

> 我者即是如來藏義。一切眾生悉有佛性，即是我義。如是我義，從本已來，常為無量煩惱所覆，是故眾生不能得見。……如來今日普示眾生諸覺寶藏，所謂佛性。[^4]
>
> *"Self is the sense of the tathāgatagarbha. All beings have the buddha-nature — this is the sense of self. This sense of self has from the very beginning been ever covered by measureless afflictions, and so beings cannot see it. … The Tathāgata today reveals to all beings the treasure-store of awakening, namely the buddha-nature."*

The scripture glosses the great nirvāṇa's "self" as the sense of the tathāgatagarbha: all beings have the buddha-nature, which is the sense of "self"; this "self" abides from the very beginning, covered by afflictions so that beings do not see it. So the *Nirvāṇa*'s "self" virtue, on the face of the words, points to the tathāgatagarbha and the buddha-nature, not the ordinary being's imagined self of the five aggregates. It must be marked with all seriousness: the adjudications this "self" virtue involves — the precise convergence of the great nirvāṇa's "true self" with the ordinary being's imagined self (the self negated by no-self), the middle way of the "self" and "no-self" teachings, the universality of "all beings have the buddha-nature" (whether the icchantika has the buddha-nature, the buddha-nature in cause and fruit), and the definitive relation of this "self = buddha-nature" to the marks' binding to the buddha-nature recorded in P07 — are all core questions of the tathāgatagarbha / nirvāṇa line, and matters this series expressly reserves to the author; this paper records the scripture (the four virtues' correspondence, self as tathāgatagarbha-buddha-nature) without adjudicating (see §V, Correction 2). This paper takes from it only the one sense: permanence-bliss-self-purity are the four virtues of the great nirvāṇa (words with meaning), not what ordinary beings imagine.

## III. The Core Argument

Section II has set out: the *Avataṃsaka* itself listing two sets of ten masteries (the immovable-ground bodhisattva, causal / the buddhas, fruit; differing in subject, items, order, 2.1); the *Nirvāṇa* judging the four virtues to have words with meaning, answering to the Tathāgata's dharma-body, nirvāṇa, and true dharma, "self" as the tathāgatagarbha and buddha-nature (2.2). This section establishes the thesis: the ten masteries (the perfected fruit-level capacity) and the four virtues (the great nirvāṇa's virtues) together disclose the perfected aspect of the buddha-fruit's mastery. In three layers.

### 3.1 The Tathāgata's ten masteries are the fruit-level perfected mastery, not the eighth-ground causal set brightened

Establish first the causal-fruit difference of the ten masteries. The eighth-ground (immovable-ground) ten masteries and the buddhas' ten masterful dharmas are not one set of ten capacities differing in brightness, but two lists of distinct levels — the proof being the *Avataṃsaka*'s own listing of them in two places, each marking its subject (2.1). First, the subject differs: one is a "bodhisattva" (immovable ground), the other "the buddhas, the World-Honored Ones." Second, the list-items differ: the eighth-ground's are marked by the names "life · mind · wealth · action · birth · vow · understanding · as-wished · wisdom · dharma," the buddhas' by the senses "mastery over all dharmas · unobstructed eloquence … manifesting all buddhas in one thought" — not one list in item-for-item correspondence. Third, the aspect differs: the culminating aspect of the buddhas' ten masterful dharmas is "in one thought all are manifested … yet never using the least skillful effort" — effortless spontaneity. The eighth ground, though named "immovable" and having attained the effortless path, still has its effortlessness within the causal level (the effortlessness of the path); the buddhas' "never using the least skillful effort" is the effortlessness of fruit-level perfection (an effortlessness with nothing further to advance to). So the Tathāgata's ten masteries are not the eighth-ground's brightened, but the fruit-level perfected mastery — precisely the witness, in the *Avataṃsaka*'s two lists, for the prior paper P02's discrimination of "two sets of ten masteries" (the causal path-bound, the fruit-level perfected).

This carries forward the axis's frame of "the fruit-level is not the causal-level brightened." As the ten epithets are not the causal-level name brightened, the four wisdoms not the causal-level wisdom brightened, the Tathāgata's ten masteries are not the eighth-ground's brightened — but the same "mastery" perfected at the fruit level (effortless spontaneity, nothing further to advance to). The eighth ground's ten masteries are still "mastery within the path" (the bodhisattva level, bound toward the fruit); the Tathāgata's are "mastery after arrival" (the buddha level, the fruit accomplished); their difference is not a brightness of degree, but the difference of causal and fruit levels.

### 3.2 Permanence-bliss-self-purity are the four virtues of the great nirvāṇa, not what ordinary beings imagine

Establish next the supramundane sense of the four virtues. The great nirvāṇa's permanence-bliss-self-purity is no magnification of what ordinary beings reckon, but supramundane virtues in the definitive sense — the proof being the *Nirvāṇa*'s judgment "the worldly has the words without the meaning, the supramundane has the words with the meaning" (2.2). The worldly's permanence-bliss-self-purity is the four inversions: taking the impermanent as permanent, the selfless as having self (having the words without the meaning, since reckoned in delusion). The supramundane great nirvāṇa's permanence-bliss-self-purity has words with meaning: permanence is the Tathāgata's dharma-body (not a reckoned permanence, but the dharma-body's true permanence), bliss is nirvāṇa (not worldly pleasure, but nirvāṇa's quiescent bliss), self is the Buddha / Tathāgata (not the five-aggregate imagined self, but the tathāgatagarbha-buddha-nature's true self), purity the buddhas' and bodhisattvas' true dharma (not a conditioned purity, but the true dharma's purity).

The four virtues again gather this division's threads. Permanence as the Tathāgata's dharma-body — this is the "permanence" of the three bodies' permanent abiding set in P03 (the dharma-body markless yet permanently abiding). Self as the Buddha / tathāgatagarbha-buddha-nature — this is the buddha-nature context recorded in P07 ("the marks and the buddha-nature listed together · inconceivable," the marks bound to the buddha-nature's "self" virtue). Bliss as nirvāṇa — this opens P10's ultimate non-abiding nirvāṇa (nirvāṇa's bliss not the quiescence of ash-and-cessation, but the ever-quiescence that benefits beings to the end of the future). Purity as the buddhas' and bodhisattvas' true dharma — this is the purity of the virtue of wisdom, the true dharma. So the four virtues are the "virtue" aspect of the buddha-fruit's mastery, gathering the threads of name, body, wisdom, and function into the great nirvāṇa. Yet the definitive sense this "self" virtue involves (the true self versus the imagined self · the middle way of self and no-self · the universality of the buddha-nature), this paper records without adjudicating, reserving it to the author (§V, Correction 2) — taking only the one sense, "the four virtues have words with meaning, being the great nirvāṇa's supramundane virtues."

### 3.3 The ten masteries (capacity) and the four virtues (virtue) together disclose the perfected aspect of the buddha-fruit's mastery, completing the frame and opening P10

The third layer gathers up in import. The Tathāgata's ten masteries and the great nirvāṇa's four virtues together disclose the perfected aspect of the buddha-fruit's mastery — the ten masteries its "capacity" aspect (the fruit-level perfected capacity of mastery: effortless spontaneity, manifesting buddhas in one thought), the four virtues its "virtue" aspect (the great nirvāṇa's virtues: permanence-bliss-self-purity, words with meaning). Capacity and virtue alike fruit-level perfected: the capacity not the eighth ground's brightened (3.1), the virtue not the worldly's reckoning (3.2), the two together disclosing the perfection by which "the buddha-fruit's mastery differs from the causal level, differs from the worldly."

This completes the axis's frame and opens the closing paper. This series sets the buddha-fruit's mastery as aspects: name (P01–02) · body (P03 · P07) · wisdom (P04) · function (P05–08), all fruit-level aspects, not the causal brightened. This paper supplies the sense of "perfection": the Tathāgata's ten masteries (the perfection of capacity) and the four virtues (the perfection of virtue) together disclose that the buddha-fruit's mastery has reached perfection — a mastery with nothing further to advance to (effortless spontaneity), virtues with words and meaning (the supramundane true permanence-bliss-self-purity). And the four virtues' "permanence" and "bliss" especially open the closing paper P10: "permanence is the Tathāgata's dharma-body," "bliss is nirvāṇa" — the buddha-fruit's permanence and bliss are not the quiescence of an ash-and-cessation nirvāṇa taken up (were a nirvāṇa-that-must-be-entered taken up, one would not be called permanent, see P08 · P10), but the ever-quiescence of "not abiding in nirvāṇa while benefiting beings to the end of the future." This ultimate aspect of "ever-quiescent yet ever-functioning, ever-functioning yet ever-quiescent" is the ultimate non-abiding nirvāṇa, gathered up at P10, the terminus of both axes. And this division's keynote of "unobstructed mastery" shows here too its terminus: the first of the fruit-level ten masteries is "mastery over all dharmas · unobstructed eloquence," the "permanence" of the four virtues the permanent abiding of the unobstructed-wisdom dharma-body — the buddha-fruit's mastery, in its perfection, takes "unobstructedness" as its aspect from beginning to end, its terminus the "unobstructed liberation" (*apratihata-vimokṣa*) of P10.

> ✅ Doctrinally sound: taken together, the three layers support "the Tathāgata's ten masteries and permanence-bliss-self-purity are the perfected aspect of the buddha-fruit's mastery" — the Tathāgata's ten masteries are the fruit-level perfected mastery, not the eighth-ground causal set brightened (the *Avataṃsaka* itself listing two sets: the immovable-ground bodhisattva [causal] / the buddhas [fruit] · differing in subject, items, order · the fruit-level aspect in "never using the least skillful effort," effortless spontaneity; P02's two sets of ten masteries here gaining full witness); permanence-bliss-self-purity are the great nirvāṇa's four virtues, not what ordinary beings imagine (the *Nirvāṇa*'s "worldly, words without meaning; supramundane, words with meaning" · the four answering to the Tathāgata's dharma-body, nirvāṇa, true dharma · self as the tathāgatagarbha-buddha-nature); the ten masteries (capacity) and the four virtues (virtue) together disclose the perfected aspect of the buddha-fruit's mastery (completing the axis's "fruit-level is not the causal-level brightened" frame · the four virtues' permanence and bliss opening P10's non-abiding nirvāṇa · ultimately taking "unobstructedness" as aspect). The *Nirvāṇa*'s "self" virtue's self / no-self middle way, the true self versus the imagined self, the universality of the buddha-nature, and the definitive sense of the marks' binding to the buddha-nature (P07) are core tathāgatagarbha adjudications, recorded without adjudicating, reserved to the author (§V, Correction 2); the "信自在" gap in the ten masteries and the item-correspondence of the two lists are recorded without supplying or adjudicating (§V, Correction 3).

## IV. Interdisciplinary Convergence

(This paper deliberately omits the interdisciplinary-convergence section; the rationale is given in §VI. The "ten masteries" might seem comparable to philosophy's debates over "free will" or "agency," and the "permanence" and "self" of permanence-bliss-self-purity to metaphysics' discussions of "eternity" and "the self." Yet those are dominated by "a finite subject's freedom of will" and "the positing of a substantial self," only superficially like this paper's sense of "the fruit-level ten masteries of effortless spontaneity" and "self as the tathāgatagarbha-buddha-nature, not the five-aggregate imagined self." The "free will" debate turns mostly on whether a finite subject's choices are determined; a Buddha's ten masteries are the spontaneity of "mastery over all dharmas · never using the least skillful effort," not a freedom of choice; "the metaphysical self" mostly posits a substantial permanent self, whereas the *Nirvāṇa*'s "self" expressly judges it to differ in word and meaning from the ordinary being's imagined self (the self negated by no-self) and points to the tathāgatagarbha-buddha-nature. To force the convergence would falsely cast "fruit-level mastery" as "freedom of will" and "the tathāgatagarbha self" as "a substantial self"; and the *Nirvāṇa*'s "self / no-self" middle way is, still more, beyond what the worldly substance / non-substance dichotomy can comprehend — so it is better left out, folded into §V as recorded without adjudicating.)

## V. Middle-Way Corrections

This section clears away one misreading and plainly marks two reservations.

**❌ Misreading 1: "The Tathāgata's ten masteries = the same set as the eighth-ground bodhisattva's (only brighter at the buddha level); permanence-bliss-self-purity = a magnification of what ordinary beings imagine."** This the paper breaks. The Tathāgata's ten masteries are not the eighth ground's brightened: the *Avataṃsaka* itself lists two sets (immovable-ground bodhisattva / the buddhas), differing in subject, items, order, the fruit-level aspect in "never using the least skillful effort," effortless spontaneity (3.1). Permanence-bliss-self-purity are not what ordinary beings imagine: the *Nirvāṇa* judges the worldly four-inversions' permanence-bliss-self-purity to have "words without meaning," the supramundane great nirvāṇa's to have "words with meaning," answering to the Tathāgata's dharma-body, nirvāṇa, true dharma (3.2). To picture a Buddha's ten masteries and four virtues as "the same set brightened / the same sense magnified" is to fall into "the fruit-level is the causal-level brightened," which this series has already broken (P02).

**⚠️ Correction 2 (the *Nirvāṇa*'s "self" virtue · the true self · the universality of the buddha-nature, reserved to the author): the *Mahāparinirvāṇa-sūtra*'s "self is the sense of Buddha," "self is the sense of the tathāgatagarbha · all beings have the buddha-nature, which is the sense of self," is recorded by this paper to establish "the four virtues have words with meaning · self is the tathāgatagarbha-buddha-nature," and puts no verdict on the self / no-self middle way, the difference of the true self from the imagined self, or the universality of the buddha-nature.** The adjudications this "self" virtue involves — the precise convergence of the great nirvāṇa's "true self" with the ordinary being's imagined self (the self negated by the no-self contemplation the śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas cultivate), the middle way of the "self" and "no-self" teachings (the scripture itself sets "the selfless is named birth-and-death, the self is named the Tathāgata" side by side, their harmonization a matter of doctrine), the universality of "all beings have the buddha-nature" (whether the icchantika has the buddha-nature, the buddha-nature in cause and fruit), and the definitive relation of this "self = buddha-nature" to the "marks bound to the buddha-nature · inconceivable" recorded in P07 — are all core questions of the tathāgatagarbha / nirvāṇa line, and matters this series expressly reserves to the author and to cross-axis convergence. This paper records the scripture's own text without adjudicating in the author's stead. It takes only the one sense: permanence-bliss-self-purity are the great nirvāṇa's supramundane definitive four virtues (words with meaning).

**⚠️ Correction 3 (the "mastery of resolve" gap in the ten masteries · the item-correspondence of the two lists, recorded without supplying):** First, the traditionally named eighth-ground ten masteries often read "life · mind · wealth · action · birth · vow · resolve-and-understanding · as-wished · wisdom · dharma" (including a "mastery of resolve-and-understanding," *adhimukti-vaśitā*); yet the *Avataṃsaka*'s immovable-ground text (2.1 · A1) reads "vow · understanding · as-wished," with a "mastery of understanding" but no independent item of "mastery of resolve"; the bodhisattva's ten masteries in the "Detachment from the World" chapter (fascicle 55) likewise have no "mastery of resolve." This scripture shows no complete list including a "mastery of resolve"; should the author's intended ten items come from another translation (such as the sixty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka*, T0278) or another treatise, it must be separately verified against the XML; this paper records the *Avataṃsaka*'s wording, neither supplying nor fabricating. Second, whether the buddhas' ten masterful dharmas (A2 · marked by sense) and the eighth-ground ten masteries (A1 · marked by name) correspond item-for-item, and which are merged or split, is a matter of doctrinal adjudication; this paper marks only the two loci, A1 (cause) and A2 (fruit), without adjudicating their correspondence.

## VI. Conclusion

**Essential restatement.** The Tathāgata's ten masteries and the great nirvāṇa's four virtues of permanence-bliss-self-purity are the perfected aspect of the buddha-fruit's mastery — the ten masteries the perfection of its "capacity" (the fruit-level effortless spontaneity), the four virtues the perfection of its "virtue" (the great nirvāṇa's words with meaning). Two supports: the Tathāgata's ten masteries are the fruit-level perfected mastery, not the eighth-ground causal set brightened — the *Avataṃsaka* itself lists the immovable-ground bodhisattva (causal) and the buddhas (fruit), differing in subject, items, order, the fruit-level aspect in "manifesting all buddhas in one thought yet never using the least skillful effort," effortless spontaneity, P02's two sets of ten masteries here gaining full witness (3.1); permanence-bliss-self-purity are the great nirvāṇa's four virtues, not what ordinary beings imagine — the *Nirvāṇa* judges the worldly four-inversions "words without meaning," the supramundane great nirvāṇa "words with meaning," the four answering to the Tathāgata's dharma-body (permanence) · nirvāṇa (bliss) · the Buddha / tathāgatagarbha-buddha-nature (self) · true dharma (purity), "self is the sense of the tathāgatagarbha · all beings have the buddha-nature" (3.2); the ten masteries (capacity) and the four virtues (virtue) together disclose the perfected aspect of the buddha-fruit's mastery, completing the axis's "the fruit-level is not the causal-level brightened" frame, the four virtues' "permanence" (the Tathāgata's dharma-body) and "bliss" (nirvāṇa) opening P10's ultimate non-abiding nirvāṇa (3.3). Carrying forward the axis's frame and keynote: capacity and virtue alike fruit-level perfected, ultimately taking "unobstructedness" as aspect.

**Guidance for practice.** This paper corrects, for the practitioner, the thought that "the fruit-level is the causal-level brightened, the supramundane the worldly magnified." First, it corrects "taking the eighth-ground ten masteries as a Buddha's ultimate" — supposing that to attain the immovable ground's ten masteries is to be near the buddha-fruit; one should know the Tathāgata's ten masteries are still higher (effortless spontaneity, nothing further to advance to), the eighth ground's mastery still "mastery within the path" (bound toward the fruit), a Buddha's "mastery after arrival" (the fruit accomplished), so the practitioner does not take the causal-level mastery as enough. Second, it corrects "seeking the nirvāṇa's four virtues by magnifying the worldly permanence-bliss-self-purity" — supposing that enlarging worldly bliss and worldly self to the utmost is the nirvāṇa's four virtues; one should know the supramundane four virtues and the worldly four-inversions differ in word and meaning, the nirvāṇa's permanence the dharma-body's true permanence (not a reckoned permanence), the nirvāṇa's self the tathāgatagarbha-buddha-nature (not the five-aggregate imagined self), so the practitioner follows the supramundane definitive sense and does not liken the nirvāṇa's virtues to the worldly reckoning. In sum: the fruit-level's capacity and virtue are alike aspects of "the buddha-cause being practiced to fullness, the fruit-level of itself accomplished," not the causal brightened, not the worldly magnified; let the practitioner only complete the work of the transformation of the basis, and capacity and virtue of themselves reach perfection. And in the *Nirvāṇa*'s "self" virtue the practitioner should especially keep to the middle way — neither grasping the five aggregates as a real self (falling into eternalism), nor, with "no-self," cutting off the true-self sense of the tathāgatagarbha-buddha-nature (falling into annihilationism); its precise middle way this paper reserves to the author, and the practitioner proceeds by the teacher's instruction.

**Open questions.** First, this paper cuts the Section IV interdisciplinary convergence (the ten masteries and "free will," the *Nirvāṇa*'s "self" and the metaphysical "self" are only superficially alike). Second, the *Nirvāṇa*'s "self" virtue — the convergence of the true self with the ordinary being's imagined self, the middle way of the self and no-self teachings, the universality of "all beings have the buddha-nature" (the icchantika and so on), the definitive relation of "self = buddha-nature" to the marks' binding to the buddha-nature (P07) — is reserved to the author and to cross-axis convergence; this paper records without adjudicating. Third, the "mastery of resolve" gap in the eighth-ground ten masteries (the *Avataṃsaka*'s text reading "mastery of understanding") and the item-correspondence and merging-or-splitting of the buddhas' ten masterful dharmas with the eighth-ground ten masteries, this paper records the loci without supplying or adjudicating. Fourth, this paper having set the perfected aspect of the buddha-fruit's mastery (capacity · virtue), one sense awaits gathering: the ultimate aspect to which the four virtues' "permanence" and "bliss" point — "not abiding in nirvāṇa while benefiting beings to the end of the future" — the ultimate non-abiding nirvāṇa, gathered up at the closing paper TATHAGATA-P10, where the terminus of both axes — realization (arrival, the sublime awakening) and attainment (the mastery after arrival, the Tathāgata's mastery) — is gathered.
