## 1.1 Ten Names, One Person

Through the door, the first thing the eye meets is a string of names.

To certify that someone has reached the buddha-fruit, scripture uses not a checklist of "what He can do" but a set of epithets: Thus-Come, Worthy-of-Offerings, Rightly-and-Fully-Awakened, Perfect-in-Knowledge-and-Conduct, Well-Gone, Knower-of-the-World, Unsurpassed-One, Tamer-of-Persons, Teacher-of-Gods-and-Humans, Buddha-World-Honored-One. Ten names. That a Buddha is a Buddha is signed by these ten. Which forces, at the outset, a strange question: why is the highest of arrivals certified by *names*? A person's greatest achievement we usually certify by "which things he brought off"; scripture certifies a Buddha by ten names. Why?

That is what this chapter must answer. In one line: because at the buddha-fruit that freedom has grown full enough to be "named to the full"—name and being, here, have become one thing.

### The Names Said in One Breath

First, see how these ten names make their entrance.

Long ago, the Buddha Śākyamuni was not yet Śākyamuni. He was then a man in training, who met a Buddha awakened long before him—Dīpaṃkara, the Lamp-lighter. The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* preserves His own later recollection: in that age Dīpaṃkara, to His face, gave the prophecy, foretelling His buddhahood-to-come. The words of the prophecy ran thus:

> Then Dīpaṃkara Buddha prophesied that I, in an age to come, after one incalculable eon, would become a Buddha named Śākyamuni—Thus-Come, Worthy-of-Offerings, Rightly-and-Fully-Awakened, Perfect-in-Knowledge-and-Conduct, Well-Gone, Knower-of-the-World, Unsurpassed-One, Tamer-of-Persons, Teacher-of-Gods-and-Humans, Buddha, World-Honored-One.

Mark the shape of the saying. Dīpaṃkara does not tick off, item by item, "he will gain a first ability, a second ability…"; in one breath He strings the ten names together and sets them upon "that person to come." Ten names, as one person's string of titles—like a man's given name and offices read off in one breath, not ten separate things. This is the eye of the chapter: the ten epithets, from the instant they enter, are one person's name, not an inventory of ten virtues.

### One Name, One Face

What, then, does each of these ten names say? The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* glosses them one by one. What matters is to see this: every name states one face of the same single freedom, and not one of them is some other, unrelated feat.

"Thus-Come," the treatise says, is "understanding as the true character of dharmas, speaking as the true character of dharmas," and "as the Buddhas come by the road of safety, so this one comes—and goes no further into further being": coming along the true character of all things, come, yet falling no more back into birth-and-death. This states His freedom of "no longer taking up birth-and-death." "Worthy-of-Offerings" the treatise parses by its sounds: the foe it slays is the foe of the defilements; the defilement-foe slain to the root, He is worthy of the offerings of all gods and humans. This states His freedom of "defilements exhausted." "Rightly-and-Fully-Awakened" is to "rightly and fully know all dharmas"—of all dharmas, knowing none but rightly, none but fully. This states His freedom of "knowing without remainder."

Further on, the name "Perfect-in-Knowledge-and-Conduct" the treatise glosses with the finest discernment, and it is the crux. The "knowledge" is the three clear knowledges—of former lives, of the divine eye, of the exhaustion of the outflows; the "conduct" is the deeds of body and speech; knowledge and conduct both complete, hence "perfect in knowledge and conduct." But here the treatise puts a question to itself: do not the arhat and the pratyekabuddha also gain these three knowledges? Wherein lies the difference from the Buddha? Its answer cuts to the bone:

> They, though they gain the three knowledges, gain a knowledge not full; the Buddha's is full in all—that is the difference.

The arhat too has the three knowledges, but "a knowledge not full"—his knowledge of former lives, say, can reach back some lifetimes, the furthest to eighty thousand eons, and beyond that cannot see; the Buddha's, in one thought, the beginning and end of the three times, leaves nothing unlit. The same "three knowledges," in the one not full, in the other full. Hold on to this word *full*—it is the key to the whole chapter, and below we shall return to it.

A few names further, the treatise gives them too: "Well-Gone" is to go well (into nirvāṇa) and to speak well (to the occasion); "Knower-of-the-World" is to know through the world's arising and ceasing; "Unsurpassed-One" is that "as among all dharmas nirvāṇa is unsurpassed, so among all beings the Buddha is unsurpassed"—among all beings none stands higher; "Tamer-of-Persons" is that He skillfully tames the trainable, "now in soft and gracious words, now in sharp and bitter words, now in mixed words," ever keeping them from losing the path; "Teacher-of-Gods-and-Humans" is that He is the teacher of gods and of humans. Coming to the name "Buddha," the treatise's words are very plain: "beneath the bodhi tree, awake and clear in full clarity—hence named Buddha." And last, "World-Honored-One," that He has virtue, possessed of every virtue.

Walk the ten names through once, and you see it: not one of them but states one face of the same single freedom—that He takes up birth-and-death no more, that His defilements are exhausted, that He knows without remainder, that He is unsurpassed. They are not ten feats to be gathered apart, counted apart; they are the one full freedom, read out from ten angles into ten names.

An image (this is an image, not a line of scripture): a man, before his children, is a father; before his students, a teacher; before the sick, a physician; next door, a neighbor. Father, teacher, physician, neighbor—four names; yet these are not four roles he "additionally takes up" (not that he was first a man and then earned four licenses), but the one man read out into four names across four relations. The ten epithets, of a Buddha, are just so: not "first He grew free, then went and acquired ten names one at a time," but "the freedom being full, read from ten angles, it is of itself these ten names."

### The Name Is a Signature, Not a Checklist

Said this far, the reading this chapter sets out to break shows its face.

To read the ten epithets as "a checklist of ten virtues the Buddha possesses" is to picture buddhahood thus: first the defilements severed, a base score gained; then, beyond that base, the ten epithets, three bodies, four wisdoms—earned item by item, like collecting ten badges. As though freedom were the base score, and the ten epithets ten further marks added on.

This misreads the relation of name and being. The ten epithets are not a thing added beside the freedom; they are that freedom's own readable signature. When a person signs his name, the name is not a gift enclosed after the letter is written; it is the readable mark that "this person is present, and is none but he"—see the signature, and you know the man has come, and is himself. The Tathāgata's ten epithets are exactly such a signature: see these ten names complete, and you know that freedom is full. To take the ten epithets for "ten separately acquired virtues" is to mistake a signature for ten gifts the signer mailed on the side—reckoning a sum that is not there (as if, beyond the freedom, ten more were gained), and positing besides a someone who goes off to "get virtues."

### Full, and Only Then the Names Complete

Why, then, is it precisely a "Buddha" who fully bears these ten names? Here the word *full* from "Perfect-in-Knowledge-and-Conduct" comes home. The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* says it straight:

> Therefore only a Buddha should receive the Buddha's names… A Buddha has two things: first, the power of great merit and spiritual penetration; second, the foremost purified mind, the fetters extinguished… The Buddha being full in both, He is called surpassing all others.

Only a Buddha can fully receive this set of a Buddha's names. Why? Because of "fullness in both"—first, the power of great merit and spiritual penetration; second, the foremost purified mind, the fetters and bonds extinguished to the root; both of these the Buddha fulfills. The treatise even draws the comparison: the gods, though they have the power of merit, have not extinguished the fetters, and their minds are unpurified; the śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas, though their fetters are extinguished and their minds purified, are thin in merit. Each full on one side and short on the other—so none can fully bear a Buddha's names. Only a Buddha is full in both.

See the grammar of it: the names' being complete (fit to receive the ten in full) rests upon the being's being full (fullness in both, the fetters extinguished). Not "the ten names lie ready and the Buddha goes off to acquire them," but "the being being full, the names of themselves are complete." The name is the readable aspect of the being's fullness. So the chapter can set down its line: **naming-full is being-full**. That the ten epithets are exactly "complete"—one short and there is no Buddha—is not a chance gathering of ten; it is that the freedom they sign has grown full to the point of nothing missing, and the names are full as the being is full.

This line the *Mahāparinirvāṇa-sūtra* furnishes with a side-witness. It was when the Buddha was about to enter nirvāṇa—outside the city of Kuśinagara, between the twin sāla trees, on the fifteenth of the second month—that He sent forth a great sound, proclaiming to all beings: let whoever has a doubt now ask; this is the last asking. In that very assembly, Mañjuśrī spoke of a far Buddha in the east, named Tathāgata-Equal-to-Space, saying He was "complete in the ten epithets." Complete in the ten epithets—this certifies a Buddha by whether the ten names are whole: of any Buddha these ten are surely complete, one short, and it is no Buddha. So these ten names are not this one Buddha's private property; they are the standard full name of the fruit "Buddha." In the *Avataṃsaka*, the bodhisattva Samantabhadra says it from another face: that the Tathāgata appears "not by one condition, not by one matter," but is accomplished by measureless dharmas, measureless conditions. The ten names, though listed as but ten, sign a freedom measureless and inexhaustible.

### Onward

Back to the strange question at the start: why is the highest arrival certified by "names"? Now it can be answered. Because at the buddha-fruit the freedom has grown full enough to be "named to the full"—full enough to be read out, whole, by a fixed set of names. The Realization axis, writing up to sublime-awakening, reads "arrived"; this volume, standing at the same sublime-awakening, reads "full"—and the place where the fullness reaches even to complete names is called the ground where naming is full.

For one who walks the road, this first removes a tendency. One heading for the buddha-fruit most easily pictures buddhahood as a "collecting of virtues": after severance, still short the ten epithets, short the three bodies, short the four wisdoms—and practice becomes an endless "one more to gain," with fullness forever "a few short." "Naming-full is being-full" is the medicine: the ten epithets are not virtues to be gathered item by item beyond severance, but names read off of themselves once the freedom is full—not "gain the ten names and then be full," but "be full, and the names are complete." As, once a man's character is formed, the names people call him by accrue to him of themselves, without his needing, beyond his character, to go seek a fine-sounding name. To seek names as one seeks objects becomes a fresh bondage; only the freedom of a cause-stage walked exhausted is the whole name that cannot be taken away.

Only—to say it back—a buried doubt here cannot be left unasked. At the eighth ground the bodhisattva too, scripture plainly says, "attains ten masteries"—the same ten, each likewise called a mastery. Is the Tathāgata's ten masteries, then, merely the bodhisattva's, turned up a notch, magnified a round? If so, the line "only a Buddha fully bears the ten epithets" cannot stand.

The next chapter steps over that threshold.
