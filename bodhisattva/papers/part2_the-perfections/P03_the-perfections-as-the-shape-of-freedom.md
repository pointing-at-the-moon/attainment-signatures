# The Six Perfections as the Shape of Freedom

**Prajñā as Guide Makes the Five Cross Over · The Six Are the Shape of the Two Provisions and the Shape of Benefiting Self-and-Other**

*中文主題：六度為自在之形——般若為導，五度乃成到彼岸之自在*

## Abstract

The previous two papers established the ground and the measure of the bodhisattva's mastery: BODHI-P01, that freedom begins from the vow and does not succeed the arhat-fruit; BODHI-P02, that it is mastery measured by accumulation (the two boundless provisions). But "the two provisions" is the *abstract measure* of what is accumulated, not its *operational shape* — in what shape does the bodhisattva's mastery actually appear in practice? This paper argues: the six pāramitās (giving, morality, patience, vigour, meditation, prajñā) are the *shape* of the bodhisattva's mastery — what the prior papers established as "mastery measured by the two boundless provisions" appears, operationally, as the shape of the six, not as six virtues set up apart from the provisions. Three layers. First, the six are the shape of the two provisions — the *Yogācārabhūmi* sorts the six into a merit-portion and a wisdom-portion (giving, morality, patience as merit; wisdom as the wisdom-portion; vigour and meditation common to both), the operational unfolding of P02's "provision of merit + provision of wisdom": the two provisions are *what* is accumulated, the six *in what shape*. Second, prajñā as guide makes the five "cross over" — the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*'s simile of the blind and the guide: the five, apart from prajñā, are "like the blind without a guide, unable to reach the path," but with prajñā guiding they are "endowed with eyes" and win the name "pāramitā (crossing to the other shore)"; what turns giving and the rest from bounded worldly goods into the freedom of crossing-over is prajñā's guidance (the wisdom of unobtainable emptiness on which P01's "not-taking" rests). Third, the six are the shape of benefiting self-and-other — the *Yogācārabhūmi* shows by many gates that all the bodhisattva's practices embrace self-benefit and other-benefit, and the *Mahāyāna-saṃgraha* assigns each pāramitā a benefiting of beings (giving = benefiting; morality = not harming; patience = bearing abuse; vigour = growing good and ending evil), taming beings and entering the consciousness-only fruit; so the shape of the bodhisattva's mastery is from the first both self-perfecting and other-benefiting, freedom legible as benefit-to-others (opening P05, the four embraces). To be marked honestly: a single connected passage assigning each of the six to self-and-other benefit is not independently found in the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s Bodhisattva-ground; this paper compensates with the general self-and-other gate + the guide-simile + the Saṃgraha's benefiting of beings, fabricating no per-pāramitā line. Conclusion: the six are the shape of the bodhisattva's mastery — of the two provisions, made into crossing-over by prajñā, the shape of benefiting self-and-other; freedom is not apart from the six perfections, freedom *appears as* them.

**Keywords**: six pāramitās, giving-morality-patience-vigour-meditation-prajñā, shape of freedom, two provisions, merit-and-wisdom portions, prajñā as guide, the blind and the guide, crossing to the other shore, self-and-other benefit, benefiting beings, entering the consciousness-only fruit, four embraces

**關鍵詞**：六波羅蜜、自在之形、二資糧、福智二分、般若為導、到彼岸、自利利他、利益眾生、四攝

## I. The Live Question

The previous two papers established two root matters of the bodhisattva's mastery: its ground (freedom begins from the vow, P01) and its measure (mastery by accumulation, the two boundless provisions, P02). But look closely at "the two provisions": it states *what* is accumulated (the provisions of merit and wisdom) and *how much* (the boundless limit), but not yet *in what shape* it is accumulated. The two provisions are an abstract measure — they tell you the bodhisattva's mastery is measured by the boundless accumulation of merit and wisdom, but not yet: in practice, in the operational texture of daily life, what does this accumulation *look like*? If someone asks, "I grasp that the bodhisattva's mastery is 'the two boundless provisions' — but if I am to practise, where do I lay hold, what is it I do?" — the prior papers still owe him an answer.

This paper is that answer: the shape of the bodhisattva's mastery is the six pāramitās. Giving, morality, patience, vigour, meditation, prajñā — these six are not six matters of good set up *apart from* "the two boundless provisions," but the very shape that "the two boundless provisions" takes when it descends to the operational level. The two provisions are the bone (the quality and quantity of what is accumulated); the six are the figure that bone presents (the shape of the accumulating). Ask "in what shape does the bodhisattva's mastery appear," and the answer is: it appears as the six perfections.

Yet here a misreading easily arises, to be flagged at the outset: taking the six as "six unrelated good deeds" — giving one virtue, morality another, patience yet another, each on its own, no different from the generosity, rule-keeping, and forbearance the world already praises. On that view the six are merely "a checklist of virtues," not "a shape of freedom." This paper argues the contrary: what makes the six "pāramitās" (crossings-over) rather than merely worldly goods, what makes them cohere into "one shape of freedom" rather than "six scattered virtues," rests on two things — first, prajñā as their guide (apart from prajñā the five are like the blind without a guide, only bounded worldly goods; with prajñā guiding they become the freedom of crossing over); second, benefiting self-and-other as their shape (the six are from the first both self-perfecting and other-benefiting, not a private virtue of cultivating oneself). With these two, the six are "the shape of the bodhisattva's mastery."

The thesis: **the six pāramitās are the shape of the bodhisattva's mastery** — the shape of the two provisions (P02), the shape made into crossing-over by prajñā's guidance, the shape of benefiting self-and-other. The argument: §II presents the canon (the six listed, the merit-wisdom division; the blind-and-guide simile; the six benefiting beings); §III argues in three layers (the six as the shape of the two provisions, 3.1; prajñā as guide making the five cross over, 3.2; the six as the shape of self-and-other benefit, 3.3); §IV is cut; §V corrects misreadings and marks one point of textual scope; §VI concludes, takes up P02, and opens P04 and P05.

## II. Canonical Basis

This section presents three clusters: the six pāramitās listed and the merit-wisdom division (2.1); the blind-and-guide simile of prajñā as guide (2.2); the six pāramitās' self-and-other benefit and their benefiting of beings (2.3). The sources are the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* (T1509), the *Yogācārabhūmi* (T1579), and the *Mahāyāna-saṃgraha* (T1593), all CBETA-verified. The four added perfections (skill, vow, power, knowledge) and their mapping to the grounds are routed to the next paper, BODHI-P04, and not transcribed here.

### 2.1 The Six Pāramitās Listed, and the Merit-Wisdom Division

The settled list of the six, the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* gives by their Sanskrit names:

> The six pāramitās complete. Which six? The dāna-pāramitā, śīla-pāramitā, kṣānti-pāramitā, vīrya-pāramitā, dhyāna-pāramitā, prajñā-pāramitā.[^1]

Dāna (giving), śīla (morality), kṣānti (patience), vīrya (vigour), dhyāna (meditation), prajñā (wisdom) — this is the skeleton of the six. And the *structure* of the six the *Yogācārabhūmi* opens into a merit-portion and a wisdom-portion:

> In brief, merit is three pāramitās: first, the giving-pāramitā; second, the morality-pāramitā; third, the patience-pāramitā. Wisdom is one pāramitā: the wisdom-pāramitā. The vigour- and meditation-pāramitās, know, are common to both portions: first, the merit-portion; second, the wisdom-portion.[^2]

Giving, morality, patience are the merit-portion; wisdom the wisdom-portion; vigour and meditation common to both. This "merit-wisdom division" is the pivot of §3.1: it joins the six directly to the prior paper's "provision of merit + provision of wisdom" (P02) — the six are not a thing apart from the two provisions, the six *are* the two provisions descended to practice as a shape (giving-morality-patience the shape of accumulating the provision of merit; wisdom the shape of accumulating the provision of wisdom; vigour and meditation the shape of accumulating both).

### 2.2 Prajñā as Guide: The Five, Apart from Prajñā, Are Like the Blind Without a Guide; With Prajñā They Cross Over

Why are the six "the shape of freedom" rather than "a checklist of worldly goods"? The key is "prajñā as guide." The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* gives the simile of the blind and the guide:

> So too the five pāramitās: apart from the prajñā-pāramitā, they are like the blind without a guide, unable to reach the path, unable to attain omniscience. Kauśika! If the five pāramitās have the prajñā-pāramitā as their guide, then the five are said to be endowed with eyes; guided by the prajñā-pāramitā, they win the name "pāramitā."[^3]

This simile is the eye of the paper. Giving, morality, patience, vigour, meditation — the five, apart from prajñā, are "like the blind without a guide, unable to reach the path, unable to attain omniscience": that is, still bounded worldly goods (doing good yet clinging to it, seeking reward and falling into the human-and-heavenly fruits, never crossing over). Only with the prajñā-pāramitā as guide are the five "endowed with eyes," do they "win the name pāramitā" — "pāramitā" being "crossing to the other shore"; guided by prajñā, the five turn from "goods of this shore" into "the freedom of crossing over."

So the "freedom" in "the shape of freedom" is conferred by prajñā. The prior paper, P01, established that the bodhisattva's mastery is signed by "not-taking," resting on "the wisdom of unobtainable emptiness" (the Śāstra's "joined with the wisdom of unobtainable emptiness, he does not fall to the śrāvaka ground"); here, prajñā's guidance is that same wisdom at work within the six, making giving the "giving empty in its three wheels" (giver, receiver, and gift all unobtainable, yet giving) and morality "morality unattached to the mark of morality," and so on for the five, each guided by prajñā into "non-abiding," into "crossing over." Without prajñā's guidance, giving is merely giving (a bounded merit, a good of this shore); with prajñā's guidance, giving becomes "the giving-pāramitā" (a shape of the freedom that crosses over). So the six's being the shape of freedom lies not in the deed-aspect of the six (in deed-aspect they are no different from worldly goods) but in prajñā's guiding them into crossing over — guided, they are "endowed with eyes"; apart from it, "blind."

### 2.3 The Six as the Shape of Self-and-Other Benefit: Benefiting Beings, Entering the Consciousness-Only Fruit

The six's being "the shape of freedom" has a further face: from the first they are the shape of "self-and-other benefit," not a private virtue of cultivating oneself. The *Yogācārabhūmi*'s Bodhisattva-ground shows by many gates that all the bodhisattva's practices embrace self-benefit and other-benefit:

> (The gates of self-benefit and other-benefit) … fourth, self-and-other benefit of the kind that is ease; fifth, self-and-other benefit embraced as cause; sixth, embraced as fruit; seventh, in this world … if the bodhisattva's self-and-other benefit, there is … the mark of embracing.[^4]

All the bodhisattva's practices (the six included) embrace both self-benefit and other-benefit — the structural witness for "self-and-other benefit." And the per-pāramitā mark of benefiting others, the *Mahāyāna-saṃgraha* assigns in its chapter on entering the surpassing character of cause and fruit:

> By the giving-pāramitā, benefiting beings; by the morality-pāramitā, not harming beings; by the patience-pāramitā, bearing their abuse and not raising a vengeful mind; by the vigour-pāramitā, growing their roots of good and ending their roots of evil; by this cause of benefit, all beings are tamed.[^5]

Giving = benefiting beings; morality = not harming; patience = bearing abuse without vengeance; vigour = growing good and ending evil — each pāramitā is a shape "toward beings," its fruit "all beings are tamed," and the bodhisattva, by these six, enters the fruit of consciousness-only (his own realization). So the shape of the six is from the first not a private practice of "I cultivate six virtues myself" but a doubly-embracing shape of "self-perfecting and other-benefiting": the bodhisattva's mastery is mastery precisely as it is legible in how it benefits and tames beings — freedom appears as benefit-to-others. (This "freedom as benefit-to-others" the next-but-one paper, P05, treats in full, as the four embraces.)

> Textual scope (see also §V): a single connected passage assigning "each of the six to self-and-other benefit" (a connected list "giving = self-and-other benefit; morality = self-and-other benefit; …") is not independently found in the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s Bodhisattva-ground; the Bodhisattva-ground has a general statement of "self-and-other benefit" by many gates (the [^4] of 2.3, fascicle 35), the per-pāramitā self-and-other senses scattered through the same ground's pāramitā sections (fascicles 35–39). This paper compensates "the six as the shape of self-and-other benefit" with the general gate (the *Yogācārabhūmi* of 2.3) + the guide-simile (the Śāstra of 2.2) + the per-pāramitā benefiting of beings (the Saṃgraha of 2.3), three witnesses, fabricating no per-pāramitā connected line.

## III. Core Argument

§II has shown the canon's express words: the six listed and the merit-wisdom division, prajñā guiding the five, the six's self-and-other benefit. This section argues in three layers: why the six are the shape of the two provisions (3.1), why prajñā as guide makes the five cross over into freedom (3.2), why the six are the shape of self-and-other benefit (3.3).

### 3.1 The Six as the Shape of the Two Provisions: The Merit-Wisdom Division Appears Operationally as the Six

The prior paper P02 established that the bodhisattva measures by accumulation, what is accumulated being the two provisions, of merit and wisdom. But the two provisions are the quality-and-quantity of "what is accumulated," not yet "in what shape it is accumulated." This layer argues: the six are the operational shape of the two provisions. The ground is the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s merit-wisdom division (2.1): giving, morality, patience as the merit-portion; wisdom as the wisdom-portion; vigour and meditation common to both. Read this division together with P02 and the two join tightly — P02's "provision of merit" appears operationally as giving, morality, patience (the merit-portion); P02's "provision of wisdom" appears operationally as wisdom (the wisdom-portion); vigour and meditation accumulate both. So the six are not a thing apart from the two provisions; the six *are* the two provisions descended to practice as a shape.

This joining matters, for it clarifies the relation of "shape" to "substance." Ask "by what is the bodhisattva's mastery measured?" — answer: by the two boundless provisions (P02, substance and amount). Ask "in what shape are the two provisions accumulated?" — answer: in the six (this paper, operational shape). The two are one thing's two faces: apart from the six, the two provisions have nowhere to be accumulated (no shape to accumulate in); apart from the two provisions, the six have nothing to accomplish (no substance to complete). So the shape of the bodhisattva's mastery is the six, the substance of it the two provisions — "mastery measured by the two boundless provisions" (P02) and "the shape of mastery is the six" (this paper) are the substance-statement and the shape-statement of one thing.

A comparison: nutrients and dishes. To be strong a person must take in two classes of nutrient, "protein and vitamins" (substance); but "protein and vitamins" is the abstract language of nutrition, and on the table it appears as concrete dishes (shape). You do not eat a serving of "protein" apart from fish and meat — protein appears as the shape of fish and meat, vitamins as the shape of vegetables and fruit. The two provisions are to the six as nutrients are to dishes: the two provisions are the "nutrients" the bodhisattva's mastery requires (the two classes, merit and wisdom), the six the "dishes" those nutrients appear as (six concrete practices). To ask "where, apart from the six, is the accumulation of the two provisions to be found" is like asking "where, apart from the dishes, are the nutrients taken in" — nowhere apart, for the accumulating is in the shape of the six.

> ✅ Doctrinally sound: the *Yogācārabhūmi* sorts the six into a merit-portion and a wisdom-portion (giving-morality-patience merit; wisdom the wisdom-portion; vigour-meditation both, 2.1), joining tightly to P02's "provision of merit + provision of wisdom." The six are the operational shape of the two provisions: the provisions are "what is accumulated" (substance and amount), the six "in what shape it is accumulated" (the shape of practice). "Mastery measured by the two boundless provisions" (P02) and "the shape of mastery is the six" (this paper) are one thing's substance- and shape-statements.

### 3.2 Prajñā as Guide: The Five Cross Over into the Freedom of the Other Shore

The deed-aspect of the six (giving, morality, and so on) is, taken as deed, no different from worldly goods — worldly people too give, too keep rules. What, then, makes the six "pāramitās" (the freedom of crossing over) rather than merely "goods of this shore"? This layer argues: prajñā as guide. The ground is the blind-and-guide simile (2.2): the five, apart from prajñā, are "like the blind without a guide, unable to reach the path, unable to attain omniscience"; with prajñā guiding, the five are "endowed with eyes" and "win the name pāramitā."

The key is the turn in "the name." The same deed of giving, apart from prajñā, bears only the name "giving" (a good of this shore, bounded, attached to the mark, seeking reward, ending in the human-and-heavenly fruits); with prajñā's guidance, it bears the name "giving-pāramitā" (the freedom of the other shore, unattached to the three wheels, not abiding in the reward, tending toward omniscience). What turns is not the deed-aspect of giving, but "the presence or absence of prajñā as guide." Prajñā's guidance is, concretely, the making of giving into "giving empty in its three wheels" — giver, receiver, and gift all seen by "the wisdom of unobtainable emptiness" as unobtainable, so that one gives without abiding in giving. This "wisdom of unobtainable emptiness" is just what the prior paper P01 established the "not-taking" to rest on (the Śāstra's "joined with the wisdom of unobtainable emptiness, he does not fall to the śrāvaka ground"); here it works within the six, making each "non-abiding," each "cross over."

So the "freedom" in "the shape of freedom" is conferred by prajñā. The six's being the shape of freedom lies not in the deed-aspect of the six (in deed-aspect, no different from worldly goods) but in prajñā's guiding them — guided, the five are "endowed with eyes" and become freedom (crossing over); apart from it, the five are "blind" and become only worldly goods (unable to reach the path). This also explains prajñā's singular place among the six: it is at once one of the six (the wisdom-pāramitā, alongside the other five) and the guide of the other five (what makes the other five pāramitās). Prajñā bears a double office — listed as one perfection (the wisdom-portion) and guiding the five (making the merit-portion perfections cross over) — not a self-contradiction, but prajñā's place (as a perfection in its own position) and its function (guiding within the perfections) coexisting.

A comparison: the map and the journey. However abundant a person's stamina, however ample his provisions of water, however strong his will (like the fullness of the five — giving, morality, vigour, and so on), if he has no correct map to guide (like prajñā), he is "like the blind without a guide" — all that stamina and water only circle in place or stray, "unable to reach the path" (never arriving). Once there is a map to guide, the same stamina and water are "endowed with eyes" — the same provisions, turned from "the waste of blind wandering" into "the means of reaching the path." Prajñā is to the five as the map is to a journey's provisions: it adds not a drop of water, yet it makes all the water "able to reach the other shore." Apart from the map, the provisions are blind waste; with it, the means of crossing over. The six's being the shape of freedom rests on this one guide, "prajñā."

> ✅ Doctrinally sound: prajñā as guide turns the five from "goods of this shore" into "the freedom of the other shore" (the blind-and-guide simile, 2.2). What turns is not the deed-aspect of the six but "the presence or absence of prajñā's guidance": guided, the five are "endowed with eyes" and become pāramitās; apart from it, "blind" and only worldly goods. The "non-abiding / unobtainable emptiness" prajñā confers (the same wisdom of unobtainable emptiness on which P01's "not-taking" rests) makes each cross over. Prajñā bears a double office — listed as one perfection (the wisdom-portion) and guiding the five (making them pāramitās) — not a contradiction, but the difference of its place and its function.

### 3.3 The Six as the Shape of Self-and-Other Benefit: Freedom Legible as Benefit-to-Others

The last layer argues: the six are the shape of "self-and-other benefit," so the bodhisattva's mastery is from the first not a private virtue of cultivating oneself but legible as benefit-to-others. The ground is the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s general statement of self-and-other benefit by many gates (2.3) and the *Mahāyāna-saṃgraha*'s per-pāramitā benefiting of beings (2.3): giving = benefiting beings; morality = not harming; patience = bearing abuse without vengeance; vigour = growing good and ending evil, the fruit being "all beings are tamed."

Why must the shape of the bodhisattva's mastery be both self-and-other? Because the bearing and signature of the bodhisattva's mastery, established in the prior papers, are themselves toward-others. P01 established that the bodhisattva's mastery is signed by "not-taking" — not taking the realization that was his, in order that "through immeasurable asaṃkhyeya lifetimes his mind not sink in weariness" while delivering beings; P02 established that its measure is the boundless accumulation "uncircumscribed to a fixed number of beings." Since the bearing of the mastery is the deliverance of beings and its measure is accumulation uncircumscribed for beings' sake, then this mastery, descended to its operational shape (the six), must necessarily be a shape "toward beings" — giving toward beings benefiting them, morality toward beings not harming them, patience toward beings not avenging. So the six's self-and-other benefit is not an added layer of "altruistic motive" on top of the six, but the shape of the bodhisattva's mastery itself oriented toward beings: the mastery is mastery precisely as it is legible in how it benefits and tames beings. A "mastery" that benefits only oneself and never reaches others is, by the definition of the bodhisattva's mastery (toward-others, uncircumscribed, not-taking), simply not the shape of the bodhisattva's mastery at all.

One distinction must be drawn against misunderstanding: "self-and-other benefit" is not two stages of "self-benefit first, then other-benefit," nor an opposition of "sacrificing self-benefit to accomplish other-benefit," but "the double embrace of one act" — one and the same giving both accomplishes one's own provision of merit (self-benefit) and benefits beings (other-benefit), one act doubly accomplishing. The *Yogācārabhūmi*'s showing by many gates that the bodhisattva's practices "all embrace self-benefit and other-benefit" (2.3) marks just this "double embrace," not an "either-or." So the other-benefit of the six (the shape of the bodhisattva's mastery) does not diminish its self-perfecting; and its self-perfecting is not apart from other-benefit — self-perfecting is accomplished *within* other-benefit (the two provisions of merit and wisdom are accumulated precisely in benefiting and taming beings). This "freedom is benefit-to-others, and benefit-to-others is self-perfecting," the double embrace, opens P05's treatment of the four embraces (the four embraces are a more focused unfolding of the six's other-benefit-shape: giving, kind speech, beneficial conduct, shared work, set up purely for embracing beings).

A comparison: a good physician's art. A good physician's "mastery of the medical art" (a practised, unobstructed art) could never be "a private skill practised only on himself, never reaching patients" — the medical art is by its very meaning oriented toward patients (curing illness, saving lives). The practisedness of his art (self-perfecting) is legible precisely in, and accomplished precisely through, how he heals the sick (other-benefit): the more sick he heals, the more practised his art (self-perfecting within other-benefit). You would not say "his medical art is very masterful, he just never treats anyone" — that is simply not the mastery of a medical art. So with the shape of the bodhisattva's mastery (the six): its mastery is legible as benefit-to-others (benefiting and taming beings), its self-perfecting (the two provisions) accumulated precisely within other-benefit; apart from other-benefit, there is no shape of the bodhisattva's mastery to speak of.

> ✅ Doctrinally sound: the six are the shape of self-and-other benefit (the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s self-and-other gates; the Saṃgraha's six benefiting beings, 2.3). The bearing (deliverance of beings, P01) and measure (uncircumscribed for beings, P02) of the bodhisattva's mastery being toward-others, its shape (the six) must embrace other-benefit. Self-and-other benefit is "the double embrace of one act" (self-perfecting accomplished within other-benefit), not "self first then other" or "self sacrificed for other." So the bodhisattva's mastery is legible as benefit-to-others; apart from other-benefit there is no shape of the bodhisattva's mastery (opening P05, the four embraces).

Three layers together: the six are the shape of the two provisions (the merit-wisdom division joining P02, 3.1); prajñā as guide makes the five cross over into freedom (the blind-and-guide simile, 3.2); the six are the shape of self-and-other benefit (freedom legible as benefit-to-others, 3.3). Together they establish the thesis: **the six pāramitās are the shape of the bodhisattva's mastery — the shape of the two provisions, made into crossing-over by prajñā's guidance, the shape of benefiting self-and-other; freedom is not apart from the six perfections, freedom appears as them.**

## IV. Interdisciplinary Convergence

(This paper deliberately omits the interdisciplinary section; the reason is given in §VI. Its axis is the Mahāyāna practice-doctrine of "the six as the shape of the bodhisattva's mastery." Modern models — "virtue ethics," "skill mastery from clumsy to fluent" — superficially resemble the six in "the cultivation of virtues" and "from raw to practised," but cannot sustain a three-layer convergence without lapsing into analogy on three points: "prajñā as guide turning good into crossing-over (non-abiding, unobtainable emptiness)," "the six as the shape of the two boundless provisions," and "the double embrace of self-and-other benefit." On the first especially, what makes giving a "pāramitā" is the direct insight of "three-wheel emptiness, the wisdom of unobtainable emptiness," with no counterpart in any model reducible to the cultivation of worldly virtue-items; virtue ethics' "generosity" and "temperance" are still bounded goods of this shore (the simile's "apart from prajñā, blind"), only superficially resembling the freedom that "crosses over" under prajñā's guidance. Hence omitted per the series' editorial convention, with this note.)

## V. Madhyamaka Corrections

This section clears three misreadings adjacent to the thesis, the first bearing on textual scope and requiring special honesty.

**❌ Misreading one: "the six = six unrelated good deeds (a checklist of virtues)."** This the paper breaks in full. If the six were only six scattered virtues (giving one good, morality another, each on its own, no different from worldly goods), they would be merely "a checklist of virtues," not "a shape of freedom." This paper argues the contrary: the six are threaded into one by prajñā's single guidance (prajñā making the five all "endowed with eyes," all "cross over," 2.2, 3.2), joined to the one accumulation of the two provisions by the merit-wisdom division (3.1), and oriented one way by self-and-other benefit (benefiting and taming beings, 3.3). So the six are not six scattered virtues but the six-faced shape of "one bodhisattva's mastery" — their unity resting on prajñā's guidance and the substance of the two provisions. To read the six as a checklist of scattered virtues loses their "pāramitā (crossing-over)" freedom-sense and their "one shape" coherence, sinking them into a list of worldly goods.

**⚠️ Correction two: a single connected passage assigning "each of the six to self-and-other benefit" is not independently found in the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s Bodhisattva-ground; this paper compensates with three witnesses, fabricating nothing.** To be marked honestly: §3.3 establishes "the six as the shape of self-and-other benefit," yet a single set passage assigning "giving = self-and-other benefit; morality = self-and-other benefit; …" in a connected list of all six is not independently found in the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s Bodhisattva-ground. The Bodhisattva-ground has a general statement of "self-and-other benefit" by many gates (the [^4] of 2.3, fascicle 35), the per-pāramitā self-and-other senses scattered through the same ground's pāramitā sections (fascicles 35–39). "The six as the shape of self-and-other benefit" is established here by the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s general self-and-other gate + the Śāstra's blind-and-guide simile + the Saṃgraha's six benefiting beings (giving benefiting, morality not harming, patience bearing, vigour growing-good-ending-evil), three witnesses, not a connected original passage of "each of the six to self-and-other benefit." This scope is marked per the recorded source-gap, not passing the compensation off as a single set passage.

**⚠️ Correction three: the "merit-wisdom division" is the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s sorting, other treatises may sort otherwise; and prajñā's "double office" is no self-contradiction.** First, the sorting used in §3.1 (giving-morality-patience merit; wisdom the wisdom-portion; vigour-meditation both) is the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s Bodhisattva-ground (the [^2] of 2.1); other treatises may sort the six against merit and wisdom otherwise (some taking meditation too into the wisdom-portion), and this paper argues on the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s standard sorting without claiming it the only one. Second, prajñā's "double office" within the six (listed as the sixth perfection, yet guide of the other five) is no self-contradiction: "listed as one perfection" is prajñā's place as the wisdom-portion in its own position, "guiding the five" is prajñā's function at work within the perfections; place and function coexisting is precisely prajñā's singularity in the structure of the six, not a logical impediment.

## VI. Conclusion

**Restatement.** The six pāramitās are the shape of the bodhisattva's mastery. In the canon, the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* lists the six (giving, morality, patience, vigour, meditation, prajñā) and gives the blind-and-guide simile for "prajñā guiding the five into crossing over (pāramitā)"; the *Yogācārabhūmi* sorts the six into merit and wisdom portions (giving-morality-patience merit, wisdom the wisdom-portion, vigour-meditation both) and shows by many gates that the bodhisattva's practices embrace self-and-other benefit; the *Mahāyāna-saṃgraha* assigns each pāramitā a benefiting of beings (giving benefiting, morality not harming, patience bearing, vigour growing-good-ending-evil), taming beings and entering the consciousness-only fruit. Doctrinally, three layers: the six as the shape of the two provisions (the merit-wisdom division joining P02, 3.1); prajñā as guide making the five cross over (3.2); the six as the shape of self-and-other benefit (freedom legible as benefit-to-others, 3.3). To be marked honestly: a single connected passage assigning each of the six to self-and-other benefit is not independently found, compensated with three witnesses (§V).

**Practice guidance.** Here the practitioner should know: the bodhisattva's mastery is not some mysterious "freedom" to be sought *apart from* the practices of giving, morality, and the rest; the bodhisattva's mastery appears as your giving, your morality, your patience this very moment — the only question is whether your giving has "prajñā as guide." The same giving, if attached to the mark and seeking reward (giver, receiver, gift standing fast, seeking merit and fame), is "blind" and falls to a good of this shore; if guided by the wisdom of unobtainable emptiness (giving empty in its three wheels, giving without abiding), it is "endowed with eyes" and becomes the freedom of the other shore. So the heart of practising the six is not to seek a freedom apart from the deed-aspect of the six, but to guide the six with prajñā — making each act "non-abiding," each toward beings (self-and-other benefit), each accumulating the boundlessness of the two provisions. And remember: the six are one body's six faces (threaded by prajñā), not six scattered virtues to pick among; to cultivate one in isolation, apart from prajñā's guidance and the orientation of other-benefit, makes what one cultivates only a worldly good, not yet the shape of the bodhisattva's mastery.

**Taking up the prior, opening the next.** This paper takes up the prior two: P01 established the ground of the mastery (from the vow), P02 its measure (the two boundless provisions); this paper establishes its *shape* (the six) — ground, measure, and shape together sketch the outline of the bodhisattva's mastery. Along the thread of this Part (Part 2 · The Perfections), the next paper, BODHI-P04, extends "the six" into "the ten": adding skill, vow, power, and knowledge after the six, and showing the ten perfections' correspondence to the ten grounds (one perfection foremost per ground) — this paper establishes the shape of the six, P04 its unfolding into ten and its assignment to the grounds. The more focused unfolding of the six's self-and-other-benefit shape (purely as the embracing of beings) the next-but-one paper, P05, treats as the four embraces. As for the Realization axis: this paper's "the six as the shape of mastery" correlates thematically with the `bodhi-path` division's BODHI-P05, "The Practice Position," and its "six perfections enacted" — the Realization axis reads the six as the conduct walked (the enactment of the ten-practices position), the Attainment axis (this paper) reads the six as the shape of mastery (the operational aspect of the freedom accrued); the seam's doctrine is the author's, and this paper does not speak for the Realization axis.

**Open questions.** First, the paper cuts §IV (the six and virtue ethics / skill mastery are only superficially alike), as above. Second, a single set passage assigning each of the six to self-and-other benefit is not independently found, compensated with three witnesses; to ground the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s per-pāramitā self-and-other connected list, one would further gather the per-pāramitā line-numbers in the Bodhisattva-ground's pāramitā sections (fascicles 35–39). Third, the "merit-wisdom division" follows the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s sorting; other treatises' sortings (e.g. meditation into the wisdom-portion) and their reconciliation are not unfolded here. Fourth, the deep sense of prajñā's guidance ("non-abiding / three-wheel emptiness," how the wisdom of unobtainable emptiness makes each perfection cross over) is here established only as what confers the freedom; its relation to the forbearance of non-arising (the cognitive signature of entering the grounds) belongs to P06, here only opened.
