# Irreversibility and Effortless Flow as the Mastery After the Forbearance

**Letting Go of All Effortful Conduct · Gaining the Effort-Free Dharma, Spontaneously Flowing · The Forbearance of Non-Arising Is Just the Avaivartika Stage · The Activity of Mind, Will, and Consciousness No Longer Manifesting — The Two Marks of Mastery After the Forbearance**

*中文主題：不退轉與任運為忍後之自在——無功用任運與阿鞞跋致不退*

## Abstract

The preceding paper, BODHI-P06, established the forbearance of non-arising as the cognitive signature of the bodhisattva's mastery — its "immovable and irreversible" cognitive turn making the mastery immovable. This paper takes that up and asks: once this cognitive turn is accomplished (after the forbearance), in what marks does the bodhisattva's mastery appear? The thesis: after the forbearance the mastery appears in two marks — effortless flow (effort-free) and irreversibility (avaivartika) — both the forbearance's "immovable" descended into the mark of mastery: effortlessness its positive mark (the mastery flowing of itself without waiting on the contrivance of effort), irreversibility its negative mark (the mastery never regressing, nothing able to break or move it). Three layers. First, effortless flow · effort-free — the *Avataṃsaka* states that abiding in the Immovable ground one "lets go of all effortful conduct, gains the effort-free dharma, the strivings of body, speech, and mind all ceasing, abiding in the conduct of recompense"; the *Daśabhūmika* (Xuanzang) brings out the word "spontaneous flow" — "apart from all effort, spontaneously flowing to gain the effort-free nature, abiding in maturation"; the *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna* (Vasubandhu) judges the eighth ground's foremost excellence over the lower grounds to be "effort-free spontaneity" (the effort-free dharma countering effortful conduct); so after the forbearance the mastery appears as the spontaneous flow of effortlessness (letting go of the deliberate strivings of effort, flowing of itself). Second, irreversibility · avaivartika — the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* judges "the forbearance of non-arising is just the irreversible (avaivartika) stage," its mark being "life after life always gaining the spiritual powers of recompense, neither lost nor regressing," and the *Daśabhūmika* states that on the Immovable ground "all the activity of mind, will, and consciousness no longer manifests … the afflictions never manifest" (the mark of cessation, as one born in the Brahmā world has no manifest desire-realm afflictions); so after the forbearance the mastery appears as the non-regress of avaivartika (the position not regressing, because the wisdom of true reality nothing can break or move). Third, the two marks are the two faces of the mastery after the forbearance, and both are bound to the causal stage (strictly distinct from the tathāgata's fruit-stage) — effortlessness and non-regress are the two senses of "immovable" (the effortless flow of not-acting · the non-regress of not-shaking); yet this eighth-ground effortless flow and non-regress is still a mark of the bodhisattva's causal stage (abiding in "the conduct of recompense / maturation" is the bodhisattva's recompense, its ten masteries the "bodhisattva's ten masteries"), strictly distinct from the tathāgata's fruit-stage (the buddhas still exhort even the eighth-ground bodhisattva "you have not yet attained," P04 2.4). To be marked with care: the three-fold scheme of "non-regress of position · non-regress of conduct · non-regress of mindfulness" is largely later doxography (Tiantai Zhiyi · Yogācāra stage-matching), **not a list directly extractable from this paper's four designated sources (Avataṃsaka / Daśabhūmika / Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna / Śāstra)**; this paper grounds "irreversibility" on the *Śāstra*'s "the forbearance is just avaivartika · neither lost nor regressing" and the *Daśabhūmika*'s "the activity of mind, will, and consciousness no longer manifests," **not passing the later three-fold scheme off as the four sources**; the precise placement of the term "non-regress of mindfulness" is the author's adjudication. And: effortless flow is not idleness or inaction — what is let go is "the strivings of effort (deliberate contrivance)," not "the conduct of delivering beings" itself; the eighth-ground bodhisattva delivers beings precisely by effortless flow (the buddhas' exhortation to rouse him is just for fear he rest in quiescence, P09 / the Realization axis). Conclusion: after the forbearance the mastery appears in the two marks of effortless flow and irreversibility, both the realization of the forbearance's "immovable," and both bound to the causal stage (the three-fold non-regress being later doxography, not passed off here as the four sources).

**Keywords**: the mastery after the forbearance, effortless flow, effort-free, letting go of all effortful conduct, gaining the effort-free dharma, abiding in the conduct of recompense, abiding in maturation, effort-free spontaneity, avaivartika, irreversibility, the spiritual powers of recompense neither lost nor regressing, the activity of mind/will/consciousness no longer manifesting, the Immovable ground, the causal stage, the scope of the three-fold non-regress doxography

## I. The Live Question

The preceding paper, P06, established the forbearance of non-arising as the cognitive signature of the bodhisattva's mastery: when the bodhisattva's mind accomplishes the direct seeing of "contemplating all dharmas as neither arising nor ceasing · entering all dharmas as the nature of empty space," accepting and bearing the "unbreakable, unmovable" reality, its mastery turns from the grasping of applied effort into "the mind penetrating without obstruction · immovable and irreversible." What P06 established is the *cognitive* turn — the irreversible settling of the mind's view of reality. This paper takes that up and asks the natural next step: once this cognitive turn is accomplished ("after the forbearance"), in what *mark* does the bodhisattva's mastery appear? Descended into the mark of mastery, what does the cognitive "immovable and irreversible" (P06) look like?

The paper's answer: after the forbearance the mastery appears in two marks — effortless flow (effort-free) and irreversibility (avaivartika). These two marks are just P06's cognitive "immovable and irreversible," descended into two faces of the mark of mastery. Why two faces? Because the word "immovable" itself holds two senses: one is "not-acting" (running of itself, without waiting on the contrivance of deliberate effort) — this is effortless flow · effort-free, the positive mark of mastery; the other is "not-shaking" (not regressing, nothing able to break or move it) — this is non-regress · avaivartika, the negative mark of mastery. P06 established the cognitive "immovable and irreversible"; this paper unfolds the two faces of that "immovable" in the mark of mastery: positively, the mastery flows of itself without waiting on effort (effortless flow); negatively, the mastery does not regress and nothing can break or move it (avaivartika non-regress).

Here two very common misreadings must be flagged at the outset. The first: reading "effortless flow · effort-free" as "idleness · inaction · doing nothing at all" — as if the eighth-ground bodhisattva, having "let go of all effortful conduct," reclines in non-doing and delivers beings no longer. This is a grave error. Below it will be shown: what is "let go" is "the strivings of effort (deliberate contrivance of mind)," not "the conduct of delivering beings" itself; the eighth-ground bodhisattva delivers beings precisely by "the spontaneous flow of effortlessness" (his delivering of beings flows of itself without waiting on deliberate contrivance) — in the sutra the buddhas must "rouse" the eighth-ground bodhisattva, precisely because the realm of his effortless flow approaches quiescence, and, fearing he rest in that effortless quiet and not rouse himself to continue delivering beings, they exhort him to rouse his original vow (this sense of "rousing" belongs to P09 and the Realization axis), which shows the eighth ground does not cease delivering beings, but delivers them by effortless flow. The second: reading "irreversibility" straight off as the later three-fold "non-regress of position · non-regress of conduct · non-regress of mindfulness" — it must be honestly marked: this three-fold doxography is largely later (Tiantai Zhiyi's *Mohe Zhiguan* · Yogācāra stage-matching), not a settled list directly extractable from this paper's four designated sources; this paper grounds "irreversibility" on the *Śāstra*'s "the forbearance is just avaivartika · neither lost nor regressing" and the *Daśabhūmika*'s "the activity of mind, will, and consciousness no longer manifests," not passing the later three-fold scheme off as the four sources.

The thesis: **after the forbearance the mastery appears in the two marks of effortless flow (effort-free) and irreversibility (avaivartika), both the forbearance of non-arising's "immovable" descended into the mark of mastery (effortlessness its positive mark · non-regress its negative mark); and this eighth-ground effortless flow and non-regress is still bound to the bodhisattva's causal stage, strictly distinct from the tathāgata's fruit-stage.** The argument: §II presents the canon (effortless flow · effort-free · non-regress · the activity of mind/will/consciousness no longer manifesting); §III argues in three layers (the positive mark of effortless flow · effort-free, 3.1; the negative mark of avaivartika · non-regress, 3.2; the two marks as the two faces of mastery after the forbearance, both bound to the causal stage, 3.3); §IV is cut; §V corrects misreadings and marks the scope of the three-fold non-regress doxography, the disputed ground, and the causal-stage boundary; §VI concludes, takes up P06, binds P04, and opens P08/P10.

> Two scope-notes flagged here (detailed in §V): first, the three-fold doxography of "non-regress of position · non-regress of conduct · non-regress of mindfulness" is largely in the later commentaries (Tiantai · Yogācāra), not a list directly extractable from this paper's four designated sources (Avataṃsaka T0279 / Daśabhūmika T0287 / Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna T1522 / Śāstra T1509); this paper grounds "irreversibility" purely on the four sources' canonical witness (the *Śāstra*'s avaivartika · neither lost nor regressing; the *Daśabhūmika*'s the activity of mind, will, and consciousness no longer manifesting), and the systematic adjudication of the three-fold terms and the precise placement of "non-regress of mindfulness" are left to the author (whether to invoke the later doxography or point to another vault witness), this paper not passing them off as the four sources. Second, the ultimate ground at which the forbearance / irreversibility is established (the seventh · the eighth established) is disputed in the canon (already presented in P06 §V), which this paper takes up without adjudicating; the marks of eighth-ground effortless flow and non-regress are all bound to the bodhisattva's causal stage, strictly distinct from the tathāgata's fruit-stage (the eighth ground's ten masteries routed to P08).

## II. Canonical Basis

This section presents two clusters: the effortless flow of the Immovable eighth ground (2.1); and the avaivartika non-regress of "the forbearance is just avaivartika," with the activity of mind, will, and consciousness no longer manifesting (2.2). The sources are the *Avataṃsaka* (T0279), the *Daśabhūmika* (Xuanzang, T0287), the *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna* (Vasubandhu, T1522), and the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* (T1509), all CBETA-verified.

### 2.1 The Effortless Flow of the Immovable Eighth Ground: Letting Go of All Effortful Conduct · Gaining the Effort-Free Dharma · Abiding in the Conduct of Recompense

The mark of effortless flow in abiding on the eighth, Immovable ground the *Avataṃsaka* gives:

> Abiding in the Immovable ground, [this bodhisattva] lets go of all effortful conduct, gains the effort-free dharma, the strivings of body, speech, and mind all ceasing, abiding in the conduct of recompense.[^1]

Abiding in the Immovable ground, one "lets go of all effortful conduct, gains the effort-free dharma, the strivings of body, speech, and mind all ceasing, abiding in the conduct of recompense" — letting go of all "effortful conduct" (the deliberately contrived strivings of mind), gaining the "effort-free dharma," the strivings of body, speech, and mind all stilled, abiding in the "conduct of recompense" (the spontaneously functioning conduct of recompense). The *Daśabhūmika* (Xuanzang) further brings out the word "spontaneous flow":

> Sons of the Buddha! The bodhisattva, as soon as he gains the Immovable ground, is likewise: apart from all effort, spontaneously flowing to gain the effort-free nature, far removed from all strivings of body, speech, and mind, abiding in maturation.[^2]

"Apart from all effort, spontaneously flowing to gain the effort-free nature, far removed from all strivings of body, speech, and mind, abiding in maturation" — apart from all effort, "spontaneously flowing" to gain the effort-free nature, far removed from the strivings of body, speech, and mind, abiding in "maturation" (recompense-gained). This phrase "spontaneous flow" marks the positive mark of mastery after the forbearance. The *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna* (Vasubandhu) judges the eighth ground's foremost excellence over the lower grounds to be "effort-free spontaneity":

> Among the lower grounds there are three kinds of excellence: first, effort-free spontaneity … Abiding in this eighth bodhisattva-ground, the Immovable, one departs from all effortful conduct and all recollection, gains the effort-free dharma, departs from the strivings of body, speech, and mind, and the conduct of recompense is accomplished.[^3]

Vasubandhu judges the eighth ground's foremost excellence over the lower grounds to be "effort-free spontaneity" — the "effort-free dharma" countering "effortful conduct," departing from all effortful conduct and recollection, abiding in the conduct of recompense. Combining the three: the mastery after the forbearance (abiding in the Immovable ground) appears as the effortless mark of "letting go of the effortful · gaining the effort-free · spontaneous flow · abiding in the conduct of recompense (maturation)." (Note: Anchor 2-4 / [^3] bears on both P07's effortless flow and P08's naming of the Immovable ground; this paper takes up its effortless-flow face, P08 separately taking the Immovable-ground-naming / ten-masteries face.)

### 2.2 The Avaivartika Non-Regress of "the Forbearance Is Just Avaivartika" · the Activity of Mind, Will, and Consciousness No Longer Manifesting

The non-regress mark of mastery after the forbearance the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* judges by "the forbearance of non-arising is just the avaivartika (irreversible) stage," marking it "life after life always gaining the spiritual powers of recompense, neither lost nor regressing":

> Single-mindedly accepting and bearing the wisdom of true reality used by all the buddhas of the ten directions, which nothing can break and nothing can move — this is named the forbearance of non-arising. The forbearance of non-arising is just the avaivartika stage.[^4]

> Abiding in the avaivartika stage, life after life one always gains the spiritual powers of recompense, neither lost nor regressing.[^5]

"The forbearance of non-arising is just the avaivartika stage" — the forbearance of non-arising is just the irreversible stage, its non-regress essence being just what P06 established as "the wisdom of true reality nothing can break or move" (the wisdom nothing can break or move, so the position does not regress); its mark "abiding in the avaivartika stage, life after life always gaining the spiritual powers of recompense, neither lost nor regressing." The *Daśabhūmika* (Xuanzang) further marks the Immovable ground's mark of cessation, "the activity of mind, will, and consciousness no longer manifesting":

> [As soon as the bodhisattva reaches the Immovable ground, all effort, without exception, ceases …] all the activity of mind, will, and consciousness no longer manifests … [in the lower grounds] the afflictions in the end do not manifest. Sons of the Buddha! When the bodhisattva abides on this Immovable ground, it is likewise.[^6]

On the Immovable ground "all the activity of mind, will, and consciousness no longer manifests … the afflictions in the end do not manifest" — all the effortful activity of mind, will, and consciousness no longer manifests, and the afflictions in the end do not manifest (the sutra uses the simile "as one born in the Brahmā world has no manifest desire-realm afflictions" to mark this still, abiding mark of non-regress). Combining the two: the mastery after the forbearance appears as the avaivartika non-regress — the forbearance is just avaivartika (non-regress of position), life after life the spiritual powers of recompense neither lost nor regressing, the activity of mind, will, and consciousness no longer manifesting (the mark of cessation, the afflictions not manifesting).

> Scope (detailed in §V): the witnesses for "non-regress" above are taken purely from this paper's four sources' literal text — "avaivartika," "neither lost nor regressing," "the activity of mind, will, and consciousness no longer manifesting." The later three-fold doxography of "non-regress of position · non-regress of conduct · non-regress of mindfulness" (Tiantai · Yogācāra) is not a list directly extractable from these four sources, and this paper does not pass it off as such; the precise placement of the term "non-regress of mindfulness" is left to the author (§V).

## III. Core Argument

§II has shown the canon's express words. This section argues in three layers: the positive mark of effortless flow · effort-free (3.1); the negative mark of avaivartika · non-regress (3.2); the two marks as the two faces of mastery after the forbearance, both bound to the causal stage (3.3).

### 3.1 Effortless Flow · Effort-Free: The Positive Mark of Mastery After the Forbearance (Letting Go of the Strivings of Effort, Not Letting Go of the Conduct of Delivering Beings)

After the forbearance, the mastery positively appears as "the spontaneous flow of effortlessness" (2.1). This layer establishes its sense and breaks the misreading "effortless flow = idleness." "Effort-free" (anābhoga) means without "the strivings of effort" — "effort" (ābhoga) denotes deliberate contrivance, the spurring of the mind (as a beginner must step by step deliberately apply his mind); "effort-free" is to have become so practised that one no longer waits on deliberate contrivance but flows of itself (as an expert acts without forethought and it comes of itself). The eighth-ground bodhisattva "lets go of all effortful conduct, gains the effort-free dharma, the strivings of body, speech, and mind all ceasing, abiding in the conduct of recompense" (2.1) — what is let go is "the strivings of body, speech, and mind (the spurring of deliberate mind)," what is gained is "the effort-free dharma" (the conduct flowing of itself without waiting on spurring); "spontaneous flow" (the express phrase of 2.2) just means this "running of itself without waiting on effort."

Why does the mastery after the forbearance appear as effortless flow? Because P06 has established: the forbearance of non-arising is the abiding in the direct seeing "apart from all discriminating thought of mind, will, and consciousness" — the mind having departed from discriminating grasping and abiding in reality, its conduct no longer needs to "deliberately spur with the discriminating mind," but can flow of itself by the power of the direct seeing. Before the seventh ground (with effort), the bodhisattva's conduct must still deliberately apply effort with the discriminating mind (as rowing a boat requires arm-strength at the oar); at the eighth ground (effort-free), having realized the forbearance of non-arising and departed from discrimination, his conduct flows of itself (as a boat entering the favorable current goes of itself, without waiting on the oar). The *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna*'s judging the eighth ground's foremost excellence to be "effort-free spontaneity," the effort-free countering the effortful (2.1), just marks this turn from the seventh ground's effort to the eighth ground's effortlessness.

But the misreading "effortless flow = idleness, inaction" must be forcefully broken. "Letting go of all effortful conduct" is by no means "letting go of the conduct of delivering beings" — what is let go is "the strivings of effort (the contriving application of mind)," not "conduct" itself. The eighth-ground bodhisattva's delivering of beings not only does not cease, but is the more "spontaneously flowing" (delivering beings of itself without waiting on deliberate contrivance). A crucial counter-witness: in the sutra the buddhas must "rouse" the eighth-ground bodhisattva — precisely because the realm of the eighth ground's effortless flow approaches quiescence (all the activity of mind, will, and consciousness no longer manifesting, 2.2), the buddhas, fearing he indulge in that effortless quiet and not rouse himself to continue delivering beings, must exhort him to arouse his original vow (this sense of "rousing" is detailed in P09 and the Realization axis's Immovable eighth ground). That the buddhas must "rouse" him is precisely the counter-proof that the eighth ground's flow is not "inaction," but "able-not-to-act yet still must act (deliver beings)" — for if effortless flow were idleness and inaction, the buddhas' rousing would be superfluous. So effortless flow is "the delivering of beings without effort" (the spontaneously flowing altruism), not "the reclining of not-delivering-beings."

A comparison: rowing the oar versus the favorable current. A person rowing a boat, against the water or with no wind, must apply arm-strength at the oar, step by step with effort (with effort), and if he rests a little the boat retreats — this is the effortful conduct before the seventh ground. Once the boat enters the favorable current (or, gaining a fair wind, raises the sail), he need row no more; the boat goes of itself, spontaneously flowing forward (effortless flow) — "letting go of the oar" is not "abandoning the voyage," but precisely that the voyage has entered the favorable current and no longer waits on the strength of rowing. The eighth ground's "letting go of all effortful conduct" is just like "entering the favorable current and letting go of the oar": what is let go is "the deliberate exertion of rowing," not "the voyage (delivering beings)" itself; the boat (the bodhisattva) is still moving forward (delivering beings), and the more smoothly, the more effortlessly (spontaneously flowing). To read "letting go of the oar" as "abandoning the voyage and reclining" is just like reading "effortless flow" as "idle, not delivering beings" — both lose the sense.

> ✅ Doctrinally sound: the positive mark of mastery after the forbearance is "the spontaneous flow of effortlessness" (the *Avataṃsaka*'s letting go of effortful conduct, gaining the effort-free dharma, abiding in the conduct of recompense · the *Daśabhūmika*'s spontaneously flowing to the effort-free nature, abiding in maturation · the *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna*'s effort-free spontaneity, 2.1). "Effort-free" means without the deliberately contrived strivings (effort / ābhoga), not without conduct; because P06's forbearance of non-arising departs from discriminating grasping and abides in reality, conduct flows of itself without waiting on discriminating spurring (as a boat entering the favorable current does not wait on the oar). "Effortless flow" is not idleness or inaction — what is let go is the deliberately contrived strivings, not the conduct of delivering beings; the eighth ground delivers beings by effortless flow (the buddhas must rouse him, which is just the counter-proof that it is not inaction, detailed in P09).

### 3.2 Avaivartika · Non-Regress: The Negative Mark of Mastery After the Forbearance (Because the Wisdom of True Reality Nothing Can Break or Move, the Position Does Not Regress)

After the forbearance, the mastery negatively appears as "the non-regress of avaivartika" (2.2). This layer establishes its sense and its binding to P06. "Avaivartika" is non-regress — position and conduct, having reached this, no longer regress or fall. The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* judges "the forbearance of non-arising is just the avaivartika stage" (2.2): why is the forbearance of non-arising just non-regress? Because P06 has established that the essence of the forbearance is "the wisdom of true reality nothing can break or move" — the cognition of reality being already "unbreakable, unmovable" (no object-condition able to break it or move it), its position is of itself "non-regressing" (nothing able to make it regress). So the essence of non-regress is just P06's cognitive "immovable": the cognition being immovable (the wisdom of true reality nothing can break or move), the position does not regress (avaivartika). One sees that P06's "cognitive immovability" and this paper's "non-regress of position" are the cognitive face and the mastery-mark face of one thing — because the cognition is immovable, the position does not regress.

Its non-regress mark is twofold. First, "life after life always gaining the spiritual powers of recompense, neither lost nor regressing" (2.2) — one abiding in the avaivartika stage, life after life always gains the spiritual powers of recompense, neither lost nor regressing (the merit and spiritual powers already gained are no longer lost or made to regress). Second, "all the activity of mind, will, and consciousness no longer manifests … the afflictions in the end do not manifest" (2.2) — the Immovable ground's mark of cessation: all the effortful activity of mind, will, and consciousness no longer manifests, and the afflictions in the end do not manifest. The sutra uses the simile "as one born in the Brahmā world has no manifest desire-realm afflictions" — one born in the Brahmā world has, of itself, no manifest desire-realm afflictions (not by the deliberateness of suppressing or severing, but by a non-manifesting that the realm itself brings about); the eighth ground's non-manifesting of afflictions is likewise, a spontaneous non-manifesting that the realized realm brings about, hence the abiding mark of "non-regress" (the afflictions not manifesting, there is of itself no danger of "being moved by afflictions and regressing").

So the negative mark of mastery after the forbearance (non-regress), its root is P06's cognitive immovability: the wisdom of true reality nothing can break or move (cognition), so the position does not regress, the spiritual powers of recompense are not lost, the afflictions do not manifest (the mark of mastery). This too binds to this axis's earlier papers: P01 established the bodhisattva's mastery signed by "not-taking," and what makes it able not to take and yet not fall back into the two-vehicle ground is just this "wisdom of unobtainable emptiness / wisdom of true reality nothing can break or move" (P03 3.2, P06 3.1) — by the time of after-the-forbearance (the eighth ground), this "unbreakable, unmovable" cognition has ripened into the avaivartika "non-regress of position."

A comparison: a rooted tree versus a planted cutting. A cutting planted in the soil (not yet rooted), though it stand upright for a time, falls in wind and rain, and can be pulled up and moved (can regress) — like the bodhisattva before realizing the forbearance of non-arising, whose roots of good are not yet deeply planted in reality, and who under adverse conditions can regress. A tree whose roots are deeply planted in the ground (already rooted), wind and rain cannot pull up, nothing can shake (does not regress) — like the bodhisattva after realizing the forbearance of non-arising, whose cognition is deeply planted in reality (the wisdom of true reality nothing can break or move), so the position does not regress and nothing can shake it. The "non-regress" of avaivartika is just the mastery-mark of "cognition deeply planted in reality and nothing can shake it" — not a non-regress propped up by external force, but a natural non-regress of roots deep in reality.

> ✅ Doctrinally sound: the negative mark of mastery after the forbearance is "the non-regress of avaivartika" (the *Śāstra*'s the forbearance is just avaivartika · life after life the spiritual powers of recompense neither lost nor regressing · the *Daśabhūmika*'s the activity of mind, will, and consciousness no longer manifesting, the afflictions not manifesting, 2.2). Its essence is just P06's cognitive "immovable": the wisdom of true reality nothing can break or move (cognition immovable), so the position does not regress (the mastery-mark of non-regress). The non-regress mark is twofold: the spiritual powers of recompense neither lost nor regressing; the activity of mind, will, and consciousness and the afflictions not manifesting (the spontaneous cessation as one born in the Brahmā world has no manifest desire-realm afflictions). The root of non-regress is deeply planted in reality (as a tree takes root), not propped up by external force.

### 3.3 The Two Marks as the Two Faces of Mastery After the Forbearance, and Both Bound to the Causal Stage (Strictly Distinct from the Tathāgata's Fruit-Stage)

The last layer joins the two marks and keeps the causal-stage boundary. Effortless flow (effort-free) and non-regress (avaivartika) are the two faces of mastery after the forbearance — both springing from P06's "immovable," and appearing severally as two senses: the effortless flow of "not-acting" (positive: the mastery running of itself without waiting on effort), the non-regress of "not-shaking" (negative: the mastery, nothing able to break or move it, not regressing). The name of the "Immovable ground" (acalā, immovable) just holds both senses — both not-acting (effortless flow) and not-shaking (avaivartika non-regress). So the mastery after the forbearance is a mastery "both flowing effortlessly and not regressing": it flows effortlessly (without exertion), because the cognition has departed from discrimination and abides in reality; it does not regress (does not fall back), because the cognition, nothing able to break or move it, is deeply planted in reality. Effortless flow and non-regress are the positive and negative marks of one and the same "immovable" cognition.

Yet the causal-stage boundary must be forcefully kept (taking up P04 2.4 and P06 §V). This eighth-ground effortless flow and non-regress, high as it is, is still a mark of the "bodhisattva's" causal stage, not the tathāgata's fruit-stage. First, the "conduct of recompense / maturation" the eighth ground abides in (2.1) is the recompense-gained of the bodhisattva's causal stage, not the ultimate of the buddha-fruit. Second, the eighth ground's ten masteries (routed to P08), the *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna* expressly puts as "this bodhisattva gains these bodhisattva's ten masteries" (the word "bodhisattva" repeated), strictly distinct from the tathāgata's ten masteries. Third, the plainest witness: the buddhas must still exhort the eighth-ground bodhisattva "the ten powers, the fearlessnesses, the eighteen distinctive dharmas of the buddhas — these you have not yet attained" (cited at P04 2.4) — even the eighth ground's effortless flow and non-regress, the buddha still plainly admonishes "you have not yet attained (the buddha-fruit)." So the effortless flow and non-regress after the forbearance is the ripened mark of "causal-stage mastery," not the ultimate of "fruit-stage mastery"; the strict distinction of the eighth ground's ten masteries from the tathāgata's ten masteries belongs to this axis's P08, this paper only keeping the boundary.

And it must be remembered (breaking "effortless flow = resting in quiescence" at a deeper level): the eighth ground's effortless flow and non-regress approaching quiescence (the activity of mind, will, and consciousness no longer manifesting) is just one peril of the bodhisattva-path — if one indulge in that effortless quiet and not further rouse oneself to deliver beings and seek the buddha-fruit, one rests at the eighth ground and goes no further. The buddhas' "rousing" in the sutra (exhorting the eighth-ground bodhisattva to rouse his original vow, to continue delivering beings and seek the buddha-fruit) is just to cross this peril. So the effortless flow and non-regress after the forbearance is not the terminus, but something that "must further advance within non-regress, must continue (delivering beings, seeking the buddha-fruit) within effortless flow" — and this sense of "not abiding in the eighth ground's quiescence" just opens this axis's P09, "Non-Abiding Nirvāṇa" (abiding neither in birth-and-death nor in nirvāṇa).

> ⚠️ Scope (detailed in §V): first, the eighth ground's effortless flow and non-regress are all bound to the bodhisattva's causal stage (the conduct of recompense / maturation · the bodhisattva's ten masteries), strictly distinct from the tathāgata's fruit-stage (the buddhas exhort "you have not yet attained"); the eighth ground's ten masteries routed to P08. Second, the ultimate ground at which the forbearance / non-regress is established (the seventh / eighth) is disputed in the canon (P06 §V), which this paper takes up without adjudicating. Third, the three-fold "non-regress of position · conduct · mindfulness" is later doxography, not a four-source list, this paper not passing it off (detailed below).

Three layers together: effortless flow (effort-free) is the positive mark of mastery after the forbearance (letting go of the strivings of effort and delivering beings by effortless flow, 3.1); avaivartika (non-regress) is its negative mark (because the wisdom of true reality nothing can break or move, the position does not regress, 3.2); the two marks are the two faces of "immovable," both bound to the causal stage (strictly distinct from the tathāgata's fruit-stage, 3.3). Together they establish the thesis: **after the forbearance the mastery appears in the two marks of effortless flow (effort-free) and irreversibility (avaivartika), both the forbearance of non-arising's "immovable" descended into the mark of mastery (effortless flow the not-acting · non-regress the not-shaking); and this eighth-ground effortless flow and non-regress is still bound to the bodhisattva's causal stage, strictly distinct from the tathāgata's fruit-stage (the three-fold non-regress being later doxography, not passed off here as the four sources).**

## IV. Interdisciplinary Convergence

(This paper deliberately omits the interdisciplinary section; the reason is given in §VI. Its axis is the Mahāyāna eighth-ground doctrine of "the mastery after the forbearance appearing as effortless flow and avaivartika non-regress." Modern models — "the automaticity / flow of expert mastery," "the irreversible entrenchment of habit" — superficially resemble effortless flow and avaivartika non-regress in "behaviour flowing of itself after mastery without waiting on deliberation" and "the stable non-regress after deep acquisition," but cannot sustain a three-layer convergence without lapsing into analogy on three points: "effortless flow springing from the forbearance of non-arising's departing-from-discrimination direct seeing of reality (not the automaticity of skill-practice)," "avaivartika non-regress rooted in the wisdom of true reality nothing can break or move (not the neural entrenchment of habit)," and "eighth-ground effortless flow and non-regress bound to the causal stage · contrasting with the unattained fruit-stage of the tathāgata · and, approaching quiescence, needing the buddhas' rousing." On the first especially, the "effort-free" of effortless flow is brought about by the "departing from the discrimination of mind, will, and consciousness" direct seeing (P06), with no counterpart in any model reducible to skill-automaticity (a practisedness still within mind, will, and consciousness); the automaticity of flow is still the practisedness of a conditioned mind-consciousness, only superficially resembling the realized realm of "the strivings of body, speech, and mind all ceasing · the activity of mind, will, and consciousness no longer manifesting." Hence omitted per the series' editorial convention, with this note.)

## V. Madhyamaka Corrections

This section clears three misreadings adjacent to the thesis, the latter two bearing on the doxographical boundary and the causal-stage boundary, both requiring special honesty.

**❌ Misreading one: "effortless flow (effort-free) = idleness · inaction · letting go of the conduct of delivering beings and reclining in non-doing."** This the paper broke at the outset, and restates here at a deeper level. What "letting go of all effortful conduct" lets go is "the strivings of effort (the contriving exertion of deliberate mind)," not "the conduct of delivering beings" itself (3.1). The eighth-ground bodhisattva's delivering of beings not only does not cease, but is the more spontaneously flowing (altruism flowing of itself without waiting on deliberate contrivance). The plainest counter-witness: in the sutra the buddhas must "rouse" the eighth-ground bodhisattva — precisely because the realm of the eighth ground's effortless flow approaches quiescence (the activity of mind, will, and consciousness no longer manifesting, 2.2), the buddhas, fearing he indulge in quiescence and not rouse himself, exhort him to arouse his original vow to continue delivering beings (3.3). The buddhas' "rousing" is precisely the counter-proof that the eighth ground is not "inaction" (if effortless flow were inaction, rousing would be superfluous), but "able-not-to-act yet still ought to act (deliver beings, seek the buddha-fruit)." So effortless flow is "the delivering of beings without effort" (the spontaneously flowing altruism), not "the idleness of not-delivering-beings"; to read "letting go of the oar" (letting go of deliberate exertion) as "abandoning the voyage and reclining" (abandoning delivering beings) loses the sense.

**⚠️ Correction two: the three-fold doxography of "non-regress of position · non-regress of conduct · non-regress of mindfulness" is largely later (Tiantai · Yogācāra), not a list directly extractable from this paper's four designated sources; this paper grounds "irreversibility" purely on the four sources' canonical witness, not passing it off.** To be marked with care: what this paper treats as "irreversibility" is established purely on the literal canonical witness of the four designated sources (Avataṃsaka T0279 · Daśabhūmika T0287 · Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna T1522 · Śāstra T1509) — the *Śāstra*'s "the forbearance is just avaivartika · life after life the spiritual powers of recompense neither lost nor regressing" (2.2), the *Daśabhūmika*'s "the activity of mind, will, and consciousness no longer manifesting · the afflictions not manifesting" (2.2). The three-fold "non-regress of position · non-regress of conduct · non-regress of mindfulness," with its three terms set out as a three-fold list, established in the later commentaries (e.g. Tiantai Zhiyi's *Mohe Zhiguan* · the Yogācāra masters' stage-matching), on a thorough check of this paper's four sources is not found as a directly extractable settled list (the *Śāstra*, in the passage cited, speaks of non-regress in general by "the avaivartika stage / neither lost nor regressing," not making the three terms into a three-fold division). So this paper does not pass the later three-fold scheme off as belonging to the four sources; the precise placement of the term "non-regress of mindfulness" (whether to invoke the later doxography such as Tiantai / Yogācāra, or to point to another vault witness) is left to the author. Let the reader not take this paper's "irreversibility" as already containing the later three-fold doxography. This scope is marked per the recorded source-gap.

**⚠️ Correction three: the eighth ground's effortless flow and non-regress are all bound to the bodhisattva's causal stage, strictly distinct from the tathāgata's fruit-stage effortless flow; and its ground (the seventh / eighth) is disputed in the canon, this paper not adjudicating.** The boundary must be kept: the effortless flow and non-regress after the forbearance this paper treats (abiding in the conduct of recompense / maturation · the eighth ground's ten masteries) are all marks of the "bodhisattva's" causal stage, not the tathāgata's fruit-stage. The *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna* expressly puts the eighth ground's ten masteries as "this bodhisattva gains these bodhisattva's ten masteries" (the word "bodhisattva" repeated, P04 2.4), and the buddhas still exhort the eighth-ground bodhisattva "the ten powers and fearlessnesses — these you have not yet attained (the buddha-fruit)." So to misread the eighth ground's effortless flow and non-regress as the ultimate effortless flow of the buddha-fruit, or to misread the eighth ground's ten masteries as the tathāgata's ten masteries, erases the course from the eighth ground upward and through to the buddha-fruit, contravening the sutra's plain admonition "you have not yet attained." The strict distinction of the eighth ground's ten masteries from the tathāgata's ten masteries belongs to this axis's P08, this paper only keeping the boundary. And the ultimate ground at which the forbearance / non-regress is established (the seventh · the eighth established) is disputed in the canon (already presented in P06 §V: the *Avataṃsaka* ties it to the eighth · the *Śāstra* to avaivartika without fixing a ground · the Yogācāra masters' seventh/eighth difference), which this paper takes up without adjudicating or taking sides.

## VI. Conclusion

**Restatement.** After the forbearance the mastery appears in the two marks of effortless flow (effort-free) and irreversibility (avaivartika). In the canon, the *Avataṃsaka* states that abiding in the Immovable ground one "lets go of all effortful conduct, gains the effort-free dharma, the strivings of body, speech, and mind all ceasing, abiding in the conduct of recompense," the *Daśabhūmika* (Xuanzang) states "apart from all effort, spontaneously flowing to gain the effort-free nature, abiding in maturation," the *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna* (Vasubandhu) judges the eighth ground's foremost excellence to be "effort-free spontaneity"; the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* judges "the forbearance of non-arising is just the avaivartika stage," "life after life the spiritual powers of recompense neither lost nor regressing," and the *Daśabhūmika* states "all the activity of mind, will, and consciousness no longer manifests · the afflictions in the end do not manifest." Doctrinally, three layers: effortless flow · effort-free is the positive mark (letting go of the strivings of effort and delivering beings by effortless flow, 3.1); avaivartika · non-regress is the negative mark (because the wisdom of true reality nothing can break or move, the position does not regress, 3.2); the two marks are the two faces of "immovable," both bound to the causal stage (strictly distinct from the tathāgata's fruit-stage, 3.3). To be marked with care: the three-fold "non-regress of position · conduct · mindfulness" is later doxography (Tiantai · Yogācāra), not a four-source list, this paper not passing it off (§V); the eighth ground's effortless flow and non-regress are all bound to the causal stage, its ground being disputed and not adjudicated here (§V).

**Practice guidance.** Here the practitioner should clear two confusions and keep one mindfulness. To clear the first confusion: do not take "effortless flow · effort-free" for idleness and reclining — what effortlessness lets go is the deliberately contrived strivings, not the conduct of delivering beings; true effortless flow is "delivering beings without exertion" (as a boat entering the favorable current still goes forward), not "abandoning the voyage and reclining." Even the eighth-ground bodhisattva the buddha still rouses to continue — how much more should the practitioner, who excuses himself with "effortless flow" and grows idle in altruism? To clear the second confusion: do not take "non-regress" for a settled security already gained and preen over it — the root of non-regress is the cognition "the wisdom of true reality nothing can break or move" (P06), not a propping-up by external force; to claim non-regress falsely without having truly realized the forbearance of non-arising is the conceit of overweening attainment. To keep one mindfulness: keep the mindfulness of "further advancing within non-regress, continuing within effortless flow" — the effortless flow and non-regress after the forbearance is not the terminus, the eighth ground's approach to quiescence being just a peril (the buddhas must rouse), so within effortless flow and non-regress one should not indulge in quiescence, but continue delivering beings and seek the buddha-fruit (this "not abiding in the eighth ground's quiescence" just opens non-abiding nirvāṇa, P09). And keep in mind the causal-stage boundary: though one gain effortless flow and non-regress, it is still a causal-stage mark, the buddha-fruit "you have not yet attained"; not taking the eighth ground's mark for the ultimate is what accords with the bodhisattva-path.

**Taking up and opening.** This paper takes up the preceding paper P06: P06 established the forbearance of non-arising as the cognitive signature "making mastery immovable" (the cognitive immovable-and-irreversible); this paper unfolds the two faces of that "immovable" descended into the mark of mastery — effortless flow (not-acting) and non-regress (not-shaking). The cognitive "immovable and irreversible" (P06) is just the mastery-mark's "effortless flow · non-regress" (this paper), the cognitive face and the mark-face of one thing. Bound to P04: this paper's "effortless flow · effort-free" binds to P04's "the four added perfections as the deployment of post-realization non-discriminating wisdom" — the post-attainment wisdom's returning-into and putting-to-use (the four added perfections), by the eighth ground, flows effortlessly without effort (without waiting on deliberate contrivance); P04 established the deployment of post-attainment wisdom, this paper establishes its effortless mark at the eighth ground. Opening forward: first, the eighth ground's ten masteries (this paper only keeping the causal-stage boundary, not unfolding them), and their strict distinction from the tathāgata's ten masteries, belong to this axis's P08, "The Ten Masteries of the Eighth Ground." Second, the "not abiding in the eighth ground's quiescence, continuing to deliver beings and seek the buddha-fruit" of effortless flow and non-regress after the forbearance, its ultimate ground (abiding neither in birth-and-death nor in nirvāṇa), belongs to this axis's P09, "Non-Abiding Nirvāṇa"; and the ultimate adjudication of the bodhisattva's and the arhat's masteries awaits P10. As for the Realization axis: this paper's effortless flow and non-regress after the forbearance correlate thematically with the `bodhi-path` division's BODHI-P09, "The Immovable Eighth Ground" — the Realization axis walks the course of the Immovable eighth ground (including effortless flow · non-regress · the buddhas' rousing), the Attainment axis (this paper) reads the two marks of mastery after the forbearance that course shows; the seam's doctrine is the author's, and this paper does not speak for the Realization axis.

**Open questions.** First, the paper cuts §IV (effortless flow and non-regress versus the automaticity of flow / the entrenchment of habit are only superficially alike), as above. Second, the three-fold "non-regress of position · conduct · mindfulness" is later doxography, not a four-source list, this paper not passing it off; the precise placement of the term "non-regress of mindfulness" (whether to invoke the later doxography) is left to the author. Third, the ultimate ground of the forbearance / non-regress (the seventh / eighth) is disputed in the canon, which this paper takes up from P06 without adjudicating. Fourth, the eighth ground's ten masteries' binding to the causal stage and their strict distinction from the tathāgata's ten masteries, this paper only keeps the boundary, the full treatment belonging to P08. Fifth, the correlation of "effortless flow · effort-free" with the Yogācāra "effort-free wisdom / path," and the full reconciliation with the eighth-ground-related passages of the *Saṃdhinirmocana* and the *Cheng Weishi Lun*, this paper does not unfold, awaiting another treatment.

- **BODHI-P08, "The Ten Masteries of the Eighth Ground"** (forthcoming): this paper's 3.3 and §V keep the boundary — the eighth ground's effortless flow and non-regress are all bound to the bodhisattva's causal stage, its ten masteries strictly distinct from the tathāgata's ten masteries; P08 treats the eighth ground's ten masteries and their boundary with the tathāgata's ten masteries.
- **BODHI-P09, "Non-Abiding Nirvāṇa"** (forthcoming): this paper's 3.3 "not indulging in the eighth ground's quiescence within effortless flow and non-regress, continuing to deliver beings and seek the buddha-fruit (the buddhas' rousing)," its ultimate ground (abiding neither in birth-and-death nor in nirvāṇa), belongs to P09's non-abiding nirvāṇa.
- **BODHI-P10, "The Ultimate Adjudication of the Bodhisattva's and the Arhat's Masteries"** (forthcoming · close): the causal-stage mark of this paper's effortless flow and non-regress, in its ultimate co-adjudication with the arhat's mastery (taking the realization · exiting), awaits P10.
- **★ Realization-axis `bodhi-path` BODHI-P09, "The Immovable Eighth Ground"** (parallel · thematically related): the Realization axis walks the course of the Immovable eighth ground (including effortless flow · non-regress · the buddhas' rousing), the Attainment axis (this paper) reads the two marks of mastery after the forbearance that course shows (effortless flow · non-regress); on the same eighth-ground canon of the Avataṃsaka / Daśabhūmika / Śāstra, read severally as conduct (walked) and as the mark of mastery (the effortless flow and non-regress after the forbearance).
