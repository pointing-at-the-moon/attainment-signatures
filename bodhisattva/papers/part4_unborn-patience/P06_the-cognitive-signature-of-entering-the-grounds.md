# The Forbearance of Non-Arising as the Cognitive Signature of Entering the Grounds

**Entering All Dharmas as the Nature of Empty Space · Contemplating All Dharmas as Neither Arising nor Ceasing · The Mind Penetrating Without Obstruction, Immovable and Irreversible · The Cognitive Turn by Which Mastery Becomes Immovable**

*中文主題：無生法忍為登地之認知簽名——自在所以不動之認知轉折*

## Abstract

The first three parts established the ground of the bodhisattva's mastery (from the vow, P01), its measure (the two boundless provisions, P02), and its shape (the six and ten perfections, the four embraces, P03–P05). But mastery, to be mastery, has a further *cognitive* root: the bodhisattva's mind must undergo a cognitive turn before its mastery becomes "immovable." This part (Part 4 · The Forbearance of Non-Arising) establishes that cognitive root; this paper establishes the signature of the turn — the forbearance of non-arising (anutpattika-dharma-kṣānti). The thesis: the forbearance of non-arising is the cognitive signature of the bodhisattva's mastery — its content the direct seeing of suchness ("entering all dharmas as having the nature of empty space · contemplating all dharmas as neither arising nor ceasing"); its essence the receptive acceptance that "in the wisdom of true reality there is nothing that can break it, nothing that can move it"; its function the turning of the mastery from "the grasping of applied effort" into "the mind penetrating without obstruction · immovable and irreversible." Three layers. First, the content of the forbearance — the *Avataṃsaka* states "apart from all discriminating thought of mind, will, and consciousness, grasping nothing, like empty space, entering all dharmas as having the nature of empty space, this is called gaining the forbearance of non-arising," and the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* gives its contemplation as "contemplating all dharmas as neither arising nor ceasing, neither not-arising nor not-ceasing," the cognitive content of the root non-discriminating wisdom directly seeing suchness (taking up P04's 3.2). Second, why it is called "forbearance" and why it is a *cognitive* signature — this "forbearance" (kṣānti) is not the patience of the perfection of patience (the conduct of bearing abuse, P03), but the cognitive settledness of "accepting, bearing, and being convinced of" true reality, whence the *Śāstra* defines it by "the mind penetrating without obstruction, immovable and irreversible," marking it "the first gate aiding the buddha-path." Third, why it is the signature of *entering the grounds*, and why the ground at which it is established is unsettled — the attainment marks the bodhisattva's entry into the holy station of directly seeing reality, the *Avataṃsaka* stating "accomplishing this forbearance, one immediately enters the eighth, Immovable ground"; yet its ultimate ground-assignment (entering the grounds / the path of seeing · the seventh ground · established at the eighth) is unsettled — the *Śāstra* ties it to "the irreversible stage" (avaivartika) without fixing a ground, the *Avataṃsaka* and *Daśabhūmika* to the eighth, the Yogācāra masters differ over seventh and eighth — this paper sets the *Avataṃsaka*'s eighth-ground establishment and the *Śāstra*'s definition side by side, **the ground-assignment being the author's adjudicated point of difference, which this paper does not take sides on**. To be marked with care: the contemplation of "neither arising nor ceasing" is no nihilistic emptiness; its four-fold negation "neither arising nor ceasing, neither not-arising nor not-ceasing" refuses the grasping of annihilation just as it refuses the grasping of arising-and-ceasing (the middle way). Conclusion: the forbearance of non-arising is the cognitive signature of the bodhisattva's mastery — directly seeing all dharmas as having the nature of empty space, accepting the unbreakable, unmovable wisdom of true reality, making the mastery immovable and irreversible; this cognitive turn is just what the next paper's "effortless flow and irreversibility after the forbearance" issues from (its ultimate ground-assignment being the author's adjudicated point of difference).

**Keywords**: forbearance of non-arising, entering all dharmas as the nature of empty space, contemplating all dharmas as neither arising nor ceasing, mind penetrating without obstruction, immovable and irreversible, the wisdom of true reality nothing can break or move, cognitive signature, root non-discriminating wisdom, directly seeing suchness, receptive conviction, the first gate aiding the buddha-path, entering the grounds, the eighth Immovable ground, the unsettled ground-assignment, the middle-way negation

## I. The Live Question

The first three parts established three matters of the bodhisattva's mastery: its ground (from the vow, P01), its measure (the two boundless provisions, P02), and its shape (the six and ten perfections, the four embraces, P03–P05). Vow, measure, and shape complete, the outline of the bodhisattva's mastery would seem to be filled in. Yet examined closely, one root is still wanting — the *cognitive* root. What is the cognitive root? What the first three parts established is largely the side of *conduct*: arousing the vow, accumulating the provisions, practising the six perfections and the four embraces. But beneath all this conduct, the bodhisattva's mind must undergo a cognitive change before the mastery this conduct accomplishes can be an "immovable" mastery, rather than a still-shakable, still-reversible conditioned good. A person who has not undergone this cognitive turn, though he arouse the great vow and practise the six perfections, has a mind still within "the grasping of arising-and-ceasing" — seeing dharmas as really arising and ceasing, raising grasping toward them; then the root of his mastery is not yet firm, and under adverse conditions it can still regress. This part (Part 4 · The Forbearance of Non-Arising) establishes that cognitive root; and to establish it, one must first identify the *signature* of the turn — the forbearance of non-arising.

What is a "signature"? A signature is the mark by which a thing is *this* thing and not confused with another. This book, *The Attainment Signatures*, identifies in each paper the unmistakable mark of one face of the bodhisattva's mastery (P01 the signature of not-taking, P02 the signature of accumulation, P05 the signature of altruism). What this paper identifies is the signature of the *cognitive* face: when the bodhisattva's mind undergoes what cognitive change do we know that "this mind has entered the bodhisattva's holy station, its mastery now immovable"? The answer: when it accomplishes the "forbearance of non-arising." The forbearance of non-arising is a cognitive event — not the cultivation of yet another good practice, but a root turn in the mind's view of "the true reality of dharmas": from "seeing dharmas as having arising and ceasing, grasping at them" into "contemplating all dharmas as neither arising nor ceasing, entering all dharmas as having the nature of empty space, in the wisdom of true reality nothing breaking or moving it." Once this turn is accomplished, the bodhisattva's mastery is "immovable and irreversible" — and this is what the paper establishes: the forbearance of non-arising is the cognitive signature of the bodhisattva's mastery.

Here a very common misreading must be broken at the outset: reading the "forbearance" (kṣānti) of "the forbearance of non-arising" as "patience-endurance," and so taking the forbearance of non-arising for a higher grade of the "perfection of patience (kṣānti)" among the six — as if it were a deeper "enduring." This must be punctured at once. The patience of the six perfections (kṣānti, P03) is *conduct* — bearing beings' abuse, heat and cold and hunger, without raising a vengeful mind; its object is adverse conditions, its function bearing-without-retaliation. The "forbearance" of the forbearance of non-arising is *cognition* — the "accepting, bearing, and being convinced of" the true reality that dharmas are unarisen (accepting and abiding in this truth). The two share a Sanskrit root (kṣānti), but the one falls on *conduct* (bearing abuse), the other on *cognition* (abiding in reality). What this paper treats is the latter: the mind's acceptance of and settledness in reality, hence "cognitive signature."

The thesis: **the forbearance of non-arising is the cognitive signature of the bodhisattva's mastery — its content the direct seeing of suchness ("entering all dharmas as the nature of empty space · contemplating all dharmas as neither arising nor ceasing"); its essence the receptive acceptance that "in the wisdom of true reality nothing can break or move it"; its function the turning of the mastery from the grasping of applied effort into the mind penetrating without obstruction · immovable and irreversible.** The argument: §II presents the canon (the content of the forbearance · its contemplation · its ground-assignment); §III argues in three layers (the content as the direct seeing of reality, 3.1; why "forbearance," why a cognitive signature, 3.2; why the signature of entering the grounds, and why the ground is unsettled, 3.3); §IV is cut; §V corrects misreadings and marks the unsettled-ground scope and the middle-way scope of "neither arising nor ceasing"; §VI concludes, takes up the first three parts, binds P04, and opens P07.

> One scope-note flagged here (detailed in §V): the ground at which the forbearance of non-arising is "ultimately established" (entering the grounds / the path of seeing · the seventh ground · the eighth, Immovable ground) is disputed in the canon: the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* ties it to "the irreversible stage" (avaivartika) without fixing a definite ground, the *Avataṃsaka* and *Daśabhūmika* tie it to the eighth, Immovable ground (accomplishing this forbearance, one enters it), and the Yogācāra masters have a seventh/eighth difference. This paper sets the *Avataṃsaka*'s eighth-ground establishment (the content of the forbearance, Anchor 1-1) and the *Śāstra*'s definition and contemplation (its essence and contemplation, 1-2/1-3) side by side, **without taking sides on the ground-assignment**; the adjudication of the ground is the author's. What this paper establishes — the "cognitive signature" function (the cognitive turn that makes mastery immovable) — does not move with the disputed ground.

## II. Canonical Basis

This section presents three clusters: the content of the forbearance (entering all dharmas as the nature of empty space · accomplishing it one enters the eighth ground, 2.1); the essence and contemplation of the forbearance (the mind penetrating without obstruction, immovable and irreversible · contemplating all dharmas as neither arising nor ceasing, 2.2); and the disputed ground-assignment of the forbearance (2.3). The sources are the *Avataṃsaka* (T0279) and the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* (T1509), all CBETA-verified.

### 2.1 The Content of the Forbearance: Entering All Dharmas as the Nature of Empty Space · Accomplishing It, One Enters the Eighth, Immovable Ground

The content of the forbearance of non-arising the *Avataṃsaka*'s Ten Grounds (the eighth, Immovable ground) gives:

> Apart from all discriminating thought of mind, will, and consciousness, grasping nothing, like empty space, entering all dharmas as having the nature of empty space — this is called gaining the forbearance of non-arising. Sons of the Buddha! The bodhisattva, accomplishing this forbearance, immediately enters the eighth, Immovable ground.[^1]

The content of the forbearance: "apart from all discriminating thought of mind, will, and consciousness, grasping nothing, like empty space, entering all dharmas as having the nature of empty space" — apart from the discriminating grasping of mind, will, and consciousness, directly seeing all dharmas as having the nature of empty space (unarisen and unceasing, grasping nothing). And its efficacy: "accomplishing this forbearance, one immediately enters the eighth, Immovable ground." This is the paper's primary witness: the content of the forbearance is the direct seeing of "entering all dharmas as the nature of empty space," and its accomplishment marks entry into the holy station of the Immovable ground.

### 2.2 The Essence and Contemplation of the Forbearance: The Mind Penetrating Without Obstruction, Immovable and Irreversible · Contemplating All Dharmas as Neither Arising nor Ceasing

The *essence* of the forbearance the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* defines by "the mind penetrating without obstruction, immovable and irreversible," marking it "the first gate aiding the buddha-path":

> Extinguishing all conceptual elaboration, the path of words cut off, deeply entering the Buddha's Dharma, the mind penetrating without obstruction, immovable and irreversible — this is named the forbearance of non-arising, the first gate aiding the buddha-path.[^2]

"Extinguishing all conceptual elaboration, the path of words cut off" (apart from all conceptual elaboration of discrimination), "deeply entering the Buddha's Dharma, the mind penetrating without obstruction" (the mind penetrating reality without obstruction), "immovable and irreversible" — this is the essence of the forbearance. The treatise marks it "the first gate aiding the buddha-path" (the first essential gate toward the buddha-path). And the *contemplation* of the forbearance (how one contemplates so as to accomplish it) the *Śāstra* further gives:

> If the bodhisattva can contemplate all dharmas as neither arising nor ceasing, neither not-arising nor not-ceasing, neither both nor not-both … single-mindedly accepting and bearing the wisdom of true reality used by all the buddhas of the ten directions, which nothing can break and nothing can move — this is named the forbearance of non-arising.[^3]

Its contemplation is "contemplating all dharmas as neither arising nor ceasing, neither not-arising nor not-ceasing" — by a four-fold negation (neither arising nor ceasing; and, not grasping even this "neither arising nor ceasing" as real, further negating "neither not-arising nor not-ceasing") negating all elaboration and disclosing reality; its essence is "single-mindedly accepting and bearing the wisdom of true reality used by all the buddhas, which nothing can break or move" — receptively accepting and bearing the wisdom of true reality the buddhas use, a wisdom nothing can break or move. Combining 2.1 and 2.2: the content of the forbearance is the direct seeing of "entering all dharmas as the nature of empty space · contemplating all dharmas as neither arising nor ceasing"; its essence the receptive conviction abiding in "the wisdom of true reality nothing can break or move"; its mark "the mind penetrating without obstruction · immovable and irreversible."

### 2.3 The Ground-Assignment of the Forbearance: Disputed (Not Adjudicated)

At what ground the forbearance of non-arising is established the canon disputes. The *Avataṃsaka* (2.1) ties it plainly to "the eighth, Immovable ground" (accomplishing this forbearance, one immediately enters it). The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*, however, ties it to "the irreversible stage" (avaivartika) without fixing a definite ground:

> The forbearance of non-arising is just the irreversible stage (avaivartika).[^4]

The *Śāstra* states plainly "the forbearance of non-arising is just the irreversible stage" — tying it to the stage of non-regress, yet "avaivartika" does not expressly name a definite ground-number (the *Śāstra* elsewhere also ties it to the bodhisattva's entry into the holy stations, the ground-number not uniform). So the ground-assignment of the forbearance — the *Avataṃsaka*/*Daśabhūmika* line ties it to the eighth, Immovable ground established, the *Śāstra* line to the irreversible stage without fixing a ground, the Yogācāra masters have a seventh/eighth difference — is **disputed**.

> Scope (detailed in §V): this paper does not take sides for the author on the ground-assignment of the forbearance. It sets the *Avataṃsaka*'s eighth-ground establishment (the content, 2.1) and the *Śāstra*'s definition, contemplation, and irreversible-stage (the essence and contemplation, 2.2, 2.3) side by side; the adjudication of the ground (entering the grounds / the path of seeing · the seventh · the eighth) is the author's. The "cognitive signature" function this paper establishes does not move with the disputed ground — at whatever ground, the forbearance's being "the cognitive turn that makes mastery immovable" is one and the same.

## III. Core Argument

§II has shown the canon's express words. This section argues in three layers: why the content of the forbearance is the direct seeing of reality (3.1); why it is called "forbearance" and is a *cognitive* signature (3.2); why it is the signature of entering the grounds, and why its ground is unsettled (3.3).

### 3.1 The Content of the Forbearance: Directly Seeing All Dharmas as the Nature of Empty Space · Neither Arising nor Ceasing (the Direct Seeing of the Root Non-Discriminating Wisdom)

The content of the forbearance is the direct seeing of reality, "entering all dharmas as the nature of empty space · contemplating all dharmas as neither arising nor ceasing" (2.1, 2.2). This layer establishes its cognitive substance: this is not a conceptual inference (knowing the principle that "dharmas neither arise nor cease"), but a direct seeing in realization (the mind itself realizing all dharmas as having the nature of empty space, apart from the discriminating grasping of mind, will, and consciousness). The *Avataṃsaka* states "apart from all discriminating thought of mind, will, and consciousness, grasping nothing, like empty space, entering all dharmas as having the nature of empty space" (2.1) — "apart from the discriminating thought of mind, will, and consciousness" is apart from the grasping subject, "entering all dharmas as the nature of empty space" is realizing the grasped reality; subject and object both dissolving, suchness is directly seen — this is just the direct seeing of the root non-discriminating wisdom (taking up P04's 3.2: the root non-discriminating wisdom directly seeing suchness is what the forbearance of non-arising bears on).

Why must the bodhisattva's mastery take this direct seeing as its cognitive root? Because the mastery the first three parts established (vow, measure, shape), without this direct seeing beneath it, is still within the cognition of "grasping at arising-and-ceasing": seeing dharmas as really arising and ceasing, raising grasping and aversion toward them, then arousing the vow has the grasping of "a self that arouses · a vow aroused," practising the six has the grasping of "a self that gives · a thing given" (the simile of "blind apart from prajñā," P03 3.2). Only by directly seeing "all dharmas as the nature of empty space · neither arising nor ceasing," subject and object both dissolving, are the bodhisattva's vow and conduct truly "non-abiding" (not abiding in the grasping of arising-and-ceasing), and his mastery truly "immovable." So the direct seeing of the forbearance is the cognitive bedrock of all the conduct of the first three parts: what "prajñā as guide" (P03 3.2) makes the five cross over by, at its height is just this direct seeing of the forbearance; the wisdom of unobtainable emptiness on which P01's "not-taking" rests, at its cognitive consummation is just this "contemplating all dharmas as neither arising nor ceasing."

A comparison: reciting the theory of swimming versus floating in the water oneself. A person who can recite the principle "water has buoyancy, the human body is less dense than water and so can float" (conceptual inference), and one who lies down in the water himself and is truly borne up by it and floats (direct realization), are two different things. The former "knows the principle of floating," the latter "directly realizes the fact of floating" — and only with the latter does his body truly "trust" the water's buoyancy, so that in the water he can relax and not thrash (immovable). The direct seeing of the forbearance is like the latter: not "knowing the principle that dharmas neither arise nor cease," but the mind's directly realizing the reality "all dharmas as the nature of empty space" — only with this direct realization does the bodhisattva's mind truly "accept and bear" reality, so that toward dharmas it can not grasp and not waver.

> ✅ Doctrinally sound: the content of the forbearance is the direct seeing of reality, "entering all dharmas as the nature of empty space · contemplating all dharmas as neither arising nor ceasing" (*Avataṃsaka*, *Śāstra*, 2.1, 2.2), the direct seeing of suchness by the root non-discriminating wisdom (subject and object both dissolving · apart from the discriminating grasping of mind, will, and consciousness, taking up P04's 3.2). This direct seeing is the cognitive bedrock of all the conduct of the first three parts (vow, measure, shape): the height of "prajñā as guide," the consummation of the wisdom of unobtainable emptiness on which "not-taking" rests, are both this direct seeing. Not conceptual inference, but direct realization.

### 3.2 Why "Forbearance," Why a Cognitive Signature: Accepting, Bearing, Being Convinced of Reality, Not Bearing Abuse

This layer establishes the sense of "forbearance," and why it is a *cognitive* signature. First it must be strictly distinguished from the patience of the six perfections (kṣānti). The "forbearance" of the perfection of patience (P03) is *conduct* — bearing beings' abuse, heat and cold and hunger and thirst, without raising anger or vengeance; its object is "adverse conditions," its function "bearing-without-retaliation." The "forbearance" of the forbearance of non-arising is *cognition* — "single-mindedly accepting and bearing the wisdom of true reality used by all the buddhas, which nothing can break or move" (2.2): its object is "reality" (the truth of dharmas' non-arising), its function "accepting · bearing · being convinced of" (accepting and abiding in this truth without wavering). The two share a Sanskrit root (kṣānti, with the manifold sense of "bearing · enduring · accepting"), but the one falls on *conduct* (bearing abuse), the other on *cognition* (abiding in reality).

Why must "accepting reality" use the word "forbearance," rather than "knowing" or "realizing"? Because the reality of "non-arising" runs *against* the cognitive habit of the ordinary mind — the ordinary mind is habituated to seeing dharmas as having arising and ceasing, as real and with self-nature; now it is said "all dharmas neither arise nor cease, are of the nature of empty space," and this runs counter to the mind's cognitive habit, so the mind, first hearing it, first contemplating it, finds it "hard to settle in" (as on hearing "the really-existent world I grasp is in fact unarisen," the mind instinctively resists). To be able, before this reality that runs counter to cognitive habit, to "abide without regress, accept without fright," is called "forbearance" — bearing this reality, abiding in this reality, being convinced of this reality. The *Śāstra*'s "the mind penetrating without obstruction, immovable and irreversible" (2.2) just describes this mark of acceptance: the mind penetrates reality without obstruction (no longer resisting), immovable and irreversible (abiding without regress). So "the forbearance of non-arising" is "the *forbearance* (acceptance and abiding) in the *dharma* (reality) of *non-arising*."

Precisely because its essence is "accepting and abiding in reality," it is the *cognitive* signature of the bodhisattva's mastery, not yet another piece of *conduct*. What it marks is not "the bodhisattva has done some further good deed," but "the bodhisattva's mind has undergone an irreversible settling in its view of reality" — when a mind truly "accepts and bears" "the non-arising of dharmas" and no longer resists, no longer wavers, that mind has cognitively entered the holy station. The *Śāstra* marks it "the first gate aiding the buddha-path" (2.2) — the first essential gate toward the buddha-path, from which all subsequent irreversibility and effortless flow (P07) issue, by this settling of cognition. Hence the forbearance's being a "cognitive signature": it is the unmistakable mark, at the cognitive level, of the bodhisattva's mastery — the mind accepting and bearing the unbreakable, unmovable reality, and so the mastery becoming immovable.

A comparison: one afraid of heights standing on a glass skywalk. A person afraid of heights, standing on the transparent glass skywalk at the outer edge of a tall building, "knows" rationally that the glass is firm and he will not fall (conceptual knowing), yet his body still instinctively trembles and dares not step — because the appearance "beneath my feet is a sheer abyss" runs counter to his cognitive habit, and his mind "cannot accept and bear" the reality that "it is in fact safe." Only when his mind truly "accepts and bears" the firmness of the glass (not merely knowing it rationally, but his whole body-and-mind abiding in it and no longer trembling) can he walk on the skywalk "the mind penetrating without obstruction, immovable and irreversible." The forbearance of non-arising is just like this accomplishment of "acceptance": not "knowing the principle that dharmas are unarisen," but the mind's wholly abiding in this reality and no longer resisting or wavering — once this abiding is accomplished, the mastery is immovable.

> ✅ Doctrinally sound: the "forbearance" of the forbearance of non-arising is the cognitive settledness of "accepting · bearing · being convinced of" reality ("single-mindedly accepting and bearing the wisdom of true reality nothing can break or move," 2.2), not the patience of the perfection of patience (the conduct of bearing abuse, P03); the two share a Sanskrit root yet one is conduct, one cognition. The word "forbearance" is used because the reality of "non-arising" runs counter to the ordinary mind's cognitive habit, and being able to abide without regress is therefore called forbearance. Precisely because its essence is "accepting and abiding in reality," it is the *cognitive* signature of the bodhisattva's mastery — the mind accepting reality, and so the mastery immovable; the *Śāstra* marks it "the first gate aiding the buddha-path."

### 3.3 Why the Signature of Entering the Grounds, and Why the Ground at Which It Is Established Is Unsettled

The last layer establishes why the forbearance is the signature of "entering the grounds," keeping the scope of its unsettled ground. "Entering the grounds" is the bodhisattva's entry into the holy station of directly seeing reality — the turn from "the causal stage's grasping of applied effort" into "the holy station of directly seeing reality," whose signature is the forbearance of non-arising. Why? Because the prior two layers have shown: the content of the forbearance is the direct seeing of reality (3.1), its essence the accepting and abiding in reality so that the mind is immovable (3.2); and "directly seeing reality · the mind accepting and abiding, immovable" is just the watershed dividing the "holy station" (the realized stations from the path of seeing upward) from the "ordinary stage / the causal stage of applied effort." The ordinary stage's mind can only conceptually infer reality, not directly see and accept it, so its mastery can move and regress; the holy station's mind directly sees and accepts reality, immovable, so its mastery is immovable and irreversible. The forbearance marks just this irreversible turn from "not-yet-directly-seen" to "directly-seen-and-accepted" — hence the cognitive signature of "entering the grounds" (entering the holy station).

Yet at what ground "entering the grounds" is set the canon disputes (2.3), and this paper does not adjudicate. The *Avataṃsaka* states "accomplishing this forbearance, one immediately enters the eighth, Immovable ground" (2.1) — tied to the eighth, Immovable ground established; the *Śāstra* ties it to "the irreversible stage" (2.3) without fixing a definite ground; the Yogācāra masters have a seventh/eighth difference. Why this divergence? Because the name "forbearance of non-arising" can denote its "first attainment" (at entering the first ground, the path of seeing, when the root non-discriminating wisdom first directly sees suchness and partially realizes non-arising) or its "ultimate establishment" (at the seventh/eighth ground, effortless and spontaneous, the forbearance perfected and irreversible) — first attainment and ultimate establishment differing in ground. This paper does not take sides for the author: it sets the *Avataṃsaka*'s eighth-ground establishment (the perfected content of the forbearance, 2.1) and the *Śāstra*'s irreversible-stage with its definition and contemplation (the essence and contemplation, 2.2, 2.3) side by side, the adjudication of the ground (entering the grounds / the path of seeing · the seventh · the eighth) being the author's.

To be stressed: this paper's "the forbearance of non-arising is the cognitive signature of entering the grounds" turns on "the function of the cognitive turn" (marking the entry from the causal stage of applied effort into the holy station of direct seeing, making the mastery immovable), not on "fixing which ground." Whether tied to the first ground (first attainment) or the seventh/eighth (ultimate establishment), the forbearance's being "the cognitive signature that makes mastery immovable" is one and the same; the disputed ground does not move this established function. And once this cognitive turn is accomplished (especially at the ultimate establishment of the seventh/eighth ground), the bodhisattva's mastery thereafter shows the marks of "effortless flow · irreversibility" — and this is what the next paper, P07, issues from.

> ⚠️ Scope (detailed in §V): the ultimate ground-assignment of the forbearance (entering the grounds / the path of seeing · the seventh ground · the eighth, Immovable ground established) is disputed in the canon (the *Avataṃsaka* ties it to the eighth, Immovable ground · the *Śāstra* to the irreversible stage without fixing a ground · the Yogācāra masters' seventh/eighth difference); this paper does not adjudicate or take sides, setting the *Avataṃsaka*'s content and the *Śāstra*'s essence-and-contemplation side by side, the ground being the author's adjudicated point of difference. The "cognitive signature" function this paper establishes does not move with the disputed ground.

Three layers together: the content of the forbearance is the direct seeing of all dharmas as the nature of empty space · neither arising nor ceasing (the direct seeing of the root non-discriminating wisdom, 3.1); its "forbearance" is the cognitive settledness of accepting and abiding in reality, hence a cognitive signature (not the patience of bearing abuse, 3.2); it is the signature of entering the holy station of direct seeing, its ground unsettled (3.3). Together they establish the thesis: **the forbearance of non-arising is the cognitive signature of the bodhisattva's mastery — directly seeing all dharmas as the nature of empty space, accepting the unbreakable, unmovable reality, turning the mastery from the grasping of applied effort into the mind penetrating without obstruction · immovable and irreversible; this cognitive turn is just what "effortless flow and irreversibility after the forbearance" issues from (its ultimate ground-assignment being the author's adjudicated point of difference).**

## IV. Interdisciplinary Convergence

(This paper deliberately omits the interdisciplinary section; the reason is given in §VI. Its axis is the Mahāyāna entering-the-grounds doctrine of "the forbearance of non-arising as the cognitive turn of directly seeing reality · making mastery immovable." Modern models — "cognitive restructuring," "paradigm shift," "the transformation of deep belief" — superficially resemble the forbearance's cognitive turn in "a root change at the cognitive level altering the stability of subsequent behaviour," but cannot sustain a three-layer convergence without lapsing into analogy on three points: "directly seeing all dharmas as the nature of empty space · neither arising nor ceasing (the root non-discriminating wisdom directly seeing suchness, not conceptual inference)," "'forbearance' as the receptive abiding in a reality that runs counter to cognitive habit (nothing breaking or moving it)," and "this turn marking entry into the holy station from the path of seeing upward." On the first especially, "directly seeing suchness" is a non-discriminating direct perception in which subject and object both dissolve, with no counterpart in any psychological model reducible to the reorganization of a conceptual framework; cognitive restructuring is still one conceptual framework replacing another (still within the discrimination of mind, will, and consciousness), only superficially resembling the direct seeing of "apart from all discriminating thought of mind, will, and consciousness, entering all dharmas as the nature of empty space." Hence omitted per the series' editorial convention, with this note.)

## V. Madhyamaka Corrections

This section clears three misreadings adjacent to the thesis, the second bearing on the unsettled-ground scope, the third on the middle-way sense of "neither arising nor ceasing," both requiring special honesty.

**❌ Misreading one: "the forbearance of non-arising = a higher grade of the perfection of patience (kṣānti) / the forbearance of non-arising is a deeper 'endurance.'"** This the paper broke at the outset, and restates here. The patience of the perfection of patience (kṣānti, P03) is *conduct* — bearing beings' abuse, heat and cold and hunger, without raising anger or vengeance; its object adverse conditions, its function bearing-without-retaliation. The "forbearance" of the forbearance of non-arising is *cognition* — single-mindedly accepting and bearing the unbreakable, unmovable wisdom of true reality (2.2); its object reality (the truth of dharmas' non-arising), its function accepting · bearing · being convinced of (accepting and abiding in this truth without wavering). The two share a Sanskrit root (kṣānti), but the one falls on conduct (bearing abuse), the other on cognition (abiding in reality). To read the forbearance of non-arising as "a deeper endurance" demotes a "cognitive signature" to a piece of "patience-conduct," losing its cognitive sense of "directly seeing reality · the mind immovable," and confusing the six perfections' patience with the entering-the-grounds forbearance.

**⚠️ Correction two: the ground at which the forbearance of non-arising is ultimately established (entering the grounds / the path of seeing · the seventh · the eighth, Immovable ground) is disputed in the canon; this paper does not adjudicate or take sides.** To be marked with care: this paper does not fix the forbearance's ground-number for the author. The *Avataṃsaka* ties it to "the eighth, Immovable ground" (accomplishing this forbearance, one enters it, 2.1); the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* ties it to "the irreversible stage" without fixing a definite ground (2.3); the Yogācāra masters have a seventh/eighth difference. The source of the divergence is that the name "forbearance of non-arising" can denote its first attainment (at entering the first ground, the path of seeing · the root non-discriminating wisdom first directly seeing suchness · partially realizing non-arising) or its ultimate establishment (at the seventh/eighth ground · effortless · the forbearance perfected) — first attainment and ultimate establishment differing in ground. This paper sets the *Avataṃsaka*'s eighth-ground establishment (the content, 2.1) and the *Śāstra*'s irreversible-stage with definition and contemplation (the essence and contemplation, 2.2, 2.3) side by side, **the adjudication of the ground being the author's**. The "cognitive signature" function this paper establishes (the cognitive turn that makes mastery immovable) does not move with the disputed ground; let the reader not take this paper's side-by-side setting for a fixing of some one ground. This scope is marked per the recorded source-gap.

**⚠️ Correction three: the contemplation of "neither arising nor ceasing" is no nihilistic emptiness; its four-fold negation refuses the grasping of annihilation just as it refuses the grasping of arising-and-ceasing (the middle way).** It must be made clear: the forbearance's "contemplating all dharmas as neither arising nor ceasing" (2.2) is by no means a nihilistic emptiness of "dharmas annihilated, void and nothing at all." The full text of its contemplation is "contemplating all dharmas as neither arising nor ceasing, neither not-arising nor not-ceasing, neither both nor not-both" (2.2) — "neither arising nor ceasing" negates the grasping of "real arising-and-ceasing," "neither not-arising nor not-ceasing" further negates the grasping that takes even this "neither arising nor ceasing" as real (which would fall into annihilation); such a four-fold negation refuses the grasping of annihilation just as it refuses the grasping of arising-and-ceasing, disclosing the middle-way reality apart from the two extremes (of the nature of empty space, not annihilated emptiness). The "like empty space" of "entering all dharmas as the nature of empty space" likens its being unobstructed and ungrasping, unarisen and unceasing, not its being "void of all things" in annihilation. To read the forbearance's "neither arising nor ceasing" as "dharmas annihilated and void" falls into the annihilation view (the *Avataṃsaka*'s "entering all dharmas as the nature of empty space" turned instead into "all dharmas are nothing," a vicious grasping of emptiness), losing the sense of middle-way reality; the direct seeing of the forbearance is a middle-way direct perception apart from arising-and-ceasing and apart from annihilation, not the stubborn void of annihilation.

## VI. Conclusion

**Restatement.** The forbearance of non-arising is the cognitive signature of the bodhisattva's mastery. In the canon, the *Avataṃsaka* gives its content as "apart from all discriminating thought of mind, will, and consciousness, grasping nothing, like empty space, entering all dharmas as having the nature of empty space," its accomplishment "immediately entering the eighth, Immovable ground"; the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* defines its essence by "the mind penetrating without obstruction, immovable and irreversible," gives its contemplation as "contemplating all dharmas as neither arising nor ceasing, neither not-arising nor not-ceasing," marks it "the first gate aiding the buddha-path," and ties it to "the irreversible stage." Doctrinally, three layers: its content is the direct seeing of all dharmas as the nature of empty space · neither arising nor ceasing (the direct seeing of the root non-discriminating wisdom, 3.1); its "forbearance" is the cognitive settledness of accepting and abiding in reality, hence a cognitive signature (not the patience of the perfection of patience, 3.2); it is the signature of entering the holy station of direct seeing, its ground unsettled (3.3). To be marked with care: the ground at which the forbearance is ultimately established is disputed in the canon, and this paper does not adjudicate (§V); "neither arising nor ceasing" is no nihilistic emptiness, but a middle-way direct perception apart from the two extremes (§V).

**Practice guidance.** Here the practitioner should distinguish one thing and seek one turn. To distinguish: do not mistake the forbearance of non-arising for "a deeper endurance" — it is not yet another piece of patience-conduct, but a root turn in the mind's view of "the reality of dharmas." Though you could bear all abuse (the perfection of patience perfected), if toward dharmas you still see real arising and ceasing, still raise grasping, then the forbearance of non-arising is not accomplished, and the cognitive root of your mastery not yet firm. To seek the turn: seek this cognitive turn of "directly seeing and accepting reality." Its point of entry is not "enduring more," but "contemplating all dharmas as neither arising nor ceasing, as the nature of empty space" — in daily use, contemplating the dharmas one meets (favorable and adverse, painful and pleasant) as all of the unarisen, unceasing nature of empty space, with no real self-nature to grasp, until the mind, before this reality, turns from "resisting, hard to settle" into "accepting, abiding" (as one afraid of heights, long standing on the glass skywalk, at last ceases to tremble). Yet keep to the middle way: this "neither arising nor ceasing" is no vicious grasping of emptiness of "dharmas annihilated, nothing at all," but the middle-way reality apart from arising-and-ceasing and apart from annihilation; if contemplated into a stubborn void of "all is nothing," it falls into annihilation and loses its sense. And remember: at what ground this cognitive turn is ultimately established (the seventh/eighth or entering the grounds) the masters differ; the practitioner has only to seek the true accomplishment of the turn, without first disputing the ground.

**Taking up and opening.** This paper takes up the first three parts: they established the bodhisattva's mastery's vow (P01), measure (P02), and shape (P03–P05); this paper establishes its cognitive root — the forbearance of non-arising. Without this cognitive bedrock of directly seeing reality, vow, measure, and shape are still within the grasping of arising-and-ceasing, their mastery shakable and reversible; only once this cognitive turn is accomplished is the mastery immovable and irreversible. Bound to P04: this paper's "the root non-discriminating wisdom directly seeing suchness" is just what P04's 3.2 bears on as "what the post-attainment wisdom is *post* to" (the post-realization non-discriminating wisdom being the post-attainment of this root wisdom) — P04 established the four added perfections as the deployment of post-attainment wisdom, this paper establishes the root wisdom it is post to (the forbearance's direct seeing of suchness). Opening forward: this paper establishes the cognitive turn "the forbearance makes mastery immovable"; the marks of mastery it shows "after the forbearance" — effortless flow (effort-free) and irreversibility (avaivartika) — are the dedicated treatment of the next paper, BODHI-P07; the forbearance's "immovable and irreversible" (cognition) becomes the "effortless flow · irreversibility" after the forbearance (the mark of mastery), which P07 unfolds. As for the Realization axis: this paper's forbearance of non-arising correlates thematically with the `bodhi-path` division's BODHI-P09, "The Immovable Eighth Ground" — the Realization axis walks the course of the immovable eighth ground (including the realization of the forbearance), the Attainment axis (this paper) reads the cognitive signature of that course (the forbearance making mastery immovable); the seam's doctrine is the author's, and this paper does not speak for the Realization axis.

**Open questions.** First, the paper cuts §IV (the forbearance and cognitive restructuring / paradigm shift are only superficially alike), as above. Second, the ultimate ground of the forbearance (entering the grounds / the path of seeing · the seventh/eighth established) is disputed in the canon, this paper not adjudicating, the ground being the author's; its correlation with the Yogācāra paths of seeing and cultivation awaits the author's separate adjudication. Third, the relation of "neither arising nor ceasing" as middle-way direct perception to the *Mūlamadhyamakakārikā*'s eight negations (neither arising nor ceasing, neither permanent nor annihilated, neither one nor many, neither coming nor going) — this paper establishes only that it is the middle way apart from the two extremes, without unfolding its full reconciliation with Nāgārjuna's Madhyamaka, awaiting another treatment. Fourth, the relation of the forbearance's direct seeing (the root non-discriminating wisdom) to its post-attainment (the post-realization non-discriminating wisdom · the deployment of the four added perfections, P04) — this paper establishes only that it is "the root it is post to" (P04 establishing its post-attainment), the full correlation of the two awaiting a synthetic treatment.
