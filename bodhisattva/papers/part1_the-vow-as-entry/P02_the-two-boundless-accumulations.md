# The Two Boundless Accumulations

**The Provisions of Merit and Wisdom · The Boundless Accumulation of Three Asaṃkhyeya Kalpas · Mastery Measured by Accumulation, Not by Subtraction**

*中文主題：二資糧無量——以積集量自在，非以斷結量自在*

## Abstract

The preceding paper, BODHI-P01, established that the bodhisattva's mastery does not succeed the arhat-fruit, and marked its measure as differing from the arhat's from the start — the arhat measures his mastery by *severance* (the exhaustion of taints, the ten fetters all cut), a subtraction; the bodhisattva measures his by *accumulation* (the two provisions, the six perfections), an addition. This paper takes up that "difference of measure" directly: by what is the bodhisattva's mastery measured? The thesis: the bodhisattva's mastery is mastery *by accumulation*, not by severance. What is accumulated is the two provisions — "the provision of merit (*puṇya-sambhāra*) and the provision of wisdom (*jñāna-sambhāra*)"; the amount accumulated is "boundless" (across three asaṃkhyeya kalpas, "to the boundless limit"); and the signature of the mastery lies in just this "uncircumscribed" boundless accumulation, set against the two vehicles' "measured and limited." Three layers. First, the two provisions are what the bodhisattva's mastery accumulates — merit and wisdom, gathered by one "long abiding in birth-and-death … as provisions" toward the Great Vehicle, the very essence the series plan marks for this division. Second, the amount that is "boundless" — an "asaṃkhyeya" is a number the reckoning of gods and humans cannot exhaust; the bodhisattva completes three asaṃkhyeya kalpas in turn, the treatise mapping five kinds of person onto them to complete the right practice of the ten grounds; the amount cannot in principle be bounded by a number. Third, mastery measured by accumulation — the bodhisattva's giving and wisdom are "uncircumscribed" (set to no fixed number), whereas the two vehicles are "measured and limited, for the deliverance of self alone"; so the bodhisattva's mastery is signed by the boundlessness of accumulation, the arhat's by the consummation of severance, the two measures reversed. To be marked honestly: a *paired* contrastive term "mastery-by-accumulation vs. fruit-by-severance" has no direct locus in the two designated sources; this paper reads them as the two measures of two masteries, the paired contrast being the author's adjudication. Conclusion: the bodhisattva measures by accumulation, the arhat by severance — the two reversed, yet both masteries real; this takes up P01's "difference of measure," the ultimate side-by-side adjudication reserved for P10.

**Keywords**: two provisions, provision of merit, provision of wisdom, accumulation, boundless, asaṃkhyeya, three asaṃkhyeya kalpas, right practice of the ten grounds, uncircumscribed, measured and limited, mastery by accumulation, severance-measure, *Mahāyāna-saṃgraha*, *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*

## I. The Live Question

To measure "arrival," we are used to asking "what has been torn down." The arhat's mastery is measured just so: by the ruler of severance — the three fetters cut, greed-and-hatred thinned, the five lower fetters ended, the taints exhausted and the ten fetters all cut; measure it down to "not one fetter left" and that is arrival (the negative freedom established in *The Mastery of the Arhat*, P01). The preceding paper, BODHI-P01, established that the bodhisattva's mastery does not succeed the arhat-fruit, and noted in passing one weighty clause: the bodhisattva's mastery is on another ruler from the start — measured not by severance but by accumulation. This paper sets that "other ruler" out for inspection: by what is the bodhisattva's mastery measured?

The question matters because, left uninspected, the bodhisattva's "accumulation" is easily misread as the mirror-negation of the arhat's subtraction — as if the bodhisattva merely "severs more, cultivates longer," measuring the same kind of accomplishment, only in greater quantity. But what the canon records the bodhisattva accumulating is not "more severance," but another kind of thing: the two provisions, of merit and of wisdom. The bodhisattva "long abides in birth-and-death" not to sever a few more fetters, but to "gather merit and wisdom as provisions"; the amount he accumulates is not "exhausted" (a finite terminus) but "the boundless limit" (an accumulation with no end-point). One measures "reduced to nothing" (the consummation of severance, finite); the other measures "accumulated without bound" (the breadth of accumulation, endless) — the rulers run in opposite directions, one's scale ending at "zero," the other's tending toward "boundless."

This paper's thesis: **the bodhisattva's mastery is measured by accumulation, not by severance.** What is accumulated is the two provisions, merit and wisdom; the amount is "boundless" (the accumulation of three asaṃkhyeya kalpas, to the boundless limit); and the signature of the mastery lies in just this "uncircumscribed" boundless accumulation — set against the two vehicles' "measured and limited, for the deliverance of self alone." This is the essence the series plan marks for this division: "the two boundless accumulations." The paper takes up the "difference of measure" opened by the preceding paper and makes it the bodhisattva's mastery's positive ruler.

The argument: §II presents the canon (the name and substance of the two provisions; the boundlessness of three asaṃkhyeyas; the uncircumscribed against the measured-and-limited); §III argues in three layers — the two provisions as what is accumulated (3.1), why "boundless" cannot be bounded by a number (3.2), mastery by accumulation against mastery by severance (3.3); §IV is cut; §V corrects the central misreadings and marks three points of textual scope; §VI concludes, takes up P01 and opens P10, and notes the thematic correlation with the Realization axis.

## II. Canonical Basis

This section presents three clusters from the two designated sources (the *Mahāyāna-saṃgraha*, Paramārtha's translation; the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*): the name and substance of the two provisions (2.1), the "boundlessness" of three asaṃkhyeyas (2.2), and the uncircumscribed against the measured-and-limited (2.3). All quotations are CBETA-verified.

### 2.1 The Provisions of Merit and Wisdom: What the Bodhisattva's Mastery Accumulates

The compound term and substance of "the two provisions" the *Mahāyāna-saṃgraha* sets out. Judging who can enter the contemplation of consciousness-only — one must, through much hearing perfumed into the stream, serving the buddhas, and the ripening of roots of good — thereby win the two provisions:

> The Mahāyāna perfuming of much hearing being continuous, having served immeasurable world-transcending buddhas, having entered the settled station of resolute faith, by the well-ripened cultivation and growth of roots of good, one therefore well wins the two provisions of merit and wisdom.[^1]

"The two provisions of merit and wisdom" — this is the settled compound for the two provisions. Their utmost amount, the *Mahāyāna-saṃgraha* marks at the chapter's close, quoting a verse of the *Mahāyāna-sūtrālaṃkāra*, as "the boundless limit":

> The bodhisattva grows merit and wisdom both, the two provisions to the boundless limit; because his mind is resolved in contemplating the Dharma, he can know the cause of the discrimination of meanings.[^2]

"The two provisions to the boundless limit" — this is the scriptural verse behind the series plan's "two boundless accumulations": the bodhisattva grows the two provisions of merit and wisdom to "the boundless limit" (a limit with no bound). And that the two provisions go with "long abiding in birth-and-death, gathering them as provisions," the *Mahāprajīāpāramitā-śāstra* shows in glossing "the great being (of great adornment)":

> This person, through immeasurable kalpas long abiding in birth-and-death, gathers merit and wisdom as provisions; among the three vehicles, for the sake of inclining to the Great Vehicle, he arouses the mind and practises the six perfections, and rides this Great Vehicle.[^3]

The bodhisattva, "through immeasurable kalpas long abiding in birth-and-death," does so to "gather merit and wisdom as provisions" — note that here the *Śāstra* reads "merit and wisdom … as provisions," a *split form*, while the *Mahāyāna-saṃgraha* reads "the two provisions of merit and wisdom," a compound (this difference of wording is treated in §V). The three together: what the bodhisattva's mastery accumulates is the two provisions of merit and wisdom; the accumulation goes with "long abiding in birth-and-death, inclining to the Great Vehicle, practising the six perfections"; and its amount is marked "the boundless limit."

### 2.2 Three Asaṃkhyeya Kalpas: Why the "Boundless" of the Accumulation Cannot Be Bounded by a Number

Why is the amount of the two provisions "boundless"? One sense is that an "asaṃkhyeya" is a number that cannot be exhausted. The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* defines "asaṃkhyeya" and lists the sequence of three asaṃkhyeyas:

> How long is called an "asaṃkhyeya"? Answer: that which one skilled in reckoning among gods and humans, reckoning to the utmost, can no longer know — this is called one asaṃkhyeya. … Reckon three asaṃkhyeyas thus: when the practice of one asaṃkhyeya is complete, one practises the second; the second complete, one practises the third.[^4]

An "asaṃkhyeya" is a number that one skilled in reckoning "reckoning to the utmost can no longer know" — that is, beyond what any reckoning can exhaust. The bodhisattva completes one, two, three asaṃkhyeyas in turn, and only past three asaṃkhyeya kalpas sows the causes of the thirty-two marks. The *Śāstra* further maps the bodhisattva's past lives onto the bounds of the three asaṃkhyeyas, marking the depth of each by "the mind's" degree of knowing it will become a buddha:

> In the first asaṃkhyeya, the mind does not itself know "will I become a buddha or not"; in the second, the mind can know "I will surely become a buddha," yet the mouth does not declare "I will become a buddha"; in the third, the mind clearly knows of itself it will become a buddha, and the mouth declares without fear: "In a future age I shall become a buddha."[^5]

And the assignment of the three asaṃkhyeyas to the completion of the right practice of the ten grounds, the *Mahāyāna-saṃgraha* makes by five kinds of person:

> In how long is the right practice of the ten grounds completed? There are five kinds of person who complete the practice in three asaṃkhyeya kalpas … the person of the ground of vowing-practice completes one asaṃkhyeya kalpa; the persons of pure-intention practice, of practice-with-marks, and of markless practice, through the sixth to the seventh ground, complete the second; thereafter the person of effortless practice, through to the tenth ground, completes the third.[^6]

Together: the accumulation of the two provisions has, for its temporal amount, three asaṃkhyeya kalpas, and "asaṃkhyeya" is by definition a number reckoning cannot exhaust; the *Mahāyāna-saṃgraha* further maps five kinds of person onto the three to complete the right practice of the ten grounds. So the amount of the two provisions cannot in principle be bounded by a fixed number — this is the first sense of "boundless": not "very many," but "not exhaustible by a number."

### 2.3 The Uncircumscribed Against the Measured-and-Limited: Why the "Boundless" of the Accumulation Becomes the Signature of Mastery

The "boundlessness" of the accumulation has a further, crucial sense: "uncircumscribed." Glossing the great being of "great adornment," the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* sets the two vehicles' "measured and limited" against the bodhisattva's "uncircumscribed":

> The śrāvaka and pratyekabuddha, though they practise the six matters of giving and so on, are measured and limited, for the deliverance of self alone … therefore they are not called "great adornment." What the bodhisattva delivers, he does not discriminate, does not circumscribe to a fixed number of beings, in giving and on through wisdom …[^7]

The two vehicles too practise the six matters of giving and so on, yet their practice is "measured and limited, for the deliverance of self alone" — set to a fixed bound (practised for one's own deliverance, the amount having its limit). The bodhisattva's giving and on through wisdom is "undiscriminating, not circumscribed to a fixed number of beings" — set to no bound of "for so-many beings," so his accumulation is boundless. The *Śāstra* also marks that the three asaṃkhyeya kalpas as stated in śrāvaka scriptures are a "measured and limited" number (a question-context, which the text below rebuts with the bodhisattva's immeasurable kalpas):

> The three asaṃkhyeya kalpas are measured and limited.[^8]

And the bodhisattva's two provisions are "to the boundless limit" (the verse of 2.1, here taken on its quantitative face):

> The bodhisattva grows merit and wisdom both, the two provisions to the boundless limit.[^9]

Together: the two vehicles' accumulation is "measured and limited, for the deliverance of self alone" (set to a bound); the bodhisattva's is "uncircumscribed," his provisions "to the boundless limit" (set to no bound). "Uncircumscribed" is the key — the bodhisattva's mastery is signed not by accumulating "more than the two vehicles" (that is still a comparison of quantities on one ruler), but by an accumulation that "sets no fixed bound" (does not count on that ruler at all). The two vehicles measure "the amount needed for self-deliverance" (limited); the bodhisattva's accumulation sets no "enough" at all (boundless). So the bodhisattva's mastery's being signed by accumulation turns on "the boundlessness of the uncircumscribed," not on "a larger measured quantity."

> A note: the *paired* contrast "mastery-by-accumulation vs. fruit-by-severance" has no direct paired locus in the two designated sources (T1509, T1593); the anchors above are the canonical pivot of the *accumulation* side ("accumulation / uncircumscribed / boundless"). The *severance* side (the subtractive measure of the arhat's mastery) is witnessed in *The Mastery of the Arhat* and the four-fruits division of the Realization axis; the paired contrast of the two measures and the adjudication of their mastery-sense are the author's (see §V).

## III. Core Argument

§II has shown the canon's express words: the two provisions as what is accumulated, the boundlessness of three asaṃkhyeyas, the uncircumscribed against the measured-and-limited. This section argues in three layers: why the two provisions are what the bodhisattva's mastery accumulates (3.1), why "boundless" cannot be bounded by a number and is not "very many" (3.2), and why mastery measured by accumulation runs opposite to mastery measured by severance (3.3).

### 3.1 The Two Provisions as What Is Accumulated: Merit and Wisdom, Not "More Severance"

What the bodhisattva's mastery accumulates is the two provisions of merit and wisdom (2.1), not "more severance." This must be settled first, or "accumulation" is misread as the same-kind extension of "severing more." The provision of merit is the merit won by giving, morality, patience, and so on; the provision of wisdom, the knowledge won by the contemplative wisdom of prajñā. The two accumulated together make the provisions of buddhahood (buddhahood requires both feet, merit and wisdom). What the arhat's mastery measures is "severance" (the exhausting of the afflictive obstruction); what the bodhisattva's mastery accumulates is "the completeness of merit and wisdom" — the latter is not the "scaled-up version" of the former but another kind of goal: the arhat measures "how much obstruction is gone," the bodhisattva measures "how fully the provisions are stocked."

Why does the bodhisattva measure "provision" rather than "severance"? Because his bearing is a buddha's omniscience (P01 established that what arising seeks is omniscience), and omniscience is not accomplished by the one matter of "exhausting the afflictions" — it requires the boundlessness of merit (accomplishing the marks and signs of the form-body, the lands, the power to embrace and convert beings) and the boundlessness of wisdom (accomplishing the omniscience of the dharma-body), both complete. So the bodhisattva "long abides in birth-and-death" not to sever more, but to "gather merit and wisdom as provisions" (the *Śāstra* of 2.1). This is why the bodhisattva's mastery's ruler is "accumulation," not "subtraction": the fruit it inclines to (omniscience, both feet of merit and wisdom) dictates that what is measured must be "the accumulation of provisions," not "the reduction of obstruction."

A comparison: demolishing an illegal structure, and raising a tower. The arhat-type subtraction is like clearing every illegal structure, clutter, and dangerous wall from a plot of land, down to "clean ground, with nothing left to trip on" — its accomplishment measured by "how cleanly it is cleared." The bodhisattva-type accumulation is like raising, on that ground, a tower that takes boundless materials and boundless time — its accomplishment measured by "how much material is stocked, how high the building stands." The "nothing left" of demolition and the "boundless materials" of building are simply two different things, two different measures. To read the bodhisattva's "accumulating the two provisions" as "severing more cleanly than the arhat" is just like saying "raising a tower" means "demolishing more thoroughly" — demolition and building run in opposite directions.

> ✅ Doctrinally sound: what the bodhisattva's mastery accumulates is the two provisions of merit and wisdom (*Mahāyāna-saṃgraha*, *Śāstra*, 2.1), not "more severance." Its measure is "the accumulation of provision," not "the reduction of obstruction," dictated by the fruit it inclines to (omniscience, both feet of merit and wisdom, P01). So "accumulation" is not the scaled-up version of the arhat's subtraction, but another kind of measure for another kind of goal.

### 3.2 The Amount That Is "Boundless": Not Exhaustible by a Number, Not "Very Many"

The bodhisattva's accumulation of the two provisions is marked "boundless," "to the boundless limit" (2.1, 2.3). This must be distinguished: "boundless" does not mean "a very large quantity," but "not exhaustible by a number." The ground is the definition of "asaṃkhyeya" — "that which one skilled in reckoning among gods and humans, reckoning to the utmost, can no longer know" (2.2): an asaṃkhyeya is a number "reckoning cannot exhaust," that is, not a large number but one beyond all reckoning. The bodhisattva completes three asaṃkhyeyas (2.2), and the *Mahāyāna-saṃgraha* further maps five kinds of person onto the three to complete the right practice of the ten grounds (2.2) — the very temporal amount of his accumulation lies in the order of "reckoning cannot exhaust."

Why must the bodhisattva's mastery be measured by a "not-bounded-by-number" boundless, rather than "a very large finite number"? Because its ruler is "uncircumscribed" (2.3): the bodhisattva's giving and on through wisdom is "undiscriminating, not circumscribed to a fixed number of beings" — he sets no bound of "enough once so-many beings are delivered, so-many provisions stocked." Once a bound is set (as the two vehicles' "measured and limited, for self-deliverance"), what is measured falls into the finitude of "stop once enough"; only with no bound set (uncircumscribed) is the accumulation truly "boundless." So the heart of "boundless" lies not in the largeness of the quantity, but in "setting no terminus of 'enough' at all." The arhat's mastery has its terminus (the taints exhausted is the consummation, nothing left to sever); the bodhisattva's mastery, on the accumulation side, has no terminus at all (beings are endless, and the accumulation of provisions is without bound) — this "no terminus" is precisely what "boundless" means.

A comparison: saving enough for retirement, and a lifetime of healing. A person saving for retirement has a number that is "enough" (reach it and one may stop, may retire) — this is measured and limited. One whose vocation is to heal and relieve the world has no number of "enough patients treated, then I stop" — not because he cannot count, but because "relieving the world" sets no bound of "enough." The bodhisattva's "boundless" two provisions are the latter: not that he has accumulated an astronomical figure, but that his accumulation has no terminus of "enough." To read "boundless" as "an extremely large finite number" is to drag it back onto the ruler of "measured and limited" — and that is the two vehicles' ruler, not the bodhisattva's.

> ✅ Doctrinally sound: "asaṃkhyeya" is a number reckoning cannot exhaust (*Śāstra*, 2.2); the accumulation of three asaṃkhyeyas lies in the order of "reckoning cannot exhaust"; the substance of its "boundless" is "uncircumscribed" (setting no terminus of "enough," 2.3), not "a very large quantity." The arhat's mastery has a terminus (the taints exhausted is the consummation); the bodhisattva's, on the accumulation side, has none — "no terminus" is the substance of "boundless."

### 3.3 Mastery by Accumulation, Not by Severance: The Two Rulers Reversed, Yet Both Masteries Real

The last layer states the thesis positively: the bodhisattva measures by accumulation, the arhat by severance — the two rulers reversed. The arhat's mastery's ruler measures "which fetters are severed" — the ten fetters all cut, the taints exhausted; measure down to "not one fetter left" and that is freedom (subtraction, the scale ending at "zero fetters"). The bodhisattva's mastery's ruler measures "how much of the two provisions is accumulated, and accumulated uncircumscribed" — merit and wisdom to the boundless limit; the measure lies in "a broad accumulation with no terminus" (addition, the scale tending toward "boundless"). Under the one name "mastery," one is signed by "the consummation of reduction," the other by "the boundlessness of accumulation"; under the one word "arrival," one takes "nothing left to trip on" for arrival, the other "provisions stocked toward buddhahood, delivering beings uncircumscribed."

Why are the two rulers reversed, yet both masteries real? Because the fruits they incline to are opposite, yet both real. The arhat inclines to "exit" (no-more-becoming, entering the remainderless); the freedom of "exit" is naturally measured by "bondage all gone" (severance) — bondage being what is to be removed, measuring "how much is removed" is its proper measure. The bodhisattva inclines to "not-exiting yet delivering beings" (P01's not-taking, non-abiding); the freedom of "non-abiding" is naturally measured by "the breadth of accumulated provision and vow" — declining the very realization of "remove-all-and-exit," he does not take "removal" for his measure but "the endless provision accumulated for delivering endless beings." So the bodhisattva accumulates "undiscriminating, uncircumscribed to a fixed number of beings" (2.3), and the boundlessness of his accumulation is the quantified aspect of his "not-taking, non-abiding, endlessly delivering" freedom. The arhat's mastery is signed by "being able to remove-all and removing all" (taking the realization); the bodhisattva's by "being able to remove-all yet not removing-all, abiding instead by an uncircumscribed accumulation in the delivering of beings" (not-taking, accumulation) — P01 established the difference of their *signatures* (taking / not-taking), this paper the difference of their *measures* (severance / accumulation), and the two are one thing's two faces: because he does not take the realization (does not make removal-all the aim), he measures not by severance but by accumulation.

> To be marked honestly (see also §V): the *paired* contrast "mastery-by-accumulation vs. fruit-by-severance" has no direct paired locus in the two designated sources. What this paper rests on is the canon of the accumulation side (uncircumscribed vs. measured-and-limited, 2.3), the difference of measure established by P01 and the Realization axis, and the subtractive severance-measure of the arhat's mastery established in *The Mastery of the Arhat*. The three read together as "the two measures of two masteries," the paired contrast being the author's adjudication.

A comparison (continuing 3.1): the demolisher's accomplishment is measured by "how clean the clearing" (ending in clean ground, finite); the builder's by "how much material, how many storeys" (tending toward boundless material, endless). Both are real accomplishments, yet one measures "removal," the other "accumulation"; the rulers run opposite without displacing each other — one does not measure a tower's height by "how clean the clearing," nor a clean plot's cleanness by "how much material." So with the two measures of the arhat's and the bodhisattva's masteries: the ruler of severance measures the arhat's (removal-all the consummation), the ruler of accumulation the bodhisattva's (boundlessness the breadth); the rulers reversed, both masteries real. Their ultimate side-by-side adjudication (the final verdict setting the two masteries against each other) belongs to the division's closing paper, P10; this paper establishes only the difference of measure.

> ✅ Doctrinally sound: the bodhisattva measures by accumulation (the two provisions of merit and wisdom to the boundless limit, uncircumscribed), the arhat by severance (the taints exhausted, the ten fetters all cut), the two rulers reversed. Their reversal is rooted in the opposition of the fruits they incline to (the bodhisattva not-exiting yet delivering / the arhat exiting into the remainderless), yet both are real. This takes up P01's "difference of measure," and is, with P01's "difference of signature" (not-taking / taking), one thing's two faces. The "accumulation vs. severance" paired contrast is the author's adjudicated reading (not a paired scripture, §V).

Three layers together: the two provisions (merit and wisdom) are what the bodhisattva's mastery accumulates, not "more severance" (3.1); the "boundless" of the accumulation lies in "not exhaustible, no terminus set," not "a very large quantity" (3.2); the bodhisattva measures by accumulation, the arhat by severance, the two rulers reversed yet both masteries real (3.3). Together they establish the thesis: **the bodhisattva's mastery is measured by accumulation, not by severance; what is accumulated is the two provisions of merit and wisdom, the amount an uncircumscribed boundlessness, the mastery signed by this boundless accumulation — set against the subtractive severance-measure of the arhat's mastery, the two measures reversed yet both masteries real.**

## IV. Interdisciplinary Convergence

(This paper deliberately omits the interdisciplinary section; the reason is given in §VI. Its axis is the Mahāyāna measure-doctrine of "mastery measured by the uncircumscribed boundless accumulation." Modern models — "growth mindset," "compound accumulation," "lifelong learning" — superficially resemble it in "measuring by accrual rather than removal," but cannot sustain a three-layer convergence without lapsing into mere analogy on three points: "the uncircumscribed boundless (setting no terminus of 'enough')," "accumulating toward omniscience, both feet of merit and wisdom," and "accumulation displaying, against severance, two measures of two masteries." On the first especially, the boundlessness is rooted in the vow of "not-taking, endlessly delivering beings" (3.3), with no counterpart in any model reducible to the accrual of a worldly goal; compound accumulation still aims at a finite goal, and only superficially resembles the bodhisattva's boundlessness of "setting no terminus of enough." Hence omitted per the series' editorial convention, with this note.)

## V. Madhyamaka Corrections

This section clears three misreadings adjacent to the thesis, the latter two bearing directly on textual scope and requiring special honesty.

**❌ Misreading one: "mastery measured by accumulation" = the worldly utilitarian "more is better," "the more accumulated the freer."** This must be cleared. The bodhisattva's accumulation of the two provisions is not a greed-driven hoarding — it is "undiscriminating, uncircumscribed to a fixed number of beings" (2.3), accumulated toward omniscience and for the deliverance of endless beings, not for getting and holding more for oneself. The heart of "boundless" is "setting no terminus of 'enough'" (3.2), and this "no bound" comes precisely from the vow of "not-taking, non-abiding, endlessly delivering" (3.3) — the opposite direction from the utilitarian accrual of "accumulating more for oneself," which is still the "measured and limited, for self-deliverance" of the two vehicles (2.3), or narrower still. So "mastery measured by accumulation" is by no means a numerical cult of "the more the freer"; the bodhisattva's mastery lies not in "accumulating much" but in "his accumulation being itself uncircumscribed (setting no bound for self)" — what is measured is the *quality* of "uncircumscribed," not the *quantity* of "more." To read it as utilitarian more-is-better is to fall back onto the measured-and-limited ruler of the two vehicles, even the worldly ruler, losing the point.

**⚠️ Correction two: the "mastery-by-accumulation vs. fruit-by-severance" paired contrast is the author's adjudicated reading, not a paired scripture.** To be marked honestly: in the two designated sources (the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*, the *Mahāyāna-saṃgraha*) there is no passage directly pairing "accumulation-measure (the bodhisattva's mastery)" with "severance-measure (the arhat's mastery)" as a set term. What this paper rests on is the canon of the accumulation side (uncircumscribed against measured-and-limited, the two provisions to the boundless limit, 2.3), the difference of measure established by P01 and the Realization axis, and the subtractive severance-measure of the arhat's mastery established in *The Mastery of the Arhat*; the three read together as "the two measures of two masteries reversed." This paired contrast is a synthesizing reading with doctrinal ground and adjudicated by the author, not a paired original text of any sūtra or śāstra. Where one sees "mastery-by-accumulation vs. fruit-by-severance" in quotation marks, know it for an interpretive pairing, not verbatim scripture. This scope is marked per the recorded source-gap.

**⚠️ Correction three: the "great" of "three great asaṃkhyeyas," the split-form "provision of merit / provision of wisdom," and the single verse taken on two faces.** First, the two designated sources (T1509, T1593) both read "three asaṃkhyeya kalpas," with no "great" of "three *great* asaṃkhyeyas"; "three great asaṃkhyeya kalpas" is a later common usage. This paper reproduces the original "three asaṃkhyeyas" faithfully, holding to the source even where it occasionally uses the common phrase. Second, the split-form compound "provision of merit / provision of wisdom" is not used by the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*, which reads only "merit and wisdom … as provisions" (split form, [^3] of 2.1); the compound "the two provisions of merit and wisdom" is found in the *Mahāyāna-saṃgraha* ([^1] of 2.1). This paper sets both forms side by side, not passing the compound off as the *Śāstra*'s original. Third, the verse of 2.1 ("the two provisions to the boundless limit") and the quantitative phrasing of 2.3 are the same quotation (T31n1593_p0124a15), taken on two faces — 2.1 on its "two provisions of merit and wisdom" naming-face, 2.3 on its "boundless limit" quantity-face; not a duplicated fabrication, marked here expressly.

## VI. Conclusion

**Restatement.** The bodhisattva's mastery is measured by accumulation, not by severance. In the canon, the *Mahāyāna-saṃgraha* sets out the compound "the two provisions of merit and wisdom" and quotes the verse "the two provisions to the boundless limit"; the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* ties the two provisions, through the bodhisattva's "long abiding in birth-and-death, gathering merit and wisdom as provisions," to inclining to the Great Vehicle and practising the six perfections, defines "asaṃkhyeya" as a number reckoning cannot exhaust, lists the sequence of three asaṃkhyeyas, and sets the two vehicles' "measured and limited, for self-deliverance" against the bodhisattva's "undiscriminating, uncircumscribed." Doctrinally, three layers: the two provisions (merit and wisdom) are what is accumulated, not "more severance" (3.1); the "boundless" lies in "not exhaustible, no terminus set," not "a very large quantity" (3.2); the bodhisattva measures by accumulation, the arhat by severance, the two rulers reversed yet both masteries real, rooted in the opposition of the fruits they incline to (not-exiting yet delivering / exiting into the remainderless, 3.3). To be marked honestly: the "accumulation vs. severance" paired contrast is the author's adjudicated reading, not a paired scripture; the "great" of "three great asaṃkhyeyas" and the split/compound forms of "provision of merit/wisdom" have their points of wording (§V).

**Practice guidance.** Here the practitioner should supplement the self-examination of "how much I have severed, how clean I have become" with another question: "the merit and wisdom I accumulate for others' benefit — have I set for myself a terminus of 'enough'?" The former question (the severance-measure) is real work, but its consummation is the arhat's mastery (remove-all and exit); the bodhisattva's mastery's measure lies in the latter question — its freedom is not in "accumulating much" (still a matter of quantity) but in "the accumulation being itself uncircumscribed for self." So accumulating the two provisions on the bodhisattva path is not a worldly "more is better" hoarding (which is rather a narrower "measured and limited"), but the boundlessness of "undiscriminating, uncircumscribed to a fixed number of beings." In giving, morality, and on through prajñā, the student should examine whether his accumulation still secretly sets a bound of "enough for me" — if so, he is still on the measured ruler; only by gradually loosening that bound, tending toward an uncircumscribed accumulation, does he accord with the bodhisattva's mastery's measure. Yet not to be misread: uncircumscribed is not "without gauging one's strength, without sequence"; the bodhisattva's boundless accumulation still proceeds by the sequence of three asaṃkhyeyas, by the right practice of the five kinds of person mapped to the grounds (2.2) — what is boundless is its limit, not its order.

**Opening forward and taking up the prior paper (with a thematic note).** This paper takes up the "difference of measure" opened by BODHI-P01: P01 established the bodhisattva's mastery's signature as differing from the arhat's (not-taking / taking), this paper its measure as differing (accumulation / severance), and the two are one thing's two faces — because he does not take the realization, he measures not by severance but by accumulation. Along the thread of this Part (Part 1 · The Vow as Entry), the later parts unfold the bodhisattva's mastery's faces in turn: the six and ten perfections as the operational form of these two provisions (P03–04), the four embraces as the freedom of altruism (P05), the forbearance of non-arising as the cognitive signature of entering the grounds (P06), closing with non-abiding nirvāṇa as the heart of "not-taking" (P09) and the ultimate adjudication of the two masteries (P10). As for the relation to the Realization axis: this paper's three asaṃkhyeyas and two provisions correlate thematically with the "three great asaṃkhyeya kalpas" framing of the fifty stages the `bodhi-path` division walks (its plan marking: the ten abodes/practices/dedications ≈ the first asaṃkhyeya; grounds 1–7 ≈ the second; grounds 8–10 ≈ the third) — the Realization axis walks the fifty stages' journey, the Attainment axis reads the boundless mastery of the two provisions that journey accrues; the seam's doctrine is the author's, and this paper does not speak for the Realization axis.

**Open questions.** First, the paper cuts §IV (mastery by accumulation and "compound accumulation / growth mindset" are only superficially alike), as above. Second, the "accumulation vs. severance" paired contrast is marked as the author's adjudicated reading, not a paired scripture; the final adjudication of the two measures' mastery-sense awaits the closing P10. Third, how "the uncircumscribed boundless" and the "sequence" of three asaṃkhyeyas stand together (boundless in limit yet ordered in practice) — this paper establishes only that "what is boundless is its limit, not its order" (practice guidance); its detailed correspondence with the ten grounds and the five-persons-mapped-to-grounds belongs to the Realization axis's ten-grounds papers and this division's later parts, and is not unfolded here. Fourth, the mutual scope, mutual inclusion, and non-partiality of the provision of merit and the provision of wisdom (both feet) — this paper establishes only the two provisions as what is accumulated; their several senses and correspondence with the six and ten perfections belong to P03–04, here only opened.
