# The Bodhisattva's Mastery Does Not Succeed the Arhat-Fruit

**Freedom Begins from the Vow · A Distinct Vehicle's Mastery, Not a Continuation of the Arhat's**

*中文主題：菩薩自在不繼阿羅漢果——自在從願起，不以果為其階*

## Abstract

This paper opens *The Bodhisattva's Mastery* and is the *seam paper* between the Attainment axis and the Realization axis: it reads, two-facedly, the same body of canon on the independence of bodhicitta that the Realization axis's BODHI-P01, "Not a Continuation of Arhatship," reads. The Realization axis asks how the bodhisattva *path* forks from arhatship at the first arising of the mind (the independence of the *road*); this paper asks how the bodhisattva's *mastery* stands without succeeding the arhat's (the independence of the *fruit*). The thesis: the bodhisattva's mastery is not an upgrade of the arhat's, nor a higher rung of one ladder, but a *distinct vehicle's freedom* — of another lineage, by another measure, forked from the first arising of bodhicitta. Three layers establish it. First, freedom begins from the vow: the bodhisattva's first arising of the mind has the "first right vow" for its nature, and once it arises he is "counted among the bodhisattvas of the Great Vehicle," so the standing on which his mastery rests is conferred at the arising of the mind, not at any prior fruition. Second, its signature is *not-taking-realization*: "fully contemplating yet not realizing," "practising the real-limit yet not realizing it," "long due for nirvāṇa yet not taking its realization." Where the arhat's mastery is signed by *taking* the realization (the exhaustion of taints, no-more-becoming), the bodhisattva's is signed by *being free to not take* the realization that was his to take — not higher, but other in kind (the hidden vein of non-abiding nirvāṇa, fully treated in P09; here only opened). Third, two distinct lineages and measures, not one ladder: the bodhisattva-lineage "surpasses all śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas" and realizes the purity of the cognitive obstruction as well; the merit of the first arising of the mind surpasses that of "teaching beings to win all four fruits and the pratyekabuddha-way." Conclusion: the Attainment axis does not take the arhat's mastery as a prior stage of the bodhisattva's; the bodhisattva's mastery rises from the vow, is measured by accumulation (→ P02), and is signed by not-taking — a distinct vehicle's freedom that does not succeed the arhat-fruit. This paper sets out from the vow and frames the whole division.

**Keywords**: bodhisattva's mastery, arhat's mastery, arising of bodhicitta, first right vow, counted among the bodhisattvas, two parallel tracks, not-taking-realization, not-realizing, bodhisattva-lineage, purity of the cognitive obstruction, merit of the first arising of the mind, a distinct vehicle's freedom, seam, non-abiding nirvāṇa

## I. The Live Question

There is a widespread and tempting picture of the freedom that comes with arrival: that the bodhisattva's mastery is a second storey built atop the arhat's. As if the arhat had won one freedom (the unobstructedness signed by the exhaustion of taints — which the companion volume, *The Mastery of the Arhat*, opens by establishing as a "negative freedom," a freedom-*from*), and the bodhisattva simply accumulates more of it and travels farther, gaining "a larger freedom of the same kind." On this picture "first arhat, then bodhisattva" is not merely a sequence of paths but a sequence of *freedoms*: one ruler, the arhat reaching one mark, the bodhisattva a higher mark on the same scale.

But take that ruler to the canon and the first measurement fails. The arhat's mastery is signed by *taking* the realization — the exhaustion of taints, no-more-becoming, the final cessation. Yet the scriptures record the bodhisattva *not taking* the very realization that was his to take: "fully contemplating yet not realizing," "practising the real-limit yet not realizing it," "long due for nirvāṇa yet not taking its realization." At the same place where a realization could be taken, the arhat takes it and is thereby free, the bodhisattva declines it and is thereby free. One freedom signed by *taking*, one by *not-taking* — how could these be two marks, high and low, on one ruler? It is not one ruler. Hence the opening question of the second division of the Attainment axis: is the bodhisattva's mastery a higher grade of the arhat's, or a freedom of another kind entirely, divergent in kind from the very first arising of the mind?

This paper's answer collides head-on with the default of "a sequence of freedoms": **the bodhisattva's mastery does not succeed the arhat-fruit.** It is not a cultivated upgrade of the arhat's freedom, but a distinct vehicle's freedom, forked from the first arising of bodhicitta — of another lineage, by another measure (accumulation, not subtraction), signed by *not-taking* rather than *taking*. The standing on which the bodhisattva's mastery rests (his very status *as* a bodhisattva) does not wait upon the four fruits for its foundation; it is constituted at the single thought of arousing bodhicitta. Hence *freedom begins from the vow* — it rises from the vow, not as a sequel to a fruit. One who has severed not a single fetter yet has aroused the great mind, the canon counts among the bodhisattvas, and his mastery has thereupon a ground to rest on; one whose four fruits are complete yet who has not aroused the great mind, the canon does not, on account of his high fruit, automatically grant the bodhisattva's mastery. The signature is in the mind, in the vow — not in the fruit.

Here the paper seams to its twin. As the seam paper it reads, in common with the Realization axis's BODHI-P01, "Not a Continuation of Arhatship," *the same body of canon on the independence of bodhicitta*, two-facedly: the Realization axis reads the *independence of the road* (how the bodhisattva path forks from arhatship at the arising of the mind), the Attainment axis the *independence of the fruit* (how the bodhisattva's mastery stands without succeeding the arhat's). The two axes are one at the arising of the mind, and divide at the walking (Realization) and the arrival (Attainment).

The thesis: the bodhisattva's mastery does not succeed the arhat-fruit; it rises from the vow and stands as a distinct vehicle. The argument: §II presents the canon, read on its mastery-face (the vow as the ground of mastery; not-taking as the signature of divergence; lineage and the merit of first arising as surpassing from the start); §III argues in three layers — freedom begins from the vow (3.1), not-taking-realization as the signature (3.2), two distinct lineages and measures, not one ladder (3.3); §IV is cut; §V corrects the central misreadings and marks two points of textual scope; §VI concludes, opens the division, and marks the seam.

## II. Canonical Basis

This section reads, in common with Realization-axis BODHI-P01, the same body of canon (the *Yogācārabhūmi* chapters on Arising-of-the-Mind and on Lineage, the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*, the *Avataṃsaka*), but takes its *mastery*-face: the vow as the ground of mastery (2.1), not-taking as the signature dividing the two masteries (2.2), and lineage and the merit of first arising as surpassing from the start (2.3). All quotations are CBETA-verified.

### 2.1 Freedom Begins from the Vow: Arising-of-the-Mind, Counted Among the Bodhisattvas

The ground of the bodhisattva's mastery is his standing *as* a bodhisattva; and the *Yogācārabhūmi*, Bodhisattva-ground, "Chapter on the Arising of the Mind," ties that standing directly to the arising of the mind:

> The bodhisattva's first arising of the mind — among all the bodhisattva's right vows, it is the first right vow, universally embracing the rest; therefore the arising of the mind has the first right vow for its nature. … Once the bodhisattva has aroused the mind, he is already said to have entered unsurpassed awakening and to be counted among the bodhisattvas of the Great Vehicle. … And only having aroused the mind can he gradually and swiftly realize unsurpassed awakening, not without arousing it; therefore the arising of the mind is the root of unsurpassed awakening.[^1]

Read on its mastery-face: what "entering," "counted among," and "root" attach to is the arising of the mind, not a fruition. Before there can be a bodhisattva's *mastery* there must be a *bodhisattva*; and that status — "counted among the bodhisattvas" — is constituted at the arising of the mind, with no need for the four fruits underneath. So the *ground* of the bodhisattva's mastery rises from the vow. Unlike the arhat's mastery, which must await the exhaustion of taints (the last cut settling) before it has anything to sign, the bodhisattva's mastery has a ground from the first vow; the three-asaṃkhyeya accumulation that follows (→ P02) fills out *the freedom this vow opens*, rather than seeking a separate freedom to set atop the four fruits. The mark of arising, in the *Avataṃsaka*'s "Ten Abodes," is concrete: seeing a buddha's majesty, hearing the vast Dharma, or seeing beings' acute suffering, the bodhisattva "arouses the mind of awakening and seeks omniscience"[^2] — what is sought is a buddha's omniscience (the ultimate bearing of mastery), not one's own escape.

The motive and the fruit-direction of arising, the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* marks in one stroke:

> Compassion is the root of the buddha-path. … The bodhisattva, seeing beings tormented by all these sufferings, gives rise to great kindness and compassion, and only then arouses the mind seeking unsurpassed perfect awakening. By the power of great compassion, too, through immeasurable asaṃkhyeya lifetimes of birth-and-death his mind does not sink in weariness; by the power of great compassion, though long due for nirvāṇa he does not take its realization.[^3]

"Long due for nirvāṇa yet not taking its realization" — this clause is the pivot of §3.2 and the first watershed dividing the bodhisattva's mastery from the arhat's. For now: the motive of arising is great compassion; its fruit-direction is *not-taking* — and not-taking is precisely the signature the next section unfolds.

### 2.2 Not Succeeding the Arhat-Fruit: Passing the Two Vehicles' Grounds, Not-Taking, Not-Realizing

The "not-succeeding" of the bodhisattva's mastery rests most directly on the cluster of "not-taking" clauses. *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*, fascicle 23:

> Question: If the bodhisattva is replete in the thirty-seven factors, how does he not enter the śrāvaka's dharma-position? Answer: "Replete" means *fully contemplating yet not realizing*; contemplating clearly and fully, hence "replete." … "Not forsaking beings, joined with the wisdom of unobtainable emptiness, he does not fall to the śrāvaka ground."[^4]

On one and the same field of contemplative wisdom, the śrāvaka uses it to take realization, enters the śrāvaka position, and wins his freedom (the freedom of the exhaustion of taints); the bodhisattva contemplates clearly yet does not take realization, and by not forsaking beings and the wisdom of unobtainable emptiness does not fall to the śrāvaka ground. So the bodhisattva's mastery is not "walking through the arhat's freedom and then continuing upward"; it holds its vow and does not take, at the very place a realization could be taken — and its *signature* thereby already differs. Fascicle 18 ties this difference to the first arising of the mind: the bodhisattva "passes beyond the grounds of śrāvaka and pratyekabuddha and enters the bodhisattva position," whereas the arhat and pratyekabuddha "at their first arising of the mind had no great vow, no great compassion … but sought only to escape the suffering of aging-sickness-death," while the bodhisattva "from the first arising of the mind makes great vows, has great compassion, seeks all merits … seeks the true character of dharmas."[^5] "Passing … entering" is passing-beyond-and-entering, not summiting-and-continuing; the dividing cut falls at the first arising of the mind.

The *Avataṃsaka*'s "Ten Grounds," at the seventh Far-Reaching Ground, puts this "surpassing from the start, not later" most vividly, in the simile of the prince:

> Like a prince, born in the royal house, born of the queen, replete with the royal marks: from birth he surpasses the whole body of ministers — but by the king's power, not by his own. … So too the bodhisattva: at the first arising of the mind, by aspiring to the great Dharma, he surpasses all śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas … Therefore the bodhisattva, though he practises the real-limit, does not realize it.[^6]

The prince surpasses the ministers by lineage and royal blood (the king's power), not by later-accrued merit (his own power); and a minister, however able, is not "upgraded" into a prince by high achievement. So too the bodhisattva's mastery: its surpassing of the two vehicles is a status-surpassing, possessed from the start "at the first arising of the mind," not a coming-from-behind after the arhat-fruit is filled out. The closing clause, "though he practises the real-limit he does not realize it," is of one tenor with "long due for nirvāṇa yet not taking" (2.1) and "fully contemplating yet not realizing" (2.2): the bodhisattva's mastery is signed by *approaching-the-realization-yet-not-taking-it*. The arhat's mastery is signed by *taking* the realization (the exhaustion of taints, entering the remainderless); the bodhisattva's by *not-taking* (holding the vow, not entering) — not a high-and-low on one ruler, but the difference of two rulers, two kinds. (The deep sense of "not-taking / non-abiding" *is* non-abiding nirvāṇa, treated in full in P09; here only its vein is opened.)

### 2.3 Two Distinct Lineages, the Merit of First Arising Surpassing: Other in Kind, Not an Upgrade

The "not-succeeding" of the bodhisattva's mastery has a deeper root still — lineage (*gotra*). *Yogācārabhūmi*, "Chapter on Lineage":

> What is lineage? In brief, two kinds: the naturally-abiding lineage and the developed lineage. … A bodhisattva who has accomplished the lineage surpasses all śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas … know that the lineage is supreme and unsurpassed. … The lineages of all śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas can realize only the purity of the afflictive obstruction, not the purity of the cognitive obstruction; the bodhisattva-lineage can realize the purity of the afflictive obstruction *and* the purity of the cognitive obstruction.[^7]

The closing distinction is the crucial one: the two-vehicle lineage realizes only the purity of the afflictive obstruction (its freedom stops at severing the afflictions); the bodhisattva-lineage realizes both the afflictive and the cognitive obstruction (its freedom severs the cognitive obstruction too). What is severed differing, and the purity realized differing, the freedom that results is *other in kind from the lineage-level up*: the bodhisattva's mastery is not the arhat's "severing more" (more of the same category) but an other-kind accomplishment that *also* severs an obstruction outside the arhat's mastery's category (the cognitive obstruction). The categories differing, where is the high-and-low of one ladder? The "Chapter on the Arising of the Mind" lists the merit of arising: the right vow of the first arising "is the first and unsurpassed among all right vows seeking the worldly and *world-transcending* good"[^8] — even the śrāvaka's world-transcending vow stands beneath the vow of bodhicitta. Most telling, the *Avataṃsaka*'s "Merit of the First Arising of the Mind" sets that merit against "the merit of teaching beings to win the four fruits and the pratyekabuddha-way":

> This person's merit, compared to the bodhisattva's merit of the first arising of the mind, is not a hundredth, not a thousandth … not even an upaniṣad-fraction. Why? … all buddhas, at the first arising of the mind, aroused the mind of awakening not only to … teach beings to win the stream-enterer's fruit … the arhat's fruit, the pratyekabuddha-way, but so that the lineage of the Tathāgata not be cut off.[^9]

The merit of "teaching beings to win all four fruits" is not a hundred-thousandth of the bodhisattva's merit of *first arising*. Were the bodhisattva's mastery truly a sequel to the arhat's, the first arising would be at most "the preface to the sequel" — how could it surpass the consummate merit of the whole "main text" (the four fruits complete)? The only coherent reading: the first arising belongs from the start to another order of magnitude, a vehicle of another lineage — its mastery surpasses the four fruits not by travelling farther than they, but by never being on their ruler at all. "So that the lineage of the Tathāgata not be cut off" marks the independent bearing of the bodhisattva's mastery: not stopping at relieving beings' suffering to the four fruits (which the śrāvaka path can accomplish), but at the continuance of the buddhas.

## III. Core Argument

§II has shown the canon's express words: the vow as the ground of mastery, not-taking as the signature of divergence, lineage and the merit of first arising surpassing from the start. This section presses the deeper question: in which three things does the bodhisattva's mastery's independence consist, such that it cannot be a higher grade of the arhat's? Three layers.

### 3.1 Freedom Begins from the Vow: the Ground Rests on Arising, Not on a Fruit

The ground of the arhat's mastery is an unconditioned subtractive accomplishment — the exhaustion of taints (the unconditioned cessation through discrimination). Until the taints are exhausted the arhat's mastery has nothing to sign; once they are, mastery co-arises with it (the "co-arising" established in *The Mastery of the Arhat*, P01). Its ground lies where the last cut settles. The bodhisattva's mastery is otherwise: its ground is the standing *as* a bodhisattva, and that standing is constituted "once he has aroused the mind … counted among the bodhisattvas" (2.1). The grounds differing — one at a fruit (exhaustion), one at a vow (arising) — the bodhisattva's mastery cannot be the arhat's continued: to continue is to accumulate further on one ground, but the two grounds are not even of the same rank or kind.

This is what "freedom begins from the vow" means. It does not say "arising = consummate freedom" (the filling-out of the bodhisattva's mastery takes three asaṃkhyeya kalpas of accumulation, treated in P02); it says the bodhisattva's mastery *has a ground, has a rising,* from the single thought of arising: the vow once made, the lineage of the mastery, its bearing (omniscience), and its motive (great compassion) are laid down, and all subsequent accumulation is the unfolding of *the freedom this vow opens*, not a separate thing set atop the four fruits.

A homely comparison. A person's *civic freedom* (the rights and liberties enjoyed as a citizen) is not won by first completing some years as a "resident" and being upgraded; it rests on the conferral of a status — naturalization. Citizenship granted, the rights are had on its basis, and the settled life that follows is that status unfolding, not a different status exchanged for it. The bodhisattva's mastery resting on arising is just so: not "a resident promoted to citizen," but enrollment *is* citizenship. To read the bodhisattva's mastery as "the arhat promoted upon completion" is like saying "one must complete a residency term before one can be naturalized into another country" — mistaking the conferral of a status for the accrual of seniority within one status.

> ✅ Doctrinally sound: "once he has aroused the mind, he is counted among the bodhisattvas of the Great Vehicle" (*Yogācārabhūmi*, Arising-of-the-Mind) positively defines the constitution of the bodhisattva's standing as resting on arising. The bodhisattva's mastery takes this standing for its ground; hence its ground rises from the vow, not from the four fruits. The ground differing in rank and kind from the arhat's mastery (which rests on the exhaustion of taints), the bodhisattva's mastery is not a higher grade of it.

### 3.2 Not-Taking-Realization as the Signature: Free to Not Take the Realization That Was His to Take

The second layer is the deepest cut of the divergence. Granting that the grounds differ, might the bodhisattva's mastery still be "the same freedom by another entrance"? No. The canon thrice seals this off in one and the same grammar: "long due for nirvāṇa yet not taking its realization" (Śāstra, 2.1), "fully contemplating yet not realizing" (Śāstra, 2.2), "practising the real-limit yet not realizing it" (Avataṃsaka, 2.2). The shared structure: *arriving at the place a realization could be taken, and not taking it.*

This not-taking is the very opposite of the two masteries' signatures. The arhat's mastery is signed by *taking* — taking the realization of nirvāṇa, the exhaustion of taints, no-more-becoming; its freedom is a "negative freedom" (freedom-*from*: burdens laid down, fetters ended, no taint left to bind, per *The Mastery of the Arhat*, P01), with "taking that final cessation" for the seal of its unobstructedness. The bodhisattva's mastery is signed by *not-taking* — though fully contemplating, though practising the real-limit, though long due for nirvāṇa, it holds the vow and does not take, by the power of great compassion and great vow, "so that through immeasurable asaṃkhyeya lifetimes his mind does not sink in weariness," to go on delivering beings and accumulating. Its freedom is not signed by "taking the final cessation," but by "*being able to not take* the cessation that was his to take" — a freedom of "being able not to enter," not a freedom of "having exited."

Two freedoms, opposite in direction. The arhat's mastery asks, "Am I now able to leave?" — able to leave *is* freedom (freedom-from birth-and-death). The bodhisattva's mastery asks, "Can I *choose not to leave* and not be bound by birth-and-death?" — the harder freedom, of being able-not-to-leave (free even to stay). For the former, freedom lies in "being able to get out"; for the latter, in "being able to get out yet choosing to stay, and staying unbound."

A comparison. Two people have alike cleared their debts and are alike "now able to retire and exit" (alike "able to take the realization"). One retires at once; his freedom is "debt-free, no longer toiling for anyone" — the arhat-type mastery (the freedom of taking-the-realization-and-exiting). The other, though able to retire, chooses not to, continuing to run the enterprise to support more people — and what makes him *free* rather than *trapped* is precisely that he is **able to retire and chooses not to** (not unable, not compelled), and runs it unburdened. The bodhisattva's mastery is the latter: freedom lies not in "being able to escape suffering," but in "being able to not take the escape that was his, and not-taking without sinking in weariness." The deep sense of this "not-taking / non-abiding" *is* non-abiding nirvāṇa (abiding neither in birth-and-death nor in nirvāṇa) — treated in full in P09; here it is established only as the *signature* of the bodhisattva's mastery, against the arhat's mastery's taking-signature.

> ✅ Doctrinally sound: the three "not-taking / not-realizing" clauses (two in the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*, one in the *Avataṃsaka*'s Ten Grounds) show consistently that the bodhisattva's mastery is signed by "approaching the realization yet not taking it" (able to not take what was his to take), the very opposite of the arhat's mastery's "taking" signature (exhaustion of taints, no-more-becoming). Not two grades of one freedom, but two signatures of two freedoms other in kind. This not-taking is the hidden vein of non-abiding nirvāṇa (→ P09).

### 3.3 Two Distinct Lineages, Two Distinct Measures, Not One Ladder

The third layer returns to the depths — vehicle-nature and measure. The picture of "bodhisattva's mastery = the arhat's upgraded" assumes at bottom that the two are of one lineage and one measure, differing only in degree. The canon denies this in two places.

First, lineage other in kind (2.3). The two-vehicle lineage realizes only the purity of the afflictive obstruction; the bodhisattva-lineage realizes the purity of the cognitive obstruction as well. What the arhat's mastery severs is the afflictive obstruction (the delusion that binds to birth-and-death); the bodhisattva's mastery *also* severs the cognitive obstruction (the delusion that obstructs omniscience) — which lies wholly outside the arhat's mastery's category. So the bodhisattva's mastery is not "the arhat's mastery severing more" (more in one category), but "an accomplishment that *also* severs an obstruction outside the arhat's mastery's category." The categories differing, whence a high-and-low on one ladder? The prince-simile (2.2) puts it most sharply: the prince surpasses the ministers by lineage, not by accrued achievement; the bodhisattva's mastery "surpasses all śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas at the first arising of the mind" — a status-surpassing, possessed from the start, not a later promotion.

Second, measure other in kind. The arhat's mastery is measured by *severance* — measuring "which fetters are severed" (the ten fetters all cut, the taints exhausted); it is subtraction. The bodhisattva's mastery is measured by *accumulation* — measuring "how much merit and wisdom, the two provisions, are built up, and for whose sake one does not take the realization that was one's to take"; it is addition (this is precisely the theme of P02: mastery measured by accumulation, not by subtraction). The measuring-rulers being reversed (one subtracts, one adds), the masteries that result are not a high-and-low of one thing, but two masteries by two measures.

So the "Merit of the First Arising" — "the merit of teaching beings to win the four fruits, compared to the bodhisattva's merit of first arising, is not a hundred-thousandth" (2.3) — becomes intelligible: were they of one ruler and one lineage, first arising could not possibly surpass the consummate four fruits; only because the bodhisattva's mastery is from the start on another ruler (accumulation) and of another lineage (severing the cognitive obstruction too) can its first arising fall outside the four fruits' order of magnitude.

> ✅ Doctrinally sound: the twofold lineage and the judgment that the bodhisattva-lineage realizes the purity of the cognitive obstruction (*Yogācārabhūmi*, Lineage) establish at the vehicle-nature level that the bodhisattva's mastery is of a different category from the arhat's (severing the cognitive obstruction, not more of one category); and in measure the bodhisattva accumulates where the arhat subtracts (→ P02), the two rulers reversed. So the two masteries are not a high-and-low of one ladder, but two distinct vehicles' freedoms by two lineages and two measures.

Three layers together: mastery rests on the vow (rising from the vow, not from a fruit, 3.1); is signed by not-taking (able to not take what was his, the opposite of the arhat's taking, 3.2); and is other in kind from the start in lineage and measure (severing the cognitive obstruction, measured by accumulation, not an upgrade, 3.3). Together they establish the thesis: **the bodhisattva's mastery does not succeed the arhat-fruit; it rises from the vow and stands as a distinct vehicle, measured by accumulation and signed by not-taking, not a higher grade or sequel of the arhat's mastery.**

## IV. Interdisciplinary Convergence

(This paper deliberately omits the interdisciplinary section; the reason is given in §VI. The independence of the bodhisattva's mastery turns on two concepts — the otherness-in-kind of lineage/vehicle-nature, and the vow of not-taking. The modern "vocation / calling" model does resemble "freedom begins from the vow, not seniority-accrual" (used as a homely figure in 3.1), but has no counterpart to "not-taking / non-abiding nirvāṇa" or "severing the cognitive obstruction"; and the freedom of "being able to not take the realization that was one's to take" only superficially resembles "delayed gratification" or "altruistic motivation" — the latter still presuppose a bound subject awaiting satisfaction, whereas the former, with that satisfaction (nirvāṇa) genuinely takeable, declines it by vow and declines it without weariness. To force the convergence would falsify; better omitted, to be treated in a later paper should a genuinely convergent deep structure appear.)

## V. Madhyamaka Corrections

This section clears three misreadings adjacent to the thesis, two of which bear directly on textual scope and require special honesty.

**❌ Misreading one: "the bodhisattva's mastery = the arhat's mastery upgraded (a higher rung of one ladder)."** This the paper breaks in full. The arhat's mastery is signed by taking (exhaustion of taints, subtraction), measured by severance, stopping at the afflictive obstruction; the bodhisattva's is signed by not-taking (holding the vow, not entering), measured by accumulation, severing the cognitive obstruction too (3.2, 3.3) — signatures reversed, rulers reversed, the severed obstructions of different categories. Not two grades on one ruler, but two distinct vehicles' freedoms by two lineages and two measures. To read the bodhisattva's mastery as the arhat's upgrade erases the two things outside the arhat's mastery's category — "not-taking" and "severing the cognitive obstruction" — and contradicts the prince-simile's plain "surpassing from the start, not later-won." Further: the question of "turning from the lesser to the greater" (becoming an arhat first, then converting to the Mahāyāna) is not this paper's subject — by the division's scope it presents the bodhisattva's mastery on Mahāyāna terms and does not enter that debate (its adjudication is the author's; the paper does not presume it).

**⚠️ Correction two: "does not succeed the arhat-fruit" is not a verbatim scriptural proposition, but a reading the synonymous clauses jointly display, adjudicated by the author.** To be marked honestly: across the sources used here, no literal negative proposition "the bodhisattva's mastery does not succeed the arhat-fruit" is found. The canon expresses the sense with a cluster of functionally equivalent clauses — "passing the grounds of śrāvaka and pratyekabuddha and entering the bodhisattva position," "not entering the śrāvaka dharma-position," "not falling to the śrāvaka ground," "fully contemplating yet not realizing," "practising the real-limit yet not realizing it," "surpassing all śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas at the first arising of the mind." This paper reads that cluster as "the mastery does not succeed, but stands as a distinct vehicle" — a synthesizing reading with ample doctrinal support and adjudicated by the author, not a verbatim citation. Where one sees "does not succeed the arhat-fruit" in quotation marks elsewhere, know it for an interpretive title, not the original sentence of any sūtra or śāstra. This scope is marked per the recorded source-gap, not passing interpretation off as original text. (The Realization-axis BODHI-P01 holds this same scope in its §V.)

**⚠️ Correction three: the "vow-bodhicitta / practice-bodhicitta" pairing is not fixed in this paper's sources; nor should "freedom begins from the vow" be inflated into "arising = consummate freedom."** First, a terminological scope: the pairing of "vow-bodhicitta / practice-bodhicitta" (*praṇidhi-/prasthāna-cittotpāda*), though a later commonplace (the Huayan Xianshou tradition, Śāntideva's *Bodhicaryāvatāra* tradition), has no settled locus in this paper's three sources (*Yogācārabhūmi*, *Śāstra*, *Avataṃsaka*); the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s "right vow (nature)" and "the support of training" are functional approximations, not that paired term itself. The paper therefore uses the sources' own "first arising of the mind / first right vow," neither setting up that pairing as a rubric nor pinning on the canon what it does not state. Second, "freedom begins from the vow" establishes that the *ground* of the bodhisattva's mastery rises from the vow (3.1), not that a single thought of arising yields consummate freedom; the filling-out of the bodhisattva's mastery takes three asaṃkhyeya kalpas of the two provisions (→ P02), and its position-by-position freedom awaits the fifty stages the Realization axis walks. To hear "freedom begins from the vow" as "arising = consummation" is the opposite extreme, not taken here.

## VI. Conclusion

**Restatement.** The bodhisattva's mastery does not succeed the arhat-fruit: it is not the arhat's mastery upgraded, but a distinct vehicle's freedom forked from the first arising of bodhicitta. In the canon, the *Yogācārabhūmi* grounds the bodhisattva's standing (the ground of mastery) on arising ("counted among the bodhisattvas"); the *Śāstra* and *Avataṃsaka* sign the not-taking ("passing the two vehicles' grounds and entering the bodhisattva position," "long due for nirvāṇa / fully contemplating / practising the real-limit yet not taking, not realizing"); the "Chapter on Lineage" grounds the otherness-in-kind of vehicle ("realizing the purity of the cognitive obstruction too"), and the "Merit of the First Arising" the difference of order of magnitude (surpassing "teaching beings to win the four fruits"). Doctrinally, three layers: freedom begins from the vow (the ground rests on arising, not on a fruit, 3.1); signed by not-taking (able to not take what was his, the opposite of the arhat's taking, 3.2); two distinct lineages and measures, not one ladder (severing the cognitive obstruction, measured by accumulation, 3.3).

**Practice guidance.** Here the practitioner should change the question from "must I first become an arhat, then upgrade to the bodhisattva's mastery?" to "is this thought of mine the freedom of escaping my own suffering, or the vow to be able to not take the realization that is mine to take?" The former imagines mastery as a high-and-low on one ladder and traps one in the anxiety of "qualifying for an upgrade"; the latter returns one to the moment of arising — the vow once made, the bodhisattva's mastery has a ground, and what follows is the three-asaṃkhyeya accumulation (not the seeking of a separate freedom to set atop the four fruits). And especially: do not take "being able to escape suffering" for the proof of the bodhisattva's mastery; its true signature is "being able to not take the escape that was one's, and not-taking without sinking in weariness." One who seeks "to leave," as the arhat does, wins the arhat's mastery (real, not inferior); one who vows "not to leave, and to be unstained" enters the gate of the bodhisattva's mastery. Both are real, but they are not one thing.

**Opening the division (and marking the seam).** This paper opens *The Bodhisattva's Mastery* and sets the division's frame: the bodhisattva's mastery rises from the vow and stands as a distinct vehicle, measured by accumulation and signed by not-taking, not succeeding the arhat-fruit. On this frame the later papers unfold its faces in turn: the two boundless accumulations as mastery measured by accumulation (P02, taking up this paper's "difference of measure"); the six and ten perfections as the shape of freedom (P03–04); the four embraces, altruism as mastery (P05); the forbearance of non-arising as cognitive signature, and effortless flow past the forbearance (P06–07); the eighth ground's ten masteries as path-bound freedom (P08, strictly distinguished from the *tathāgata*'s ten masteries in the *tathagata* division); and, at the core, non-abiding nirvāṇa as the heart of the bodhisattva's mastery (P09, taking up this paper's hidden vein of "not-taking"); closing with the ultimate adjudication of the bodhisattva's and the arhat's masteries (P10). The division holds throughout to one sense: the bodhisattva's mastery is a distinct vehicle's freedom, signed by the two boundless accumulations, rising from the vow, not succeeding the arhat-fruit.

As the *seam paper*, it meets the Realization axis's BODHI-P01, "Not a Continuation of Arhatship," at the independence of bodhicitta: the same body of canon, read by Realization as "the independence of the road" (how the bodhisattva path forks from arhatship at arising), by Attainment as "the independence of the fruit" (how the bodhisattva's mastery stands without succeeding the arhat's). The two are one at arising, and divide at the walking and the arrival. **Realization writes "walking without succeeding"; Attainment writes "a mastery that does not succeed but stands of itself"; the two axes hand over at the one point of arising, each completing its own face.**

**Open questions.** First, this paper takes "not-taking" as the signature of the bodhisattva's mastery (3.2); its deep sense (non-abiding nirvāṇa, abiding neither in birth-and-death nor in nirvāṇa) is here only opened as a vein, its full doctrine belonging to P09. Second, the paper cuts §IV (the bodhisattva's mastery and "vocation / calling" are only superficially alike); whether the freedom of "being able to not take the realization that was one's, without weariness" admits a positively constructible deep structure, distinct in root from all subject-presupposing theories of motivation, is left to a later paper. Third, how the bodhisattva's mastery's "measured by accumulation" (an additive freedom) and the arhat's mastery's "measured by severance" (a subtractive freedom) stand together under one name, "mastery," as two real freedoms without displacing each other — this paper establishes them as two distinct measures (3.3); their full mutual display belongs to P02, their ultimate adjudication to P10; here only the question is opened.
