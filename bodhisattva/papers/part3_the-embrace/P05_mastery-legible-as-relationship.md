# The Four Embraces, Altruism as Mastery

**The Four Embraces as the Shape of Embracing Beings · Shared Work = Having the Same Undertaking · Their Predominance Discloses by Ground · Their Realm Seen Only by the Eye of Pure Knowing**

*中文主題：四攝為利他即自在之形——攝化眾生之形，關係中可見之自在*

## Abstract

The preceding paper, BODHI-P03, established the six pāramitās as the shape of the bodhisattva's mastery, and marked its third face — the six as the shape of benefiting self-and-other (freedom legible as benefit-to-others, 3.3). But the six embrace both self-benefit and other-benefit; their *purely other-benefiting* face the treatises articulate in a dedicated set, the four embraces (giving, kind speech, beneficial conduct, shared work), set up purely for the embracing of beings. This paper takes up P03's third face and unfolds it in focus, arguing: the four embraces are the *purely other-benefiting* shape of the bodhisattva's mastery — and altruism is mastery: the bodhisattva's freedom is not held apart from the four embraces; the freedom is legible, directly and in the present, precisely in the *relationship* of embracing beings. Three layers. First, the four are the shape of embracing beings — the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* and the *Yogācārabhūmi* list the four, the *Yogācārabhūmi* judging them the four means of embracing, taming, and ripening beings (drawing-in, embracing, leading-in, accompanying), "beyond which there is nothing further or greater," the *Śāstra* showing they cause beings to abide in the ten courses of good. Second, shared work = "having the same undertaking as the other," whereby the efficacy of altruism is directly visible in the relationship — the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s chapter on the means of embracing gives the essence of shared work: whatever good the bodhisattva exhorts another to take up, he himself manifestly takes up and trains in, equally or to a greater degree, so that he is "of the same undertaking" and cannot be met with the rebuke "you cannot train in the good yourself, how do you exhort another," and the one matured, seeing this, becomes "firm and settled, without regress, in his own taking-up of the roots of good"; so the bodhisattva's mastery (his having himself first perfected the good he commends) is precisely what is visible and verified within the relationship of shared work — mastery legible as relationship. Third, the four's predominance discloses by ground, and their realm is seen only by the eye of pure knowing — the *Avataṃsaka*'s Ten Grounds judges the four to predominate by ground (beneficial conduct at the third, Radiant ground; shared work at the fourth, Blazing ground), as the four added perfections rise foremost ground by ground (P04), marking the embracing-of-others rising with the realized stage; and the Entry into the Dharma-Realm shows the realm in which the bodhisattva embraces beings by the four to be one "the great śrāvakas cannot see" — precisely what the śrāvaka cannot see (the freedom of exiting-and-taking-the-realization does not reach the freedom of returning-to-deliver-beings) — and this is the adjudication of altruism *as* mastery. To be marked with care: the "*is* mastery" of "altruism is mastery" is this book's framing-angle (routing) and the author's adjudicated reading; the canon literally states the marks of altruism, the four's predominance, and "seen only by the eye of pure knowing," but does not itself pronounce "is mastery"; this paper composes that adjudication and marks its scope transparently, not passing the author's adjudication off as the canon's literal word. Conclusion: the four are the purely-other-benefiting shape of the bodhisattva's mastery, the freedom visible and verifiable within the relationship of embracing beings (shared work cannot be rebuked), rising by ground and seen only by the eye of pure knowing — altruism is mastery (this adjudication being the author's framing-angle).

**Keywords**: four embraces, giving, kind speech, beneficial conduct, shared work, embracing beings, the four means, drawing-in/embracing/leading-in/accompanying, abiding in the ten courses of good, same undertaking, cannot be rebuked, mastery legible as relationship, the four's predominance by ground, seen only by the eye of pure knowing, the śrāvaka cannot see, altruism is mastery

## I. The Live Question

The prior paper, P03, established the six pāramitās as the shape of the bodhisattva's mastery, and at its third layer (3.3) marked one face: the six are the shape of "self-and-other benefit," so the bodhisattva's mastery is legible as benefit-to-others. But P03's "self-and-other benefit" is a double embrace — one and the same giving both accomplishes one's own merit (self-benefit) and benefits beings (other-benefit), one act doubly accomplishing. Can the *purely other-benefiting* face within that double embrace be drawn out on its own and given a dedicated unfolding? The treatises answer yes: beside the six (which doubly embrace self and other), they set up a further set, the four embraces — giving, kind speech, beneficial conduct, shared work — purely for the *embracing of beings*. The six's other-benefit is "what one and the same self-benefiting act also accomplishes"; the four embraces' other-benefit is "a gate established purely for embracing beings." This paper takes up P03's third face and unfolds this purely-other-benefiting four.

But what this paper establishes is more than the description "the four are the purely-other-benefiting shape." It is a stronger thesis: **altruism is mastery.** What does "is" mean? It means: the bodhisattva's mastery is not a "mastery" accomplished first somewhere else (in meditation, in the severance of defilement) and then taken up to do good deeds toward others — as if mastery were the substance and altruism its use, mastery within and altruism without. This paper argues the contrary: the bodhisattva's mastery is legible, directly and in the present, precisely in the *relationship* of embracing beings — apart from that relationship, there is nowhere to point to the bodhisattva's mastery. To put it sharply: not "having mastery, he can therefore benefit others," but "the relationship of benefiting others is the very place where the mastery resides and shows." This is what the title means by *mastery legible as relationship* — mastery readable in relationship.

Here a misreading easily arises, to be flagged at the outset: taking the four embraces as "social charity done apart from mastery" — as if the bodhisattva, on his cushion, in concentration, accomplished some inner mastery, and in his spare time went out to do good (giving to the poor, speaking gently to the wayward), like a worldly good person doing philanthropy. On that view mastery and altruism become two things: mastery a privately held inner state, altruism its outward almsgiving. This paper breaks that in full. Below it will be seen: the second of the four (shared work) has just this structure — "the bodhisattva himself first perfects the good he commends, and so is 'of the same undertaking' as the one he matures" — the bodhisattva's accomplishment (mastery) is constituted, made visible, and made effective precisely *within* the relationship of "having the same undertaking as beings"; apart from that relationship, "shared work" is not shared work at all. So altruism is not mastery's leftover business; the relationship of altruism is the direct presentation of mastery.

The thesis: **the four embraces (giving, kind speech, beneficial conduct, shared work) are the purely-other-benefiting shape of the bodhisattva's mastery; and altruism is mastery — the freedom visible and verifiable within the relationship of embracing beings (shared work cannot be rebuked), rising by ground, and the mark distinguishing the bodhisattva's mastery from the śrāvaka's (seen only by the eye of pure knowing).** The argument: §II presents the canon (the four listed and item by item; the four as the means of embracing; abiding in the ten courses of good; predominance by ground; seen only by the eye of pure knowing); §III argues in three layers (the four as the shape of embracing beings, 3.1; shared work = same undertaking, altruism visible in the relationship, 3.2; altruism is mastery — rising by ground, seen only by the eye of pure knowing, 3.3); §IV is cut; §V corrects misreadings and marks the framing-angle scope of "is mastery" and the textual-vein scope of the *Avataṃsaka* passages; §VI concludes, takes up P03, binds P01/P02/P04, and opens P09/P10.

> One scope-note for the whole paper, flagged here with care (detailed in §V): the "*is* mastery" of the title "altruism is mastery" is this book's framing-angle (routing) and the author's adjudicated reading. The canon cited literally states the "marks of altruism," "the four's predominance by ground," and "the bodhisattva's realm of the four embraces, seen only by the eye of pure knowing, not seen by the śrāvaka," but does not itself pronounce "altruism *is* mastery." This paper composes the argument for "is mastery" (on three grounds: the un-rebukable relational structure of shared work, the four's rising by ground, and the śrāvaka's not seeing), and marks it transparently as the author's framing-angle, not passing this adjudication off as the canon's literal word. Where the reader sees "altruism is mastery," let it be known as this book's adjudication, not the verbatim of any one passage.

## II. Canonical Basis

This section presents three clusters: the four listed and item by item (2.1); the four as the means of embracing beings and abiding in the ten courses of good (2.2); the four's predominance by ground and "seen only by the eye of pure knowing" (2.3). The sources are the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* (T1509), the *Yogācārabhūmi* (T1579), and the *Avataṃsaka* (T0279), all CBETA-verified.

### 2.1 The Four Listed and Item by Item: Giving, Kind Speech, Beneficial Conduct, Shared Work

The settled list of the four, the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* gives, citing the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā*:

> The bodhisattva-mahāsattvas embrace beings by four matters. Which four? Giving, kind speech, benefiting (beneficial conduct), shared work.[^1]

Giving, kind speech, benefiting (beneficial conduct), shared work — the standard four gates of the embraces (catuḥ-saṃgraha-vastu), by which the bodhisattva embraces beings. Their item-by-item gloss the *Śāstra* further fixes, marking especially the fourth, shared work:

> "Benefiting" is what is called the four embraces. By the two givings, of goods and of Dharma, beings are embraced … "shared work" means: the bodhisattva teaches and transforms beings, causing them to practise the good and to share in what he does; the bodhisattva's mind being good and the being's mind evil, he can transform that evil, causing it to share in his own good.[^2]

Giving divides into the giving of goods and of Dharma, kind speech and beneficial conduct each have their gloss; and "shared work" is "the bodhisattva teaches and transforms beings, causing them to practise the good and to share in what he does … transforming their evil, causing it to share in his own good." This structure of "sharing in what he does · causing it to share in his own good" is the pivot of §3.2. The *Yogācārabhūmi*'s chapter on the means of embracing sets the four within a nine-aspect system, and gives the essence of "shared work":

> What is the bodhisattva's shared work? Whatever meaning, whatever root of good the bodhisattva exhorts another to take up and train in, in that very meaning, that very root of good, he himself manifestly takes up and trains, equally or to a greater degree. Thus the bodhisattva is of the same undertaking as the other, and so it is called shared work.[^3]

The essence of shared work: whatever good the bodhisattva exhorts another to take up and train in, he himself "equally or to a greater degree" manifestly takes up and trains, being "of the same undertaking" as the one he matures — hence "shared work." This is "having the same undertaking": the bodhisattva himself first (and to no less a degree than he commends) does the good he commends, and so stands in the relationship of "same undertaking" with the one he matures. (Note: to keep the anchor-count in check, of the four only shared work's essence is quoted here; the essences of giving, kind speech, and beneficial conduct are located in the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s chapter on the means of embracing, with line-numbers ready, but not individually quoted, see §V.)

### 2.2 The Four as the Means of Embracing Beings · Abiding in the Ten Courses of Good

That the four are the shape of "embracing beings," the *Yogācārabhūmi* judges by making them the four means of embracing, taming, and ripening beings, "beyond which there is nothing further or greater":

> For what cause are only the four embracing-matters called means? The bodhisattva, by the four means embraced in these embracing-matters, can universally embrace, tame, and ripen beings. Beyond these there is nothing further or greater. What are the four means? First, the drawing-in means; second, the embracing means; third, the leading-in means; fourth, the accompanying means.[^4]

The four embraces are the bodhisattva's four means of embracing beings (drawing-in, embracing, leading-in, accompanying); the whole way of embracing, taming, and ripening beings is exhausted in these four, "beyond which there is nothing further or greater" — marking the four's embracing-function as one complete schema. Their item-by-item assignment the *Yogācārabhūmi* further gives:

> If the bodhisattva first practises giving, know it is named the drawing-in means … next practises kind speech … this is named the embracing means … next practises beneficial conduct, drawing beings out of the unwholesome and, in the wholesome, exhorting, taming, settling, and establishing them. This beneficial conduct, know, is named the leading-in means … the fourth, shared work, know, is named the accompanying means.[^5]

Giving = drawing-in (first benefiting them so they receive the teaching) → kind speech = embracing (clearing their confusion so they discern right principle) → beneficial conduct = leading-in (drawing out of the unwholesome, settling in the wholesome) → shared work = accompanying (sharing the same right work so they go along) — the four gates embrace beings in sequence. And the destination of the four's embracing the *Śāstra* marks as "abiding in the ten courses of good":

> This bodhisattva, by the four embraces, embraces beings and causes them to abide in the ten courses of good — this is the broad statement of the meaning of the four embraces.[^6]

The bodhisattva by the four embraces causes beings to abide in the ten courses of good — the broad meaning of the four. The *Avataṃsaka*'s Worthy-Head chapter further binds the four directly to "boundless benefit for beings":

> If one gains the wisdom of well embracing beings, one can accomplish the four embraces; if one can accomplish the four embraces, one gives beings boundless benefit.[^7]

By "the wisdom of well embracing beings" one accomplishes the four, and accomplishing the four "gives beings boundless benefit" — the four point directly to the boundless benefiting of beings. Together: the four are the bodhisattva's complete means of embracing, taming, ripening, settling in the good, and benefiting beings without bound, set up purely for the embracing of beings.

### 2.3 The Four's Predominance Discloses by Ground · Their Realm Seen Only by the Eye of Pure Knowing

The four are not laid out flat and static, but have a predominant disclosure by the bodhisattva's realized ground. The *Avataṃsaka*'s Ten Grounds judges beneficial conduct predominant at the third, Radiant ground, and shared work predominant at the fourth, Blazing ground:

> (The third, Radiant ground) Among the four embraces, this bodhisattva has beneficial conduct predominant; among the ten pāramitās, patience predominant; the rest are not unpractised, but according to one's power and portion.[^8]

> (The fourth, Blazing ground) Among the four embraces, this bodhisattva has shared work predominant; among the ten pāramitās, vigour predominant; the rest are not unpractised, but according to one's power and portion.[^9]

The third ground "beneficial conduct predominant," the fourth ground "shared work predominant" — the four predominate by ground in succession (as the four added perfections rise foremost ground by ground, P04), marking the embracing-of-others rising with the realized stage. And the *Avataṃsaka*'s Entry into the Dharma-Realm marks: the realm in which the bodhisattva embraces beings by the four, the great śrāvakas cannot see:

> The great bodhisattvas have the eye of pure knowing, able to enter the inconceivable profound realm of the Tathāgata … able by the right Dharma to awaken beings, able by the four embraces to embrace beings; the great śrāvakas cannot see the Tathāgata's spiritual power, nor can they see the host of bodhisattvas.[^10]

The great bodhisattvas "by the four embraces embrace beings," and that realm "the great śrāvakas cannot see" — the embracing of the four is a surpassing mark of the bodhisattva-path, seen only by "the eye of pure knowing," beyond the śrāvaka's eye. (Note: this Entry-into-the-Dharma-Realm passage, on the bodhisattva's realm of the four embraces seen only by the eye of pure knowing and not by the śrāvaka, belongs to the vein of "the difference of bodhisattva and śrāvaka realms," not the same vein as the preceding "predominance by ground"; this paper uses them severally, see §V.)

> Scope restated (detailed in §V): the three clusters above literally state "the four listed and item by item," "the four as the means of embracing · abiding in the ten courses of good," "the four's predominance by ground · seen only by the eye of pure knowing." The "is mastery" adjudication of "altruism is mastery" is this book's framing-angle, not the verbatim of any passage above; this paper composes its argument from these three matters and does not pronounce "is mastery" on the canon's behalf.

## III. Core Argument

§II has shown the canon's express words. This section argues in three layers: why the four are the purely-other-benefiting shape of the bodhisattva's mastery (3.1); why shared work's "same undertaking" makes the efficacy of altruism directly visible in the relationship (3.2); why altruism is mastery — rising by ground, seen only by the eye of pure knowing (3.3).

### 3.1 The Four as the Shape of Embracing Beings: The Purely-Other-Benefiting Shape of the Bodhisattva's Mastery

The prior paper P03 established the six as the shape of the bodhisattva's mastery, its third face (3.3) marking the six as the double-embrace shape of self-and-other benefit. This layer establishes: the four are the dedicated shape of the *purely other-benefiting* face within that double embrace. Why must the four be set up beside the six? Because the six's other-benefit is "what one and the same self-benefiting act also accomplishes" (the same giving accumulates one's own merit and benefits beings), its centre of gravity still passing through self-perfecting; whereas the embracing of beings (how to bring a being of evil mind to receive the teaching, enter the good, abide in the ten courses of good) requires a gate set up purely "toward beings" — and this is the four embraces. The ground is the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s "only the four embracing-matters are called means … can universally embrace, tame, and ripen beings, beyond which there is nothing further or greater" (2.2): the complete way of embracing beings is exhausted in giving (drawing-in), kind speech (embracing), beneficial conduct (leading-in), shared work (accompanying), and "nothing further or greater" (neither more nor less — exactly complete).

So the four are the shape the bodhisattva's mastery takes purely as the embracing of beings. Their difference from the six is like the difference between "the whole of a medical art" and "the dedicated procedure of clinical contact with the patient": the six are like the physician's whole cultivation (including the advance of his own learning, self-benefit together with other-benefit), the four like the physician's dedicated procedure of "receiving, reassuring, leading into treatment, accompanying through recovery," set up purely toward the patient. The sequence of the four — first giving to benefit so they receive the teaching (drawing-in, as the physician first wins the patient's trust by kind reception), next kind speech to clear confusion so they discern principle (embracing, as the physician explains the illness so the patient understands), next beneficial conduct to draw out of the unwholesome and settle in the wholesome (leading-in, as the physician treats so the patient leaves illness and enters health), finally shared work to share the same right work so they go along (accompanying, as the physician accompanies the patient in sustained convalescence so he can do it himself) — each is an embracing motion "toward beings," none an inner chore of "self-cultivation." So the four are the shape the bodhisattva's mastery takes oriented toward beings, purely as embracing; the "other-benefit" face of the bodhisattva's mastery here gains its dedicated outline.

> ✅ Doctrinally sound: the four embraces are the shape the bodhisattva's mastery takes purely as the embracing of beings. The *Yogācārabhūmi* judges them the four means of embracing, taming, ripening beings (drawing-in, embracing, leading-in, accompanying), "beyond which there is nothing further or greater" (2.2); the *Śāstra* shows they cause beings to abide in the ten courses of good (2.2). The four's difference from the six: the six are the double-embrace shape of self-and-other (P03 3.3), the four the dedicated shape of the "purely other-benefiting" face within it.

### 3.2 Shared Work = Having the Same Undertaking: The Efficacy of Altruism Is Directly Visible in the Relationship (Mastery Legible as Relationship)

This layer is the pivot of the whole paper. The fourth embrace, shared work, has the structure that best shows the "is" of "altruism is mastery": the bodhisattva's mastery (his having himself first perfected the good he commends) is directly visible and verified precisely within the relationship of "shared work." The ground is shared work's essence (the [^3] of 2.1) and its efficacy:

> The beings he matures, knowing this bodhisattva's cultivation of shared work, become firm and settled, without regress, in their own taking-up of the roots of good … Nor, when the bodhisattva exhorts and guides beings by such shared work, can another rebuke him, saying: "you yourself cannot train in the good, how do you so earnestly exhort others by the good"…[^11]

Its structure has three moments. First, the constitution of "shared work" rests on the bodhisattva's own prior practice: whatever good he exhorts another to take up, he himself "equally or to a greater degree" manifestly takes up and trains (2.1) — he himself first (and to no less a degree than he commends) does the good commended. Second, because of his own prior practice, he is "of the same undertaking" as the one matured, and so cannot be rebuked: the one matured cannot rebuke him with "you yourself cannot train in the good, how do you exhort me" — a person who cannot do it himself yet exhorts another, his exhortation is empty, exposable to his face ("you can't even do it yourself, by what right do you exhort me"); the bodhisattva, being "of the same undertaking" (himself first perfecting the good), cannot have his exhortation so exposed. Third, because un-rebukable, it is effective: the one matured, "knowing this bodhisattva's cultivation of shared work," becomes "firm and settled, without regress" in his own taking-up of the roots of good — the one matured, precisely seeing the bodhisattva's own perfection (mastery), becomes firm and irreversible in his roots of good.

Taking the three moments together, one thing is seen: the bodhisattva's mastery (his having himself first perfected the good commended) is not a privately held state stored within, waiting to be dispensed outward; it is constituted, identified, verified, and made effective precisely *within* the relationship of "shared work." Apart from the relationship of "having the same undertaking as beings," the fact that "the bodhisattva himself has done the good he commends" has no way of being seen, no way of being verified, no way of producing the effect of "making the other not regress" — "shared work" is not shared work at all (there is no "other" to be "same" with). So the bodhisattva's mastery is directly visible within this relationship: the one matured's "knowing this bodhisattva's cultivation of shared work" — that "knowing" is just mastery read out (legible) in the relationship. This is *mastery legible as relationship* — mastery does not exist apart from the relationship and then enter it; mastery is readable and verifiable within the relationship of altruism.

A comparison: a swimming coach and a learner. A coach who can truly swim (his "mastery of the water"), and a person who has only read swimming theory and never enters the water, may, lecturing on the bank, sound no different. But once in the water, "of the same undertaking" — the coach in the same water, demonstrating in person, his own body buoyant and at ease — the learner, "knowing the coach's cultivation of shared work," becomes "firm and settled, without regress" in his own learning (for he sees with his own eyes the coach at ease in the same water, and knows the thing can be done). The theory-only person, once in the water, is exposed to the learner's face ("you can't even float yourself, by what right do you teach me"), and his exhortation is empty. The coach's "mastery of the water" is directly visible and verified precisely within the relationship of "being in the same water with the learner" — apart from the relationship of entering the water together, there is no way to identify that he can truly swim. So with the bodhisattva's mastery in the four embraces (especially shared work): mastery is readable and verifiable within the relationship of embracing beings, not a privately held state apart from the relationship.

> ✅ Doctrinally sound: shared work = having the same undertaking as the other (*Yogācārabhūmi*'s chapter on the means of embracing, 2.1, [^11]). Its structure in three moments: the bodhisattva's own prior practice (equally or to a greater degree taking up the good commended) → so being of the same undertaking, un-rebukable for "exhorting what he has not done himself" → so the one matured, seeing this, becomes firm and settled, without regress. Hence the bodhisattva's mastery (his having himself first perfected the good commended) is directly visible, verified, and effective within the relationship of shared work; apart from the relationship, "shared work" is not constituted and mastery cannot be read. Mastery legible as relationship — mastery readable within the relationship of altruism, not a privately held state apart from it.

### 3.3 Altruism Is Mastery: The Four's Predominance Discloses by Ground · Seen Only by the Eye of Pure Knowing (the Śrāvaka Cannot See)

The last layer establishes the "is mastery" adjudication (and keeps its framing-angle scope). §3.2 established "mastery visible within the relationship"; this layer further establishes "altruism is mastery" by two faces of demonstration: first, the four's predominance discloses by ground — the embracing-of-others gate strengthens with the bodhisattva's realized stage, so altruism is not a static external chore but something that increases with the increase of mastery; second, the realm of the four's embracing is seen only by the eye of pure knowing, not by the śrāvaka — the freedom of altruism is precisely what the śrāvaka cannot see, and here lies the adjudication of "altruism *as* mastery."

Take the first. The *Avataṃsaka*'s Ten Grounds judges the four to predominate by ground: at the third, Radiant ground "beneficial conduct predominant," at the fourth, Blazing ground "shared work predominant" (2.3). This is isomorphic with P04's "one of the ten perfections foremost per ground" — the four too predominate by ground in succession. Its sense: altruism (the four) is not a fixed quantity of added good deeds, but something that predominates and strengthens in succession with the ascent of the bodhisattva's realized ground; the higher the ground, the more one of its embracing-gates predominates. Were altruism merely "charity apart from mastery," it ought to bear no relation to the realized ground (the quantity of charity does not hang on the depth of inner realization); yet the texts plainly judge the four to predominate by ground, showing the strength of altruism to hang on the depth of the realized stage — altruism is a function of mastery, strengthening with it. This is the first face of demonstrating "altruism is mastery": altruism increases with mastery, so the two are not two things.

Take the second. The *Avataṃsaka*'s Entry into the Dharma-Realm marks: the realm in which the bodhisattva embraces beings by the four, "the great śrāvakas cannot see" (2.3). Why can the śrāvaka not see? Because the śrāvaka's freedom is the freedom of "exiting-and-taking-the-realization" (the exhaustion of taints, the severance of fetters, entering the remainderless, ARHAT-P01; this axis's P01 marks it signed by "taking the realization"), his horizon stopping at "his own liberation" and not reaching the matter of "returning into birth-and-death, embracing beings" — so the realm of the bodhisattva's "embracing beings by the four embraces" simply does not fall within the śrāvaka's field of view (as a blind man come to a treasure-isle does not see the treasure). And this "what the śrāvaka cannot see" is precisely where the surpassing mark of the bodhisattva's mastery lies: the bodhisattva's mastery, in what makes it differ from the śrāvaka's, in what makes it "the *bodhisattva's* mastery," lies just in this altruistic realm of "returning to deliver beings, embracing by the four" — this realm is the very site of the freedom P01 established as "not-taking, returning to deliver beings" and P02 as "accumulating uncircumscribed for beings." So "altruism" is not an addition outside the bodhisattva's mastery, but precisely where the bodhisattva's mastery "differs from the śrāvaka's," its very badge of being "the bodhisattva's mastery" — what the śrāvaka cannot see is just this altruism-as-freedom. This is the second face of demonstrating "altruism is mastery": the realm of altruism is precisely where the bodhisattva's mastery differs from the śrāvaka's, so altruism *is* the (bodhisattva's) mastery.

Taking the two faces together: altruism strengthens with mastery (predominance by ground), and the realm of altruism is precisely where the bodhisattva's mastery differs from the śrāvaka's (seen only by the eye of pure knowing) — so "altruism is mastery": the bodhisattva's mastery is not held apart from altruism; the mastery is visible within the relationship of embracing beings (3.2), strengthens with mastery (3.3, first), and is its badge of difference from the śrāvaka (3.3, second).

> ⚠️ Scope (detailed in §V): the "is mastery" adjudication of "altruism is mastery" is this book's framing-angle and the author's adjudicated reading. The *Avataṃsaka*'s Ten Grounds (the four's predominance by ground) and Entry into the Dharma-Realm (seen only by the eye of pure knowing, not by the śrāvaka) cited above literally state "such conduct predominant" and "the realm profound · the śrāvaka does not see," not the words "altruism *is* mastery." This paper, on three matters — the un-rebukable relational structure of shared work (3.2) + the four's strengthening by ground (3.3, first) + the śrāvaka's not seeing (3.3, second) — composes the argument for "altruism is mastery," and marks it transparently as the author's framing-angle, not passing this adjudication off as the canon's literal word.

Three layers together: the four are the purely-other-benefiting shape of the bodhisattva's mastery (3.1); shared work = having the same undertaking, so mastery is directly visible within the relationship (3.2); altruism strengthens with mastery and is its badge of difference from the śrāvaka (3.3). Together they establish the thesis: **the four embraces (giving, kind speech, beneficial conduct, shared work) are the purely-other-benefiting shape of the bodhisattva's mastery; altruism is mastery — the freedom visible and verifiable within the relationship of embracing beings (shared work cannot be rebuked), rising by ground, the badge distinguishing the bodhisattva's mastery from the śrāvaka's (seen only by the eye of pure knowing).** (The "is mastery" adjudication being the author's framing-angle, §V.)

## IV. Interdisciplinary Convergence

(This paper deliberately omits the interdisciplinary section; the reason is given in §VI. Its axis is the Mahāyāna four-embraces doctrine of "altruism is mastery · mastery visible within relationship." Modern models — "the relational self," "servant leadership," "altruism and flourishing" — superficially resemble "mastery visible within relationship" in "the self constituted in relationship," "leadership that serves first," and "altruism enhancing well-being," but cannot sustain a three-layer convergence without lapsing into analogy on three points: "the un-rebukable structure of shared work (only by first perfecting the good commended is one of the same undertaking as the matured)," "the four's predominance by realized ground (altruism a function of mastery)," and "the realm of the four seen only by the eye of pure knowing · not by the śrāvaka." On the last especially, "the śrāvaka cannot see" marks a soteriological level-difference — "the freedom of exiting-and-taking-the-realization does not reach the freedom of returning-to-deliver-beings" — with no counterpart in any model reducible to worldly relational psychology or leadership studies; servant leadership's "service" is still a conditioned altruistic act, only superficially resembling the realized-stage difference of "the bodhisattva's realm of the four embraces seen only by the eye of pure knowing." Hence omitted per the series' editorial convention, with this note.)

## V. Madhyamaka Corrections

This section clears three misreadings adjacent to the thesis, the second bearing on the framing-angle scope of "is mastery," the third on textual vein and anchor scope, both requiring special honesty.

**❌ Misreading one: "the four embraces are social charity done apart from mastery (mastery within, altruism without; mastery accomplished first, then in spare time going out to do good)."** This the paper breaks in full. If the four were merely "charity apart from mastery," mastery and altruism would become two things: mastery a privately held inner state, altruism its outward almsgiving. But §3.2 has shown: the "shared work" of the four has just the structure "the bodhisattva himself first perfects the good he commends, and so is of the same undertaking, un-rebukable, making the other not regress" — the bodhisattva's mastery is identified, verified, and made effective precisely within the relationship of "having the same undertaking as beings"; apart from the relationship, "shared work" is not constituted and mastery cannot be read. And §3.3 shows the four predominate by realized ground (altruism a function of mastery, strengthening with it) and their realm is what the śrāvaka cannot see (altruism precisely the badge distinguishing the bodhisattva's mastery from the śrāvaka's). So altruism is not mastery's leftover business, not an outward almsgiving; the relationship of altruism is the direct presentation and badge of mastery. To read the four as "charity apart from mastery" splits mastery and altruism into two, sinking into the dualism of "inner realization + outer good," losing the "is."

**⚠️ Correction two: the "is mastery" of "altruism is mastery" is this book's framing-angle and the author's adjudicated reading, not the verbatim of any passage.** To be marked with care: the canon this paper cites (the *Śāstra*, the *Yogācārabhūmi*, the *Avataṃsaka*) literally states the marks of altruism — "the four listed and item by item," "the four as the means of embracing · abiding in the ten courses of good · boundless benefit," "the four's predominance by ground," "the bodhisattva's realm of the four embraces seen only by the eye of pure knowing · not seen by the śrāvaka" — but does not itself pronounce "altruism *is* mastery." The "is mastery" adjudication is the framing-angle (routing) of this book's *Bodhisattva's Mastery* division and the author's adjudicated reading: this paper, on three matters — the un-rebukable relational structure of shared work (3.2) + the four's strengthening by ground (3.3, first) + the śrāvaka's not seeing (3.3, second) — composes the argument for "altruism is mastery." This argument has canonical ground for its three matters, but the *identification* "is mastery" is itself the author's adjudication, not the verbatim of any one passage. Where the reader sees "altruism is mastery," let it be known as this book's adjudication, not the canon's literal word. This scope is marked per the source routing-note, not passing the author's adjudication off as the canon's plain statement.

**⚠️ Correction three: the Entry-into-the-Dharma-Realm's "seen only by the eye of pure knowing" and the Ten Grounds' "the four's predominance by ground" are not the same vein; and of the four, only shared work's essence is quoted, the other three essences not individually cited.** First, §3.3 uses two kinds of *Avataṃsaka* passage: the Ten Grounds (beneficial conduct predominant at the third ground, shared work at the fourth) belongs to the vein of "the four's predominance by ground"; the Entry into the Dharma-Realm (the great śrāvakas cannot see the bodhisattva's realm of the four embraces) belongs to another vein, "the difference of bodhisattva and śrāvaka realms," not a predominance-by-ground passage. This paper uses them severally (the former to demonstrate "altruism strengthens with mastery," the latter to demonstrate "altruism is the badge of difference from the śrāvaka"), not conflating the two veins; let the reader not take the Entry-into-the-Dharma-Realm passage as a predominance-judgment of some ground. Second, to keep the anchor-count in check, of the four only shared work's essence is quoted (the [^3], [^11] of 2.1), because shared work is the pivot of §3.2's "same undertaking · visible in relationship"; the essences of giving, kind speech, and beneficial conduct are located in the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s chapter on the means of embracing, with line-numbers ready (essence of giving T30n1579_p0505b06; essence of kind speech T30n1579_p0529c24; essence of beneficial conduct T30n1579_p0530c17), but not individually quoted here. Should a symmetrical item-by-item essence of all four be required later, the line-numbers can be supplied; this paper does not take "not quoted" for "without ground."

## VI. Conclusion

**Restatement.** The four embraces are the purely-other-benefiting shape of the bodhisattva's mastery; altruism is mastery. In the canon, the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* and the *Yogācārabhūmi* list the four (giving, kind speech, beneficial conduct, shared work) and gloss them item by item (shared work = of the same undertaking as the matured · causing them to share in his own good), the *Yogācārabhūmi* judging the four the four means of embracing, taming, ripening beings (drawing-in, embracing, leading-in, accompanying), "beyond which there is nothing further or greater," the *Śāstra* showing they cause beings to abide in the ten courses of good, the *Avataṃsaka*'s Worthy-Head chapter binding them to boundless benefit for beings; the *Avataṃsaka*'s Ten Grounds judges the four to predominate by ground (beneficial conduct at the third, shared work at the fourth), and the Entry into the Dharma-Realm marks the bodhisattva's realm of the four embraces seen only by the eye of pure knowing, not by the śrāvaka. Doctrinally, three layers: the four as the purely-other-benefiting shape (3.1); shared work = same undertaking, so mastery is directly visible and verifiable within the relationship (3.2); altruism strengthens with mastery and is its badge of difference from the śrāvaka (3.3). To be marked with care: the "is mastery" of "altruism is mastery" is this book's framing-angle and the author's adjudication (§V); the canon literally states the marks of altruism, the four's predominance, "seen only by the eye of pure knowing."

**Practice guidance.** Here the practitioner should clear one confusion and set up one practice. The confusion to clear: do not suppose the bodhisattva's mastery is a privately held inner state accomplished first on the cushion, in concentration, and only then taken out in spare time to do good — for on that view mastery and altruism become two poles. This paper shows: mastery is visible and verifiable precisely within the relationship of "embracing beings." The practice to set up: take "shared work" as the mirror of self-verification. If you exhort another to the good (kind speech, beneficial conduct), first ask yourself: this good, have I myself, "equally or to a greater degree," manifestly taken it up and trained in it? If, not having done it myself, I merely exhort another, the exhortation is empty, exposable to his face ("you can't even do it yourself"), and cannot make him firm and unregressing; only by first perfecting the good myself, being "of the same undertaking" with the one exhorted, does the one matured, seeing it, become firm and settled in his roots of good. So the heart of practising the four is not "doing good deeds apart from mastery," but making oneself first accomplish the good commended (mastery) and making that accomplishment visible within the relationship of having the same undertaking with beings (altruism) — mastery and altruism are one in "shared work." And remember: the four predominate by the realized ground, the strength of altruism hanging on the depth of inner realization; so neither preen nor despair over the momentary quantity of altruism, but take the depth of the realized stage as the root of altruism.

**Taking up, binding, and opening.** This paper takes up the prior paper P03: P03 established the six as the double-embrace shape of self-and-other (3.3), this paper unfolds its "purely other-benefiting" face as the four embraces and further establishes "altruism is mastery." Bound to the earlier part: the four's "beyond which nothing further or greater" embracing is just P02's "accumulating uncircumscribed for beings" made concrete in embracing; the four's realm "the śrāvaka cannot see" is just the site where P01's "not-taking, returning to deliver beings" freedom differs from the śrāvaka's. Bound to P04: the four's predominance by ground (beneficial conduct at the third, shared work at the fourth) is isomorphic with the ten perfections' one-foremost-per-ground, both the mark of "mastery disclosing ground by ground." Opening forward: this paper's "altruism is mastery · mastery visible within relationship" has its ultimate ground — abiding neither in the realization of self-benefit nor in the quiescence of exiting — in this axis's P09, "Non-Abiding Nirvāṇa"; and the ultimate adjudication of the bodhisattva's and the arhat's masteries (its edge already shown in this paper's 3.3 "the śrāvaka cannot see") awaits this axis's load-bearing close, P10. As for the Realization axis: this paper's four-embraces predominance by ground correlates thematically with the `bodhi-path` division's ten-grounds papers — the Realization axis walks the course of the ten grounds (including the ground-by-ground four-embraces conduct, beneficial conduct at the third, shared work at the fourth), the Attainment axis (this paper) reads the "altruism is mastery" that course discloses; the seam's doctrine is the author's, and this paper does not speak for the Realization axis.

**Open questions.** First, the paper cuts §IV (altruism-is-mastery and the relational self / servant leadership / altruism-and-flourishing are only superficially alike), as above. Second, the "is mastery" of "altruism is mastery" is the author's framing-angle, marked transparently; its relation to the canon's literal marks of altruism is "an adjudication composed from three canonical matters," not the canon's plain statement. Third, of the four, only shared work's essence is quoted; the essences of giving, kind speech, beneficial conduct (line-numbers ready) are not individually cited; should a symmetrical item-by-item essence be required, it can be supplied. Fourth, the strict distinction and reconciliation of the four embraces and the six pāramitās (the six doubly embrace self and other, the four are purely other-benefiting; and the six have a self-benefit portion the four lack, while the four have an embracing sequence the six do not dedicate) — this paper establishes only their difference (3.1), the detailed correlation awaiting another treatment. Fifth, this Mahāyāna division has no Pāli twin-track obligation; this paper has not gathered the Āgama / Nikāya four embracing-matters (dāna piyavajja atthacariyā samānattatā, DN 33 / AN 4.32) correspondence; a cross-collection comparison would be a separate matter.
