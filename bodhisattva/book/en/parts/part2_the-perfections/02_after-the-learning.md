## 2.2 After the Learning Is Done

The last chapter ended on a question: above the six, why do the treatises open out to ten—are the added skillful means, vow, power, and knowledge four new exercises? This chapter answers, and looks too at how the ten disclose themselves ground by ground.

First, what these four are. The *Mahāyāna-saṃgraha* defines each:

> [Skillful means] … distributes to all beings equally, dedicating on behalf of all beings to unsurpassed awakening. … [Power] … can draw the six perfections into continuous arising, with no break. … The latter four perfections should be known as post-realization non-discriminating wisdom, embracing all the perfections.

Skillful means (upāya-kauśalya) dedicates the roots of good grown by the six to all beings, and on their behalf toward unsurpassed awakening; vow (praṇidhāna) makes vows that draw in the conditions for practicing the six in lives to come; power (bala) subdues what obstructs and keeps the six "arising continuously, with no break"; knowledge (jñāna) establishes the wisdom of the prior six. Read their definitions and a common thread shows: not one is a fresh undertaking on its own; each is done *toward the six*—dedicating their roots of good, drawing their future conditions, guarding their continuity, establishing their wisdom.

### Not four new subjects, but the using of what was learned

The *Mahāyāna-saṃgraha* gives these four a verdict: "the latter four perfections should be known as post-realization non-discriminating wisdom." That phrase is the key.

"Post-realization non-discriminating wisdom" is the wisdom that arises *after* non-discriminating wisdom. A bodhisattva who has entered the grounds first wins the non-discriminating wisdom—the root wisdom that directly beholds suchness, subject and object alike stilled; but root wisdom only beholds suchness and raises no distinctions, and cannot, among the thousand differences of things, set its hands to work and benefit beings. So after root wisdom there arises a "subsequently-attained wisdom"—able to return into the differentiated world, to distinguish and establish anew, to raise means and vow and power to deliver beings. The four added perfections are the unfolding of this subsequent wisdom. The *Yogācārabhūmi* likewise calls the four plainly auxiliaries to the six:

> The perfection of skillful means serves as an auxiliary to the first three; … the perfection of vow serves as an auxiliary to the perfection of vigour.

Skillful means assists the first three; vow assists vigour—the four are aides to the six, not another road ranged beside them.

So "ten perfections" is not "ten exercises in a row," but "the six, plus four uses that complete them and keep them unbroken." It is like a person who has mastered a great body of learning. The root learning won by hard study, if it stays shut in the breast and never returns to the clinic and the teaching of others, benefits no one. *After* the mastering, he puts it to use: the skill to treat each illness as it comes, the long vow to relieve more of the sick, the sustained power to clear away what obstructs the work so it never stops, the matured judgment that brings all the learning to bear on a hard case—these "four uses after the learning is done" are not four new subjects learned beside his knowledge; they are that same knowledge "come back into the world to be used." So with the four and the six: not four new subjects, but a mastery already won, turning back around to complete the six and to deliver beings without end.

This runs right along the cord of the bodhisattva's mastery. His mastery is signed in "not taking realization"—not taking the realization that would "abide in root wisdom alone, stilled in suchness," but choosing, by subsequent wisdom, to come back into the differentiated world and deliver beings without ceasing. The four added perfections are just that "realized, yet not abiding; turning back to deliver beings" mastery, put to use in practice. In the arhat's mastery there is no talk of "four added perfections"—for his mastery rests in severing, taking realization, entering the remainderless; he does not walk the road of "turning back into the differentiated to benefit beings." The bodhisattva has the four added perfections precisely because his mastery is, from the first, a mastery of "realizing and not abiding, turning back to deliver." Their presence or absence marks, once more, the difference of the two masteries.

### Ground by ground, one perfection leading

The ten are not laid out flat all at once; they disclose themselves along the ten grounds, one perfection foremost at each.  First, let the names of these "ten grounds" be set out in full—they are the ten stations between entering the grounds and buddhahood. In the *Avataṃsaka*'s Ten Grounds, the bodhisattva Vajra-Treasury, by the Buddha's spiritual power, lists the ten one by one:

> Sons of the Buddha! The bodhisattva-mahāsattva's grounds of knowing are ten, which the buddhas of past, future, and present have declared, will declare, and now declare; and so do I declare. What are the ten? First, the Ground of Joy; second, the Stainless ground; third, the Radiant ground; fourth, the Blazing ground; fifth, the ground Hard to Conquer; sixth, the ground of Presence; seventh, the Far-Reaching ground; eighth, the Immovable ground; ninth, the ground of Good Insight; tenth, the Dharma-Cloud ground.

The Ground of Joy, the Stainless, the Radiant, the Blazing, the Hard-to-Conquer, the Present, the Far-Reaching, the Immovable, the Good-Insight, the Dharma-Cloud—from the "joy" of first entering the grounds to the "Dharma-cloud" that covers all like a cloud, ten stations rising in a row. The "eighth, Immovable ground" this book returns to again and again is just the eighth of these ten; and the forbearance of non-arising and the ten masteries of Parts Four and Five fall on this same eighth station. With this ten-rung ruler in mind first, "one perfection foremost at each ground" has somewhere to sit.

The *Avataṃsaka*, in the first ground, the Ground of Joy, sets the formula:

> Among the ten perfections this bodhisattva has the perfection of giving foremost; the other perfections are not unpracticed, but according to his power and portion.

The first ground has giving "foremost"—prominent, leading; yet "the other perfections are not unpracticed"—the remaining nine have not stopped, only follow "according to power and portion." Read "foremost" rightly: not "the first ground practices giving alone, and nothing else," but "the first ground leads with giving, the rest following as it can." Each of the ten grounds has one leading: the texts expressly mark "foremost" for giving (1st), patience (3rd), meditation (5th), prajñā (6th), and from the seventh on, the four added perfections lead—skillful means (7th), vow (8th), power (9th), knowledge (10th). (The second ground is paired with morality and the fourth with vigour by the standard reading that follows the sequence; the texts here do not use the "foremost" formula for them, which is noted plainly and not supplied.)

It is like an ensemble of ten instruments. In each movement one instrument leads, while the others have not fallen silent—only drawn back to accompany as their portion allows. The bodhisattva's ten are all present at every ground; only, ground by ground, one comes to the front. And the disclosure has a direction: through the first six grounds the first six perfections lead in turn (giving, morality, patience, vigour, meditation, prajñā), and at the sixth, where prajñā leads, root wisdom comes forth; from the seventh on, skillful means, vow, power, and knowledge lead by turns—just as said above: root wisdom having come forth, the disclosure of mastery turns into the turning-back use of subsequent wisdom. So the shape of the bodhisattva's mastery is no still life of ten objects, but a living road that opens stage by stage.

### A measure of plainness: this is all still the causal stage

But here a measure of plainness must be kept, lest the words overreach. The mastery that rises, ground by ground, foremost by foremost—even at the eighth ground, with the masteries that ground confers—is still the virtue of a *bodhisattva* at the causal stage, not buddhahood. The *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna*, at the eighth ground, weights the word "bodhisattva" twice over:

> This bodhisattva attains the bodhisattva's ten masteries.

It is a *bodhisattva* who attains a *bodhisattva's* ten masteries—and that doubled "bodhisattva" pins it to the causal stage. The *Avataṃsaka* records, further, that to a bodhisattva on the eighth ground, risen so high, the Buddhas come in person to remind him that the Buddha's ten powers, fearlessnesses, and eighteen distinctive qualities "you have not yet attained." If even an eighth-ground bodhisattva is so counseled, how much more those of us still on the early road. So this ground-by-ground ascent is "the staged disclosure of a causal-stage mastery," not "the fullness of the fruit-stage": the eighth ground's vow, the eighth ground's masteries, high as they are, are still parted from the Tathāgata's mastery by the road of the ninth ground, the tenth, and buddhahood itself. How the two masteries finally differ, and where that line is drawn, is matter for later (Part Five will treat the eighth ground's ten masteries on its own); here only the line is held: however high, the causal-stage mastery is not yet home.

### Onward

Here the part closes. The bodhisattva's mastery—its ground (arising from the vow), its ruler (the two boundless provisions), its shape (the six), and the unfolding of that shape with the grounds (the ten, leading by turns ground by ground)—four together, and the outline of the mastery stands whole.

And that one face of the six—the hand reaching toward others, the benefiting, not-harming, bearing, and nurturing of beings—can be drawn closer and finer still. The canon sets up another set, purely on "how to gather beings in and lead them onto the path," called the four embraces: giving, kind speech, beneficial conduct, and shared work. The next part watches this outreaching hand of mastery take the shape of the four.
