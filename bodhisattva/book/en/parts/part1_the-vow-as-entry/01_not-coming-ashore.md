## 1.1 Not Coming Ashore

The opening chapter left two questions. One: where does this mastery begin? The other: that door, plainly standing open—why does he not walk through it? The two are really two faces of one thing, and this chapter answers them together.

Take the first. The arhat's mastery rests on a completed reckoning—the exhaustion of the taints. While the taints are not yet exhausted, that mastery has nowhere to be sealed; the moment they are exhausted, the seal sets (the previous volume established this). So the ground of the arhat's mastery falls at that last stroke of severance. The bodhisattva's mastery is not like this. Its ground is no fruit at all; it is a single thought of aspiration.

### One vow arises, and he is already counted in

The chapter on the Ten Abodes in the *Avataṃsaka-sūtra* is where the canon states, head-on, from where the very status "bodhisattva" comes to stand. At that assembly the bodhisattva Dharma-Wisdom, having received the Buddhas' anointing on the crown and risen from concentration, set out for the assembled bodhisattvas the bodhisattva's "ten abodes"—ten dwelling-stations. He named the ten, then explained how the first, the Abode of Arousing the Mind, is entered:

> This bodhisattva sees the World-Honored One—majestic in form, perfect in feature, a joy to behold, hard to encounter, of great power; or sees his spiritual powers; or hears his prophecies; or hears his admonitions; or sees beings undergoing intense sufferings; or hears the vast Buddha-dharma of the Tathāgata—and arouses bodhicitta, seeking all-knowledge.

See how this mind arises, and what it seeks. The occasion is seeing the Buddha's majesty, or hearing the vast Dharma, or simply "seeing beings undergoing intense sufferings"—seeing one being in pain, and the heart cannot settle. And what it seeks is "all-knowledge"—the Buddha's own unobstructed wisdom, sought so as to turn back and deliver that suffering being, not one's own exit. This single thought arisen, he has entered the Abode of Arousing the Mind—the first of the bodhisattva's stations, whose entrance mark is just this thought of aspiration itself.

The treatises put it more bluntly. The chapter on the Arising of the Mind in the *Yogācārabhūmi* defines the weight of these very words:

> Once bodhisattvas have first aroused the mind, they are thereby said to have entered unsurpassed awakening and to be counted among the bodhisattvas of the Great Vehicle. … Only after arousing the mind can bodhisattvas progressively and swiftly realize unsurpassed, perfect awakening—never without it; therefore the arising of the mind is the very root of unsurpassed awakening.

"Entered," "counted among," "root"—each is tied to the arising of the mind, none to any prior fruit. Once the mind has arisen, one has *entered* unsurpassed awakening; once it has arisen, one is *counted among* the bodhisattvas of the Great Vehicle—identity conferred in this one thought, with no need of the four fruits beneath it. The treatise adds that this mind is "an outflow of compassion": it streams out of great compassion.

So the real sense of "mastery begins in the vow" is here: from the first thought of aspiration the bodhisattva's mastery already has its ground and its rising. With the vow arisen, its lineage, its aim (the Buddha's all-knowledge), its motive (great compassion) are all laid down; and the three incalculable eons of accumulation that follow fill out the very mastery that vow opened—they do not set some further thing atop the four fruits. The one who has severed not a fetter yet has aroused the great mind, the canon counts among the bodhisattvas, and his mastery thenceforth has its ground; the one perfect in the four fruits who has not aroused the great mind, the canon does not, on the strength of his fruit, automatically count a bodhisattva's mastery. Signed in the vow, not in the fruit.

### Long fit to leave, and not leaving

The first question answered, the second presses up at once: the vow arisen, the ground laid—what does this mastery actually look like? The answer falls on one decisive act: not taking.

In a passage of the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* asking why, of all the qualities in the Buddha's heart, compassion alone is called "great," the answer ends on a sentence that decides everything:

> By the power of great compassion, through measureless eons of birth-and-death his heart does not sink in weariness; by the power of great compassion, though long due for nirvāṇa, he does not take its realization.

"Though long due for nirvāṇa, he does not take its realization"—a person rooted in compassion reaches the place where nirvāṇa has long been his to take, and does not take it. This sentence is the character of the bodhisattva's mastery, and it is the answer to the second question: why does he not walk through that open door? Because he has long been fit to leave, and by his vow does not leave.

This carries us back to the threshold of the previous volume. That volume wrote the arhat: reaching the exhaustion of the taints, he takes the realization—no further becoming—and goes out of birth-and-death; that volume's mastery is the freedom of "able to leave, and leaving," signed in "departing cleanly." This volume's bodhisattva, standing at the same door of "long due for nirvāṇa," does not take that realization. Another exchange in the same treatise makes the "not taking" plain: asked why the bodhisattva, who also fully cultivates the thirty-seven factors the śrāvaka cultivates, does not fall into the śrāvaka's fruit, it answers:

> "Fully" means: fully contemplating and knowing, yet not taking realization; knowing it through and through—this is called "fully."

And it names what holds him back from dropping in:

> Because he does not abandon beings, and because this is conjoined with the wisdom of the unattainable emptiness, he does not fall to the śrāvaka ground.

What holds him is the vow that will not leave, and the wisdom that is not bound: the vow declines to go, the wisdom is not caught—and so he stands at that open door and stays. In the chapter on the Ten Grounds in the *Avataṃsaka*, where the bodhisattva Vajra-Treasury describes the seventh ground to the bodhisattva Liberation-Moon, the close is an echo of the same sentence: "Therefore the bodhisattva, though he courses in the limit of reality, does not take its realization."

The two masteries now stand clear. The arhat's mastery is sealed in "able to leave, and leaving"; the bodhisattva's, in "able to leave, and not leaving." The weight is on *able*—his not-leaving is not an inability to leave (that would be confinement, not mastery), but a long-standing fitness to leave that chooses not to. One mastery lies in "the way out is open"; the other in "the way out is open, and he chooses to stay, and stays unbound by birth-and-death."

### Different in kind, not in height

Here the easiest error springs up: to take "able to leave, and not leaving" as one rank higher than "able to leave, and leaving." That error must be pinned on the spot.

The two are not high and low; they are different in kind—what each severs, what each seeks, do not even fall in the same category. The chapter on Lineage in the *Yogācārabhūmi* states it sharply:

> The lineages of all śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas can attain only the purity that is freedom from the affliction-obstruction, and cannot attain the purity that is freedom from the obstruction to the knowable; the bodhisattva-lineage can attain both the purity from the affliction-obstruction and the purity from the obstruction to the knowable.

What the arhat's mastery clears is the affliction-obstruction—the defilements that bind one in birth-and-death; the bodhisattva's mastery, beyond that, must clear an obstruction-to-the-knowable as well—the subtle nescience that veils that "all-knowledge"—and this obstruction lies wholly outside the arhat's category. So the bodhisattva's mastery is not "the arhat's mastery, severing more of the same"; it is a different kind of attainment that clears an obstruction the arhat's range never included. Categories differing, where is the high-and-low along one ruler?

The chapter on the Merit of the First Arising of the Mind, in the *Avataṃsaka*, holds a comparison that shows this "difference in kind" best. At that assembly the Lord of the Gods asked about merit, and the bodhisattva Dharma-Wisdom first set up a mounting ladder of giving and teaching: suppose someone gives to measureless beings throughout the ten directions, and teaches them on up through the four meditations and the boundless states, all the way to the stream-enterer's fruit, the once-returner's, the non-returner's, the arhat's, the pratyekabuddha's way—Dharma-Wisdom asks the Lord of the Gods whether that person's merit is great. The Lord of the Gods says only a Buddha could know. Only then does Dharma-Wisdom let the verdict fall:

> This person's merit, set beside the merit of a bodhisattva's first arising of the mind, does not reach one part in a hundred, one part in a thousand, one part in a hundred thousand—down to not one part in an upaniṣad-fraction.

The merit of teaching beings to the four fruits and on to the pratyekabuddha's way—so vast a merit—set beside the merit of a bodhisattva's "first arising of the mind," is less than a fraction past counting. Why so far apart? The sūtra answers itself: the Buddhas, at their first arising of the mind, arouse it not merely to teach beings the four fruits and the pratyekabuddha's way, but "for the sake of the Tathāgata-lineage not being cut off"—for the continuance of the Buddhas. Were the bodhisattva's mastery truly a sequel to the arhat's, the first arising would be at most a sequel's preface; how could it instead surpass the whole "main text" entire? The only reading that holds is this: that one thought, from the start, was never on the ruler of the four fruits at all.

But "difference in kind" has to stand at both ends, or it slips into partiality. The arhat's "able to leave, and leaving" is true mastery—the previous volume established it from end to end; that unbound freedom is real, not the least bit lesser. The bodhisattva's "able to leave, and not leaving" is another true mastery. One measures "departing cleanly," the other "staying fully"; you would not measure a person who chooses to stay by "how cleanly he departed," any more than you would measure a steelyard with a ruler. Ruler and steelyard weigh different things; neither is higher. One who seeks the end of suffering, as the arhat does, gains the arhat's mastery; one who vows not to leave and yet not to be stained enters the door of the bodhisattva's mastery. Both are true, and they are not one thing.

### Onward

Gather the chapter into a sentence: the bodhisattva's mastery arises from the vow—signed in the vow, not the fruit; its mark is "able to take the realization within reach, and not taking it," not the arhat's "take the realization and leave the table"; and this "not taking," set against the arhat's "taking," is not height but kind.

He is like a ferryman who, having well reached the far shore, does not come ashore but turns the bow back into the current—his freedom lies not in "which bank he has landed on" but in "both banks within reach, and mooring at neither." (This is a comparison, not scripture; the depth of that "not coming ashore"—abiding in neither birth-and-death nor nirvāṇa—belongs to the last part of this book, and is only touched here.)

But the words have reached only as far as "not taking." A person whose aim is not "take the realization and leave"—what is he busy with, this whole long life, and by what is his arrival measured? He does not count "how many fetters severed"—that is the ledger of one going out. He measures something else, and measures it in a way directly opposite to severance. The next chapter looks at that ruler of his.
